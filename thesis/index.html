From Cultural Entropy to Regeneration

Designing Sustainable Management Architectures for Artist Estates.

Abstract

The thesis argues that posthumous devaluation is structural, not primarily a question of merit, intention, or isolated negligence. Once the artist disappears as coordinating agent, coordination costs rise and coherence decays unless continuity is intentionally designed.

Using a Popper-Deutsch explanatory standard, regenerative systems reasoning, and polycentric governance, estates are framed as long-horizon coordination systems. Cultural entropy is the directional tendency toward fragmentation under absent infrastructure.

The architectural response is two-part: RSA as a technical integrity layer for provenance-relevant stewardship statements, and ALMA as a polycentric governance network that diffuses standards without centralizing sovereignty.

Key definitions

Cultural entropy

The predictable drift of posthumous cultural systems toward fragmentation and reduced legibility unless counter-entropic structures are maintained.

Coordination tax

The cumulative cost of verifying truth, provenance, and legitimacy when shared infrastructure is missing.

Integrity layer

A durable and independently verifiable sequence of stewardship statements that resists silent mutation and sequence loss.

Polycentric governance

Multiple overlapping centers of authority coordinating through interoperable procedures rather than central command.

Regenerative vs extractive

Regenerative systems replenish future coordination capacity; extractive systems consume it for short-term outputs.

Read highlights

Chapter 1: Conditions of structural devaluation

Four mechanisms recur: narrative exclusion, institutional fragility, market opacity, and fragmented governance. Treated together, they form a recursive system rather than isolated failures.

Chapter 2: Cultural entropy and explanation quality

Using the Popper-Deutsch hard-to-vary standard, the thesis argues persistence requires explanation, while decay does not. Inaction is not neutral because delay exports costs into the future.

Chapter 3: Regenerative principles translated into constraints

Regenerative economics is translated into design constraints for estates: governance clarity, robust information flow, corrigibility, and cross-scale balance.

Chapter 4: ERL as boundary-condition stress test

The Emilio Rodríguez-Larraín estate is treated analytically as a worst-case entropic configuration to test whether regeneration can be designed under maximal constraint.

Chapter 5: RSA and why a shielded public Layer 1

RSA is specified as an append-only integrity protocol for stewardship statements. It is intentionally not a marketplace, valuation engine, or ownership court.

Chapter 6: ALMA adoption and polycentric layers

ALMA is proposed as a multi-layer governance network: micro estates, meso national associations, and macro continental coordination.

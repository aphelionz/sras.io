### **From Cultural Entropy to Regenerative Architecture:**

Designing Durable Coordination Architectures for Artist Estates

## **Abstract**

Why do so many artist legacies deteriorate after death—even when the work is historically significant, institutionally engaged, and widely respected?

This thesis argues that posthumous devaluation is not the result of neglect, poor intentions, or artistic failure. It is a structural default. Cultural value does not persist by merit alone. In the absence of durable coordination, trust, and stewardship infrastructure, artistic legacies drift toward fragmentation and uncertainty over time.

Drawing on the problem-solving epistemology of Karl Popper and David Deutsch, principles from regenerative economics, and theories of polycentric governance, this study treats artist estates as living systems. When the artist—the primary coordinating agent—disappears, coordination costs rise sharply. Without shared procedures capable of surviving succession, disagreement, and institutional change, coherence decays. Archives fragment, provenance weakens, institutional confidence erodes, and markets discount uncertainty. This directional tendency is described as cultural entropy.

Rather than proposing moral remedies or retrospective recognition, the thesis advances a design-oriented response. It develops a two-part regenerative architecture that shifts continuity from individuals to systems:

1. **The Registry of Shielded Artworks (RSA):** A technical integrity layer that stabilizes authorship, provenance, and stewardship claims over time, preserving corrigibility and reducing the need for repeated verification resets.  
2. **ALMA (Asociación de Legados de la Modernidad Americana):** A complementary polycentric governance network that diffuses shared stewardship protocols, enabling collective adoption and "Sovereign Legibility" without centralization or loss of estate sovereignty.

The architecture is stress-tested against real-world constraints—including fragmented archives, weak secondary markets, and jurisdictional complexity—using the estate of Emilio Rodríguez-Larraín as a boundary-condition case. It is further evaluated for interoperability with established international frameworks governing institutions (ReACH), markets (Responsible Art Market), and academic historiography.

The central contribution of this thesis is neither biographical nor normative. It is architectural. It demonstrates that cultural persistence depends not on exceptional individuals, but on ordinary adherence to well-designed systems. Trust must be encoded into process, not requested from persons. Stewardship becomes sustainable only when it is infrastructural rather than heroic.

The thesis concludes that while artistic death is inevitable, cultural decay is not. Entropy is the default—but regeneration can be designed.

## **Preface**

From Uneasiness to Explanation

**“The motive to change is always some uneasiness.”**

**— John Locke, An Essay Concerning Human Understanding (1690, Book II, Ch. XXI)**

The engine of this inquiry is uneasiness.

This uneasiness did not begin with the death of my father, the twentieth-century Peruvian visual artist Emilio Rodríguez-Larraín, on December 23, 2015\. It began years earlier, as I gradually witnessed his cognitive and physical decline. As his memory faded, I observed how his body of work and personal possessions were being divided, sold, and dispersed piecemeal—often driven by short-term necessity, yet dangerously lacking any cohesive long-term strategy. These two processes unfolded in parallel: as his mind deteriorated, his legacy fragmented. At the time, I experienced a concrete sense of powerlessness. I did not know what mechanisms existed to protect a fragile legacy, nor how an heir ought to act responsibly once the artist no longer had the capacity to do so.

When my father died, this uneasiness intensified. Grief was compounded by obligation. I inherited not only the emotional weight of loss, but the responsibility of stewarding a cultural legacy, alongside a complex set of legal, economic, and administrative complications for which I was wholly unprepared. I found myself returning repeatedly to a single unavoidable question: what, in practical terms, must be done to prevent a legacy from losing value, relevance, and meaning once the artist is no longer present?

Seeking answers, I enrolled in the Executive Master in Art Market Studies at the University of Zurich in 2017\. This decision was not driven by abstract academic curiosity, but by necessity. I required analytical tools capable of confronting inherited disorder with clarity rather than intuition. While my father’s estate was the initial catalyst, it soon became clear that the difficulties I encountered were not personal anomalies. Across Latin America—and increasingly beyond—I observed a recurring pattern: historically significant artist estates repeatedly confront coordination failure, institutional fragility, market opacity, and the gradual erosion of both symbolic and economic value after the artist’s death (SRL 2025; see also later essays on Latin American modernism).

Rather than treating these outcomes as contingent stories of misfortune, this thesis interrogates the problem at the level of underlying structure. It advances three core claims:

1. Posthumous devaluation is not primarily a problem of artistic merit, recognition, or moral failure, but a predictable outcome of rising coordination costs once the artist—the primary coordinating agent—is no longer present.

2. This condition can be explained as a form of cultural entropy: a directional drift toward fragmentation and loss of coherence in long-horizon cultural systems that lack counter-entropic design.

3. Resisting this drift requires architecture rather than advocacy—specifically, a regenerative legacy-management model capable of stabilizing trust, verification, and coordination through shared infrastructure and polycentric governance.

Taken together, these claims reframe legacy decay not as an exception to be corrected, but as a default condition to be designed against.

In a Popperian framework, a problem originates in a contradiction between expectations and observed reality. (Popper 1963, 1972\) The uneasiness that animates this inquiry arises from precisely such a paradox. On the one hand, there exists a widely shared expectation—held by artists, heirs, institutions, and the public—that cultural value and historical significance, once established, ought to endure across time. On the other hand, empirical observation repeatedly contradicts this belief. This contradiction between expected persistence and observed decay constitutes the core problem-situation addressed in this thesis.

Lived experience made this contradiction visible; it did not make it intelligible. In the absence of explanation, posthumous decline is typically attributed to contingent causes: mismanagement, insufficient recognition, or bad luck. Yet such accounts fail to explain why similar patterns recur across artists, regions, and institutional contexts. Treating these outcomes as isolated failures obscures their structural regularity.

Accordingly, the uneasiness that motivates this inquiry is treated as an epistemic signal: an indication that existing explanations are inadequate. Resolving the contradiction requires distance from particulars and a shift toward first-principles reasoning capable of yielding general, criticizable explanations. Inspired by the analytical tradition of Karl Popper and David Deutsch, this thesis proceeds from explanation to conjecture, and from conjecture to design. What begins as a personal and overwhelming situation is deliberately reframed as a general problem—one that does not belong to a single individual, but can be examined, tested, and addressed by others.

Explanation alone, however, is insufficient. Understanding imposes responsibility. Once a problem is rendered intelligible, continued inaction becomes difficult to justify. The progression from diagnosis to conjecture, and from conjecture to design, is therefore logical rather than rhetorical. The regenerative legacy-management model developed here emerges as a conjectural solution: a theory-laden hypothesis about how cultural value might be stabilized under conditions of high coordination costs.

Importantly, this framework is already transitioning into practice. The Registry of Shielded Artworks (RSA), developed in Chapter 5, has moved beyond theory into early product design and implementation. In parallel, ALMA (Asociación de Legados de la Modernidad Americana) has been constituted as a polycentric institutional response to the coordination failures identified in this study. The Emilio Rodríguez-Larraín estate is a founding member, and ALMA is scheduled to publicly activate through an exhibition on July 4, 2026, at the Art Museum of the Americas (AMA) in Washington, D.C.

It is necessary to clarify the scope of this thesis. It does not seek to establish or defend the artistic merit of Emilio Rodríguez-Larraín, nor of any individual artist from Latin America. The regenerative architecture proposed here is deliberately artist-agnostic. Cultural value does not persist by merit alone; it endures only where trust, stewardship, and coordination are structurally designed to outlast individuals.

What follows is therefore not a defense of a single artistic legacy, nor an attempt to secure recognition for one artist. It is an effort to identify and design the conditions under which any artist’s legacy can remain coherent, legible, and viable after the artist’s death. The uneasiness that initiated this inquiry is transformed here into a shared diagnostic and practical framework—one that can be examined, criticized, and adapted by others. Its purpose is to shift stewardship away from improvised, burdensome, and isolated efforts toward a coordinated practice in which cultural value persists through designed structures rather than individual struggle.

## **Introduction**

Artist Estates as Living Systems: Cultural Entropy, Regeneration, and the Architecture of Persistence

This thesis examines the posthumous fate of artist estates through a problem-oriented, explanatory, and design-driven epistemology. Drawing on the critical rationalism of Karl Popper and David Deutsch, together with principles from regenerative economics and theories of polycentric governance, it approaches artist estates as complex socio-cultural systems whose persistence depends on coordination, feedback, and long-term institutional coherence (Popper, 1959; Popper, 1963; Deutsch, 2011; Ostrom, 2010; Fullerton, 2015). The central objective is twofold: first, to explain why cultural value tends to decay by default after an artist’s death; and second, to develop architectural responses capable of resisting that decay over extended time horizons.

The thesis does not propose new philosophical doctrines. Its contribution lies instead in synthesis and application. It brings together three mature and well-established intellectual traditions—problem-oriented epistemology, regenerative systems thinking, and polycentric governance—and applies them to a domain in which they have rarely been integrated in a coherent and implementable manner: the long-term stewardship of artistic legacies. By doing so, it moves beyond descriptive diagnosis toward explanations capable of informing institutional and technical design.

---

## **The Problem-Situation**

Across Latin America—and increasingly across other global regions—the posthumous trajectories of modern artist estates exhibit a striking regularity. Artists whose work is historically significant, critically rigorous, and culturally consequential frequently experience systematic devaluation after their deaths. This devaluation rarely manifests as a single rupture; instead, it unfolds as a gradual erosion of coherence. Archives fragment, provenance weakens, authorship becomes contested, and institutional relationships lapse. As scholarly discourse thins, markets begin to discount the rising uncertainty. Over time, narratives that once sustained relevance lose visibility and force (Greet, 2018; Velthuis, 2005).

Existing scholarship has documented many dimensions of this phenomenon. Art history has examined canon formation and narrative exclusion; market studies have analyzed opacity and illiquidity; institutional analyses have highlighted underfunded infrastructures and governance fragility (Velthuis, 2005; Würtenberger, 2016). While these accounts are descriptively accurate, they leave unresolved a deeper explanatory question:

**Why does cultural value so often fail to persist after an artist’s death, even when artistic quality, historical importance, and episodic recognition are present?**

In Popperian terms, this question constitutes a problem-situation (P1​). It is neither a moral grievance nor an empirical anomaly, but a signal that prevailing explanations are inadequate (Popper, 1972). If posthumous devaluation recurs predictably across different countries, generations, and institutional contexts, then its causes cannot be contingent or incidental. They must be structural.

---

### **Explanatory Framework**

The theoretical architecture of this thesis is constructed from three distinct but mutually reinforcing intellectual traditions:

#### **I. Critical Rationalism: The Logic of Inquiry**

From critical rationalism, the thesis adopts several foundational commitments. First, it treats inquiry as originating in problem-situations (P1​): moments in which a "horizon of expectations" is disappointed by observed outcomes (Popper, 1963; Popper, 1972). Second, it prioritizes explanation over induction, seeking accounts that explain the underlying reality—the "reach" of a theory—rather than merely documenting a pattern of occurrences (Popper, 1963; Deutsch, 2011). Third, it adopts the criterion that explanations must be hard to vary—meaning that every detail of the explanation plays a functional role, making it resistant to the *ad hoc* modifications typically used to protect failed theories (Deutsch, 2011).

#### **II. Regenerative Systems: The Metabolism of Value**

From regenerative economics and general systems theory, the thesis adopts the insight that complex socio-economic systems persist only insofar as they continuously **regenerate** the conditions of their own viability. Such systems endure not through static preservation—which leads to entropy—but through feedback loops and institutional reinvestment that allow value to compound rather than dissipate (Bertalanffy, 1968; Meadows, 2008). This perspective shifts the objective of an artist estate from the "liquidation of assets" to the "maintenance of a system's capacity to produce trust" (Fullerton, 2015).

#### **III. Polycentric Governance: The Architecture of Cooperation**

From polycentric governance theory, the thesis draws the principle that coordination among heterogeneous actors—heirs, institutions, markets, and scholars—does not require a single, central authority. Durable cooperation can emerge through interoperable standards, shared rules of due diligence, and nested institutional arrangements that preserve local autonomy while enabling collective verification. The thesis adopts Ostrom’s (2010) critique of the "monocentric" trap. It argues that artist estates are complex economic systems that cannot be managed by the market alone (which liquidates) or the state alone (which is often sluggish). Instead, it proposes a polycentric approach where multiple centers of authority coordinate through shared protocols. (Ostrom, 1990; Ostrom, 2010; Hess & Ostrom, 2007).

Synthesis: Artist Estates as Living Systems Taken together, these frameworks provide the analytical basis for treating artist estates as living systems. Their survival depends on their capacity to coordinate across autonomous actors, regenerate trust through a verifiable technical substrate (RSA), and adapt through a polycentric institutional network (ALMA) once the artist—the original coordinating agent—is no longer present.

## **Core Thesis Claims**

To address this problem-situation, this thesis advances three interrelated conjectures:

### **I. Structural Devaluation as Coordination Failure**

Posthumous devaluation is not primarily a problem of artistic merit, recognition, or individual failure. It is a predictable outcome of rising **coordination costs**. Once the artist—the system's primary "organizing agent"—is no longer present to stabilize authorship, narrative authority, and trust, the costs of maintaining that coherence exceed the capacity of traditional heirs or uncoordinated market actors to absorb them (Coase, 1937; Williamson, 1981).

### **II. Cultural Entropy**

This outcome can be explained as a directional drift toward fragmentation and loss of coherence in cultural systems that lack counter-entropic design. **Cultural entropy**, as utilized in this research, denotes a structural tendency rather than a metaphor: in the absence of a stable technical and institutional substrate, the default trajectory for information and trust is dissipation (Bertalanffy, 1968; Deutsch, 2011).

### **III. Regenerative Architecture**

Resisting this drift requires architecture rather than advocacy. Cultural value can persist only where trust, stewardship, and coordination are stabilized through shared technical infrastructure (RSA) and polycentric governance (ALMA). This architecture must be capable of operating under high uncertainty and across long-time horizons, replacing "heroic" individual intervention with scalable, interoperable protocols (Ostrom, 2010; Fullerton, 2015).

Synthesis: Taken together, these claims reframe posthumous decay not as an exception to be corrected after the fact, but as a default condition of the art market that must be deliberately designed against.

---

## **Methodology: Design-Oriented Inquiry and the Stress Test**

Traditional art market research often relies on induction—the accumulation of various case studies to find a pattern. This thesis departs from that model, adopting instead a design-oriented approach rooted in "The Sciences of the Artificial" (Simon, 1969). The goal is not to document what *is*, but to design what *must be* for a system to function.

### **I. From Induction to Deduction: The Design-Led Approach**

Following the Popperian schema, this research begins with a conjecture (TS): that cultural entropy can be mitigated by a technical and social substrate. The methodology is therefore deductive. The thesis designs an "operating system" (RSA and ALMA) based on first principles and then evaluates its viability against the complexities of the real world (Popper, 1972).

### **II. The "Boundary-Condition" Case Study**

The estate of Emilio Rodríguez-Larraín (ERL) is not treated as a typical case study or a biographical survey. Instead, it is utilized as an analytically extreme boundary-condition case.

In engineering, a boundary condition is a limit-state used to test the stability of a system. The ERL estate provides this because it maximizes "coordination costs" through:

* **Information Fragmentation:** Decades of undocumented provenance and dispersed archives.  
* **Geographic/Jurisdictional Complexity:** Operative across multiple continents with varying legal standards.  
* **Institutional Disengagement:** A legacy that is historically significant but structurally "orphaned" by the market.

By treating the ERL estate as a **stress test**, the thesis asks: *If the proposed architecture (RSA/ALMA) can stabilize this specific, high-complexity legacy, can it be generalized as a solution for the broader artist estate ecosystem?*

### **III. Lived Experience as an Epistemic Trigger**

The thesis acknowledges the author’s role as an heir not as a source of subjective bias, but as an epistemic trigger. First-hand experience with the decline of the ERL legacy provided the "refuting facts" that exposed the inadequacy of current market theories. This methodology uses "insider" access to identify failure modes that are often invisible to outside observers, using that data to build more robust, objective explanations (Deutsch, 2011).

Cultural value does not persist by merit alone. ## **Chapter 1**

## **The Conditions of Structural Devaluation**

### **1.1 The Problem-Situation: A Vicious Cycle of Devaluation**

This inquiry begins with a concrete and recurring problem-situation observed across Latin American modern artist estates: a vicious cycle of posthumous devaluation that unfolds despite demonstrable artistic merit, historical relevance, and episodic recognition.

Once the artist is no longer present to function as the central coordinating node—stabilizing authorship, meaning, and intent—estates frequently enter a downward spiral. This study identifies that the breakdown occurs across what can be termed the "Triad of Validation": the three interdependent domains that convert an artist's work into "Symbolic Capital" (Bourdieu, 1993). These domains constitute the standard Value-Creation Chain of the art market (Velthuis, 2005):

1. **Academia (Intellectual Meaning):** Scholars and critics provide the narrative authority and historical positioning necessary for an artist to enter the canon.  
2. **Institutions (Public Legitimacy):** Museums and non-profit spaces provide the "custodial seal" of approval, signaling to the public that the work is of enduring cultural significance.  
3. **The Market (Economic Value):** Galleries and auction houses provide liquidity and price discovery, reflecting the consensus reached by the other two domains.

The systemic devaluation addressed in this thesis is driven by four interlocking conditions that paralyze this triad:

* **Narrative Exclusion (Academic):** Sustained scholarly engagement thins as the "search costs" for fragmented archives become prohibitive, leading to the artist’s omission from history (Greet, 2018).  
* **Institutional Fragility (Institutional):** Weak or discontinuous cultural infrastructures fail to provide the long-term physical and symbolic "homes" required for legacy preservation.  
* **Market Opacity (Financial):** Information asymmetry and a lack of verifiable provenance lead the market to treat works as "lemons," triggering a risk-adjusted discounting of value (Akerlof, 1970; Velthuis, 2005).  
* **Fragmented Governance and Unprofessional Management (Systemic Layer):** The absence of an authoritative coordination protocol often leads to management that is reactive, emotional, or technically deficient. This unprofessionalism—manifesting as opaque authentication processes and a lack of standardized due diligence—erodes the trust of external stakeholders and prevents "Symbolic Capital" from compounding (Würtenberger, 2016).

Individually, these conditions are well-documented within their respective silos. Collectively, however, they form a **self-reinforcing system**. Narrative exclusion leads to institutional disengagement, which in turn fuels market opacity. The result is not an episodic breakdown, but a **systemic default toward decline**.

---

### **1.2 From Diagnostic Mapping to Explanatory Insufficiency**

Earlier research (Appendix I) was diagnostically successful in demonstrating that the failure modes of Latin American artist estates were patterned rather than anecdotal. However, diagnostic mapping—the mere cataloging of symptoms—proved insufficient for solving the problem.

While identifying mechanisms clarified *where* breakdowns occurred, it did not explain *why* these breakdowns reproduced themselves with such regularity, nor why corrective actions (such as episodic exhibitions or isolated sales) so often failed to generate a durable reversal. As Karl Popper emphasized, the persistence of a problem signals not a lack of data, but an insufficiency of explanation (Popper, 1972). To proceed, the inquiry requires a transition from descriptive mapping to a hard-to-vary explanation (Deutsch, 2011)—one that accounts for the underlying logic of the system and explains why posthumous cultural systems default toward decay.

---

### **1.3 Reframing the Estate: From Assets to Coordination Systems**

The first step toward a structural explanation is conceptual: reframing the artist estate. Rather than treating it as a static repository of material assets, this thesis treats the estate as a long-horizon coordination system. This is a socio-cultural structure tasked with maintaining coherence across time and among diverse actors once the original source of authority is removed.

From this perspective, the artist’s death constitutes a critical phase transition in the system's physics. During their lifetime, the artist functions as a "low-entropy hub": a single, authoritative coordinating agent who stabilizes authorship, meaning, and verification. Their disappearance removes this coordinating center. In the absence of a replacement architecture—such as formal governance protocols or institutionalized verification mechanisms—complexity theory suggests that the system will tend toward increasing disorder (Bertalanffy, 1968). Fragmentation, therefore, is not an accident of history; it is structurally expected.

---

### **1.4 Stakeholders and the "Coordination Tax"**

An artist estate is an ecosystem populated by heterogeneous actors—heirs, scholars, dealers, institutions, and collectors—who operate with asymmetric information, divergent incentives, and distinct time horizons. Because no single actor can sustain systemic coherence alone, stability depends on shared reference points: credible attribution, standardized provenance, and recognized governance authority.

When this coordination infrastructure is absent, a prohibitive coordination tax emerges (Coase, 1937). This tax represents the escalating cost of verifying truth, managing risk, and sustaining trust in a fragmented environment. Under the weight of this tax, actions that are individually rational—such as an heir seeking immediate liquidity or a scholar disengaging due to archival uncertainty—produce collectively destructive outcomes for the legacy. Devaluation, therefore, reflects a failure of coordination architecture rather than a failure of personal merit or ethical intention**.** 

---

### **1.5 The Four Mechanisms as Systemic Expressions**

Within this systems framing, the four conditions identified in the "Triad of Validation" can be reinterpreted as interacting expressions of interface failure. In engineering and computer science, an interface is the boundary where two systems meet and exchange information; in the artist estate ecosystem, these failures prevent the flow of trust and value between the legacy and its stakeholders.

* **Narrative Exclusion** reflects a breakdown at the academic and curatorial interface. When archives are inaccessible, the "search costs" for scholars become prohibitive, causing the system to ignore the artist in favor of more "legible" subjects.  
* **Institutional Fragility** reflects failures in the custodianship interface. It represents a breakdown in the metabolic transition from private ownership to public memory infrastructure.  
* **Market Opacity** reflects a failure in the verification and price-discovery interface. Without a "trusted substrate" for provenance, the market cannot price the work accurately, resulting in a permanent "uncertainty discount" (Akerlof, 1970).  
* **Fragmented Governance and Unprofessional Management** reflect the absence of an authoritative coordination protocol. Without a clear "operating system," heirs and managers cannot interface professionally with the specialized requirements of scholars, museums, or the market (Würtenberger, 2016).

These interface failures degrade five interdependent dimensions of cultural value, which serve as the **Diagnostic Matrix** for this research:

1. **Material Integrity:** Grounded in Conservation Science (ICOM; Getty), this dimension acknowledges that value requires the work and its archival evidence to physically exist and be stable. If the primary evidence rots, the "material proof" of the legacy vanishes.  
2. **Attribution Clarity:** Drawing from Connoisseurship and Art Law, this dimension addresses the "signal-to-noise" ratio of authorship. As Akerlof (1970) suggests, if the signal is contested, the asset's quality becomes uncertain, triggering market withdrawal.  
3. **Interpretive Continuity:** Rooted in Art History and Hermeneutics, this is the "narrative" dimension. As Bourdieu (1993) argues, works require an "intellectual field" to maintain symbolic capital. Without active discourse, the work becomes "silent" and loses its historical weight.  
4. **Institutional Legitimacy:** Derived from Institutional Theory (Dickie, 1974; Danto, 1964), this dimension views value as a "status" granted by the "Artworld." Without museum engagement or scholarly oversight, an estate loses its "custodial seal" of approval.  
5. **Market Legibility:** Based on Information Economics (Velthuis, 2005; Coase, 1937), this is the ability of a buyer to "read" the work’s value through provenance and price transparency. Without this legibility, the Coordination Tax renders the legacy illiquid.

Degradation in any one dimension propagates stress across the others, accelerating fragmentation. Crucially, ethical behavior alone cannot halt this process. As the coordination tax rises, "good intentions" do not compound; instead, they are consumed by the friction of an inefficient system.

---

### **1.6 A Recursive System, Not a Linear Problem**

Because these mechanisms are recursive—meaning they feed back into and reinforce one another—the system resists linear or piecemeal solutions. In a fragmented environment, isolated interventions often fail to generate a "return on coordination."

For example, a high-profile solo exhibition (a local institutional success) will fail to generate long-term value if the archives remain un-stabilized (an academic failure) or if the provenance remains unverified (a market failure). Without a unified architecture to capture the "validation" generated by the exhibition, the legacy will inevitably revert to its default trajectory: drift.

This recursive nature justifies the need for the dual architecture proposed in this thesis (RSA and ALMA). To stop the drift, the coordination architecture must be changed at the fundamental level—shifting from a reliance on the "virtue" of individual heirs to the "reliability" of shared, technical, and polycentric protocols.

---

 **1.7 From Conditions to Explanation**

This chapter has established that posthumous devaluation is neither accidental nor episodic. It is the predictable behavior of artist estates understood as long-horizon coordination systems operating under stress without an adequate technical or institutional substrate. The "vicious cycle" identified—where narrative exclusion, institutional fragility, and market opacity feed into one another—is not a series of unfortunate events, but the functional output of a system characterized by high coordination costs and fragmented governance.

The task now shifts from mapping these conditions to explaining the structural force that produces them. In the logic of critical rationalism, we have completed the description of the "problem-situation" (P1​); we must now move beyond the descriptive *what* to the explanatory *why*.

Chapter 2 introduces Cultural Entropy as the unifying explanatory principle. By defining entropy not as a metaphor, but as the directional drift of information and trust in the absence of counter-entropic design, we can render the observed pattern of decay "hard to vary" (Deutsch, 2011). This shift demonstrates why regeneration—the metabolism of value—cannot be assumed as a natural byproduct of artistic merit, but must be deliberately engineered through a new architecture of persistence.

## **Chapter 2**

## **Cultural Entropy as a Structural Explanation**

### **2.1 From Structural Diagnosis to Explanation**

Chapter 1 established that the posthumous devaluation of modernist artist estates is neither accidental nor idiosyncratic. Drawing on comparative empirical research conducted as part of the author’s prior work (see Annex 1), the analysis demonstrated that across national contexts, institutional settings, and market environments, the same four mechanisms recur: narrative exclusion, institutional fragility, market opacity, and fragmented governance. Together, these form a self-reinforcing cycle of devaluation.

However, in the philosophy of science, identifying recurring mechanisms is not equivalent to explaining them. A catalogue of failures does not resolve the decisive question raised by the **problem-situation** (P1​) itself: **Why does cultural value so reliably fail to persist once the artist is no longer present to coordinate the system?**

This thesis treats the persistence of this problem as **epistemically significant**. Following the tradition of Karl Popper and David Deutsch, such dissatisfaction is not interpreted as a call for more data or further case-study analysis. **In this context, additional case studies would merely provide inductive confirmation of a known pattern without revealing its underlying cause. This research moves beyond the "historical narrative" of individual legacies to seek the structural "laws of motion" that govern them.** The task of this chapter is therefore not to refine the diagnosis through further documentation, but to replace it with a **structural explanation** that:

1. **Accounts for the observed pattern as a whole**, rather than treating each failure as an isolated event;  
2. **Explains why alternative outcomes (stability) would require fundamentally different conditions**, shifting the burden of proof from why estates fail to why they ever succeed; and  
3. **Remains resistant to *ad hoc* adjustment**, ensuring the theory cannot be "fixed" with minor excuses when it meets contradictory data.

To meet these criteria, the chapter advances **Cultural Entropy** as a hard-to-vary explanation for posthumous devaluation. It moves the inquiry from the realm of art-historical contingency into the realm of **system dynamics**.

### **2.2 Explanation Quality: The Popper–Deutsch Standard**

Popper’s central contribution to epistemology was to shift the criterion of knowledge away from confirmation (the accumulation of supporting instances) and toward explanatory power. A theory is not strong because it fits observations—any number of "soft" myths can be made to fit data—but because it constrains what can happen. Strong explanations are "hard to vary": they have a specific internal logic that cannot be altered without destroying the entire theory (Popper, 1963; Deutsch, 2011).

To achieve this rigor, the thesis draws on Popper’s Evolutionary Epistemology, specifically his schema of Problem Solving (Popper, 1972). This framework suggests that systems—whether biological, scientific, or institutional—evolve by producing "tentative solutions" (TS) to "problem-situations" (P1​). The persistence of devaluation in artist estates is a P1​ that has survived all current institutional "tentative solutions," signaling a need for a radical shift in architecture rather than just more data (Popper, 1994).

Weak explanations in the art world are typically retrospective and rely on what can be termed the "Meritocratic Myth."This is the romanticized assumption—common in 19th-century historiography and still prevalent in market discourse—that artistic "quality" possesses an inherent agency; that great work will inevitably "rise to the top" regardless of structural barriers. This view treats art history as a fair trial of merit. However, as sociologists like Bourdieu (1993) and economists like Velthuis (2005) have noted, "merit" is a social construct that requires institutional "consecration" to be visible.

A Popper–Deutsch approach reframes the inquiry asymmetrically:

* **Persistence requires explanation.**  
* **Decay does not.**

In any complex system, the default trajectory is toward the dissipation of information and the breakdown of order. If posthumous cultural systems routinely fragment, then the burden of explanation lies not with the decline—which is the expected result of a lack of work—but with stability.

By adopting this standard, this thesis rejects the meritocratic myth. It asks a more rigorous structural question: What specific coordination architecture must be present for cultural value to persist at all once the original coordinating agent (the artist) is no longer present? This shift moves the thesis from the realm of Advocacy (hoping for recognition) to the realm of Architecture (designing for persistence).

### **2.3 The Three Worlds of the Artist Estate**

To explain why decay is the default, we must define what an artist estate actually *is*. This inquiry adopts Popper’s Pluralist Ontology, which distinguishes between three interacting worlds (Popper, 1972; 1978):

1. **World 1 (The Physical):** The material artworks, the paper in the archives, and the physical storage spaces.  
2. **World 2 (The Subjective):** The mental states, intentions, and memories of the heirs, the desires of collectors, and the opinions of curators.  
3. **World 3 (The Objective):** The objective contents of the legacy—the artist’s theories, the documented provenance, the historical narrative, and the *content* of the catalogue raisonné.

The artist estate is essentially a World 3 system that relies on a World 1 substrate (archives) to be accessible to World 2 agents. During the artist’s lifetime, the artist acts as a bridge between all three worlds. Upon their death, this bridge collapses.

Without a specialized "problem-solving institution" (Popper, 1994\) to maintain the objective contents of World 3, the legacy becomes trapped in World 2 (subjective family disputes) or decays in World 1 (physical deterioration). Cultural Entropy is thus defined as the process by which World 3 information dissipates when its World 1 and World 2 anchors fail.

### **2.4 Cultural Entropy: The Explanatory Conjecture**

This chapter advances Cultural Entropy as the structural explanation for the "vicious cycle" identified in Chapter 1\. By applying the Popper–Deutsch standard, we move from a list of symptoms to a hard-to-vary explanation: one where every part of the diagnostic matrix is a logical consequence of the system’s entropic drift.

In this framework, the Four Self-Reinforcing Mechanisms are reinterpreted as the functional output of a system losing its "World 3" anchors:

1. **Narrative Exclusion (Academic Interface):** As archival entropy increases, the "signal" of the artist's historical relevance fades into "noise." Scholars do not exclude the artist out of malice, but because the information cost of navigating a disordered archive makes research non-viable.  
2. **Institutional Fragility (Custodial Interface):** Institutions require "World 3" stability to commit resources. When an estate cannot provide clear metadata or provenance, it fails the institution's risk-assessment protocols, leading to disengagement.  
3. **Market Opacity (Financial Interface):** Entropy creates Information Asymmetry. As provenance records dissipate, the market can no longer distinguish "high-quality" works from "lemons" (Akerlof, 1970), leading to the systemic price discounting observed in Chapter 1\.  
4. **Fragmented Governance (Systemic Layer):** Without a counter-entropic "Operating System" (ALMA), management defaults to the subjective, high-entropy impulses of World 2 (unprofessional/emotional management).

The power of this explanation lies in its restrictive nature. A "soft" explanation would suggest that a single "good" exhibition or a "lucky" auction record could save a legacy. ### **2.5 Entropy as a Feedback Process: The Coordination Tax**

Cultural entropy is not a static state; it is a self-accelerating feedback process. As World 3 information (provenance, archives, narrative) dissipates, the system’s internal friction increases. This friction is expressed economically as the Coordination Tax: the cumulative effort, time, and risk premium required for stakeholders to establish "truth" in the absence of a trusted, shared infrastructure.

In a low-entropy system (while the artist is alive), the coordination tax is low because the artist serves as the "authoritative registry." Upon their death, the tax begins to rise. We can define this process through a recursive loop:

1. **Increased Uncertainty:** As records become fragmented (Material/Attribution decay), the "search costs" for a curator or collector rise exponentially.  
2. **Rational Disengagement:** When the Coordination Tax exceeds the perceived value of the artwork, rational actors—museums, top-tier galleries, and serious scholars—exit the system to avoid the risk of "lemons" (Akerlof, 1970).  
3. **Incentive Collapse:** As high-value actors exit, the incentive for heirs to invest in professional management (ALMA-type structures) diminishes, further accelerating the loss of World 3 data.

This feedback structure explains why ethical behavior and artistic quality are insufficient to arrest decline. "Good intentions" do not lower the coordination tax. Only a change in the information substrate (the architecture) can reduce the tax and invite high-caliber stakeholders back into the legacy’s orbit.

### **3.3 Living Systems and the Nature of Cultural Persistence**

To establish a generative model, we must first redefine the object of study. 

Regenerative economics begins from the premise that economies are not mechanistic machines, but **living systems**(Fullerton, 2015). To analyze an artist estate through this lens, we must utilize the concept of **Autopoiesis** (Maturana & Varela, 1980). Originally a term from theoretical biology, autopoiesis (literally "self-creation") describes a system capable of reproducing and maintaining itself through the internal generation of its own components.

Unlike a machine, which is a depreciating asset requiring external repair to remain static, an autopoietic system persists by continuously regenerating its internal relationships and information flows. When applied to the posthumous artist legacy, this perspective shifts the definition of "assets" from static objects to metabolic processes. An artist estate is a living socio-cultural system whose value is distributed across a **World 3 Commons**:

* **Informational Metabolism:** The metadata and archival records that provide the "DNA" of the oeuvre. In a living system, information is not just stored; it must be processed and updated to remain "vital" (Capra & Luisi, 2014).  
* **Narrative Continuity:** The interpretive frameworks that keep the artist’s work "legible" to changing cultural contexts.  
* **Institutional Trust:** The "consecration" (Bourdieu, 1993\) held by external stakeholders. In systems theory, this is the **Coupling** between the estate and its environment.  
* **Coordination Capacity:** The internal "nervous system" capable of synthesizing these elements.

#### **The Loss of the Metabolic Center**

During an artist’s lifetime, the system is maintained by the artist as the **low-entropy hub**. They are the source of all autopoietic "work"—resolving attributions, defining meaning, and creating provenance. Death, in this framework, is a **systemic rupture**: the removal of the metabolic center.

Without an architecture designed to replace this coordinating node, the system loses its autopoietic capacity. It can no longer "self-repair" the errors introduced by time (Entropy). Consequently, posthumous decline is not an anomaly—it is the structurally expected result of a system that has lost its metabolism. As information dissipates, the system "dies" ontologically long before the physical canvases (World 1\) deteriorate.

### **3.4 From Cultural Entropy to System Differentiation**

If the estate is a living system, the divergence observed in Section 3.1 is explained by the system's **metabolic strategy**. Systems exposed to entropic pressure differentiate into two distinct structural paths based on how they manage their World 3 capital:

**1\. Extractive Management (The Linear Path):** This architecture optimizes for short-term World 1 outputs (liquidity and episodic sales). Because it fails to reinvest in the World 3 commons, it is effectively **"mining" the legacy**. Following the logic of **Extractive Economies** (Acemoglu & Robinson, 2012), this management style treats the artist’s name as a finite resource. Every sale without documented provenance and every exhibition without archival consolidation extracts value from the future to serve the present.

**2\. Generative Stewardship (The Recursive Path):** This architecture reinvests present activity into future legibility. It treats the legacy as a system where every action must leave an **"informational residue"**. This concept is an adaptation of **Feedback Loops** in General Systems Theory (von Bertalanffy, 1968): the output of a transaction (a sale or a loan) becomes the input for the system's future stability (updated provenance/metadata). This reinvestment reduces the "Coordination Tax" and allows trust to compound.

This distinction is functional, not moral. It answers a single diagnostic question: **Does the system consume coordination capacity, or does it replenish it?** Following Fullerton (2015), systems that optimize extraction at the expense of viability ultimately undermine their own capacity to persist.

### **3.5 The Economic Foundation: Rivalry, Commons, and Polycentricity**

To understand why a generative architecture is necessary, we must classify the "assets" of an estate using the terminology of **Institutional Economics** (Ostrom, 1990; Hess & Ostrom, 2007).

#### **The Distinction of Rivalry**

In economics, a **Rivalrous Good** is one whose consumption by one person prevents its consumption by another. A physical painting (World 1\) is a **Private, Rivalrous Good**. If one collector owns it, another cannot.

However, the **Information** about that painting (its authenticity, provenance, and historical significance) is a **Non-Rivalrous Good**. One person knowing a painting is authentic does not prevent others from knowing it; in fact, the more people who "consume" (trust) this information, the more the value of the physical object compounds.

#### **The Knowledge Commons**

This body of non-rivalrous information constitutes a **Knowledge Commons** (Hess & Ostrom, 2007). Unlike physical commons (like a forest or a fishery), which can be "over-grazed," a knowledge commons is subject to **"The Tragedy of the Underground"**: it doesn't disappear through over-use, but through **fragmentation, opacity, and neglect**.

#### **Polycentric Governance**

To manage a commons, **Elinor Ostrom** (1990) proposed **Polycentric Governance**—a system where multiple authorities overlap and coordinate based on shared rules, rather than a single, centralized hierarchy. For an artist estate, this means moving away from a "monarchy" (the sole heir) toward a "republic" of stakeholders (scholars, archives, and forensic tools) who all contribute to and protect the World 3 integrity of the oeuvre.

### **3.6 Regenerative Economics as a Design Framework**

To formalize the transition from economic theory to architectural execution, we must apply Fullerton’s (2015) principles through the lens of **Institutional Economics** and **Common-Pool Resource (CPR) Theory** (Ostrom, 1990). The objective is to define the specific **Design Constraints** that a counter-entropic architecture must satisfy to facilitate **Generative Stewardship**.

In this framework, we distinguish between the **Rivalrous Private Asset** (the physical World 1 object) and the **Non-Rivalrous Knowledge Commons** (the World 3 integrity of attribution and provenance). A **Rivalrous** good is one where use by one party precludes use by another; in contrast, a **Non-Rivalrous** good—like information—can be used by many without depletion. A regenerative architecture ensures that the protection of the latter—the shared information—directly compounds the value and liquidity of the former.

**Principle 1: Right Relationship** \* **System Logic:** In living systems, the health of the whole depends on the quality of the relationships between the parts.

Ambiguity regarding who has the authority to authenticate or represent the oeuvre generates a "Coordination Tax" (Coase, 1937\) that accelerates fragmentation. Authority must be a stable relationship, not an episodic conflict.

Monetization strategies that optimize for immediate cash flow while degrading archival clarity or institutional trust are structurally extractive—they liquidate the system's foundation for a temporary pulse.

**Principle 3: Innovative, Adaptive Capacity**

* **System Logic:** Persistence requires **corrigibility**—the ability to learn from errors and evolve.  
* **Estate Design Constraint:** **Iterative Validation.** Catalogues raisonnés and attribution frameworks must function as **evolving digital protocols** rather than static, closed endpoints. The architecture must allow for the "Popperian growth of knowledge," where new data updates the system without collapsing its historical authority.

**Principle 4: Empowered Participation**

* **System Logic:** Healthy systems are **polycentric** (Ostrom, 1990); they distribute agency across the network rather than concentrating it in a single point of failure.  
* **Estate Design Constraint:** **Procedural Legitimacy.** Agency must be distributed **procedurally** rather than **discretionarily**. Systemic legitimacy scales when participation is auditable and governed by rules, reducing the "Key Person Risk" inherent in personalized, centralized estates.

**Principle 5: Edge Effect Abundance**

* **System Logic:** In ecology, the greatest diversity and stress occur at the "edges" where different ecosystems meet.  
* **Estate Design Constraint:** **Interface Stability.** The highest entropic pressure occurs at the interfaces: Estate–Market, Archive–Institution, and Scholarship–Verification. The architecture must stabilize these boundary conditions through shared reference standards, ensuring information does not "leak" or degrade as it crosses from private to public domains.

**Principle 6: Robust Circulatory Flow**

* **System Logic:** Metabolism requires the continuous circulation of nutrients (information/capital) and the excretion of waste.  
* **Estate Design Constraint:** **Informational Residue.** Opacity is an entropic "clog" that produces informational waste. The architecture must ensure that every circulation event (a sale, a loan, an exhibition) leaves a reusable World 3 residue—permanent data updates that lower future verification costs for all stakeholders.

**Principle 7: Balance Across Scales and Time Horizons**

* **System Logic:** Systems fail when they over-optimize for the small/fast scale (short-term profit) at the expense of the large/slow scale (long-term survival).  
* **Estate Design Constraint:** **Strategic Equilibrium.** The system must structurally balance short-term liquidity, long-term conservation, and institutional engagement. Optimizing for the market scale while ignoring the historical/institutional scale produces a "fragile" legacy that collapses under minor stress.

**Principle 8: Honor Community and Place**

* **System Logic:** Systems persist when they are **coupled** with their specific cultural and ecological contexts.  
* **Estate Design Constraint:** **Scholarly Embeddedness.** Artist estates cannot sustain legitimacy in a vacuum. Long-term coherence requires embeddedness within regional institutions and scholarly communities. Detachment from this cultural "place" severs the feedback loops (feedback from critics, historians, and the public) that sustain the work's objective meaning.

With the design constraints established, we can now define the **analytic discriminator** that explains the divergence of legacies. #### **The Extractive Trap: Informational Depletion**

**Extractive Management** occurs when a system architecture optimizes for short-term outputs—typically immediate liquidity or episodic market visibility—while failing to reinvest in the World 3 commons. This mirrors the logic of **Extractive Institutions**, a term coined by **Acemoglu and Robinson (2012)** in *Why Nations Fail*. They define extractive institutions as those designed to extract wealth from a system to benefit a narrow group of stakeholders, ultimately leading to systemic exhaustion and collapse.

In an extractive estate, the legacy is treated as a finite "mine" rather than a renewable resource. The symptoms of this depletion include:

* **Decoupled Transactions:** Sales occur in World 1 without updating the World 3 registry. This creates "provenance gaps"—missing links in the chain of ownership—that raise future verification costs and create **Information Asymmetry** (Akerlof, 1970), where buyers cannot distinguish high-quality assets from "lemons."  
* **Discretionary Authority:** Authentication depends on the "charismatic authority" of a gatekeeper. Because this authority is not anchored in auditable data, when the gatekeeper dies, the trust in the entire oeuvre "resets" to zero.  
* **Narrative Consumption:** Art is used for "hype" to drive price, but the scholarly substrate is ignored. The result is a legacy that is famous but intellectually "hollow," making it vulnerable to market volatility.

#### **The Generative Path: Informational Capitalization**

**Generative Stewardship** is a recursive architecture where every action—an exhibition, a sale, a research project—must leave a **"reusable informational residue"** (Fullerton, 2015). This aligns with the concept of **Autopoiesis**, a term introduced by biologists **Maturana and Varela (1980)**. Autopoiesis (from Greek *auto* "self" and *poiesis* "creation") describes a system capable of reproducing and maintaining itself by regenerating its own internal components.

In a generative estate, the system is **mechanically coupled**: market energy (World 1\) is used to rebuild the informational structure (World 3):

* **Coupled Circulation:** Every time a work moves, the registry is updated. The metadata of the event becomes a "nutrient" for the system, strengthening the provenance record and reducing future uncertainty.  
* **Procedural Authority:** Verification follows an auditable protocol (RSA). Legitimacy is held by the **system**, not the individual, ensuring the legacy remains "legible" across generations.  
* **Compounding Trust:** Because the "Knowledge Commons" is constantly replenished, the **Coordination Tax**—the effort required for stakeholders to agree on the "truth" of a work—decreases over time, increasing the asset’s long-term viability.

The region’s history is defined by a "Colonial Extractive Logic"—a centuries-old pattern where material wealth (silver, guano, rubber) was "mined" for export, while the institutional and informational infrastructures of the host countries were left to decay. This created the paradox of "material-rich yet institutionally poor" nations.

We see a structural parallel in the management of Latin American artist estates. Too often, the "raw material" (the artworks) is exported to northern markets in undocumented "dark" transactions. The local "Knowledge Commons"—national archives and institutional memory—is not replenished. The legacy is "mined" for immediate liquidity, leaving the artist's home context institutionally hollowed out.

To design against this, a **Regenerative Architecture** must be explicitly **Anti-Extractive**. It must ensure that the "wealth" of a master like Emilio Rodríguez-Larraín is not just liquidated into private hands, but used to capitalize a permanent, non-rivalrous World 3 infrastructure. ### **5.1.2 The Failure of the “Default Database”: Why Impersonal Anchoring Is Required**

A legitimate objection arises at this juncture. If the problem is informational fragmentation, why would a professionally managed centralized database not suffice? Why introduce a decentralized integrity substrate at all?

The answer follows directly from the entropy thesis developed in Chapter 2 and the boundary-condition stress test of Chapter 4\.

A centralized database remains a World 2 institutional artifact. Its persistence depends on ongoing administrative stability, funding continuity, governance coherence, and uncontested authority. Yet these are precisely the variables shown to be unstable in posthumous estates operating under high coordination costs.

To clarify the distinction, it is necessary to identify three structural vulnerabilities inherent to centralized archival systems.

---

#### **1\. Succession Fragility**

A database is only as durable as the institution that administers it.

In the event of inheritance disputes, governance transitions, or legal restructuring, control of database credentials becomes contested. Access can be frozen, altered, or selectively restricted. The integrity of the archive becomes dependent on the outcome of World 2 conflicts.

This is not a theoretical risk. Chapter 4 demonstrated that ambiguity in authority immediately increases the coordination tax and triggers institutional withdrawal. A database that is internally controlled by a contested entity amplifies this fragility.

A counter-entropic substrate must therefore survive succession events without requiring unanimous cooperation among heirs.

---

#### **2\. Retroactive Editability and Epistemic Erasure**

A conventional database permits silent modification.

Records can be overwritten. Entries can be altered without preserving a public audit trail of change. Corrections erase the trace of prior error. While this flexibility appears administratively convenient, it undermines one of the core requirements derived in Chapter 3: corrigibility without erasure.

Popperian epistemology requires that knowledge growth be traceable. Error elimination must leave a record of what was corrected. A mutable database collapses the historical chain of conjecture and refutation into a present-tense snapshot, eroding epistemic accountability.

In entropic systems, the ability to silently rewrite history becomes a source of institutional mistrust. Museums and scholars, facing opaque record systems, rationally increase verification costs.

An append-only structure, by contrast, preserves the chronology of stewardship claims—even when they are revised.

---

#### **3\. Institutional Perishability**

Databases require continuous metabolism:

* Server maintenance

* Software updates

* Funding streams

* Administrative oversight

If the estate dissolves, loses funding, or enters prolonged dispute, the digital archive can degrade or disappear. In this scenario, World 3 collapses back into World 2 memory or fragmented World 1 documents.

Cultural entropy is accelerated not by malice but by neglect.

A counter-entropic architecture must therefore minimize reliance on estate-level institutional survival for the persistence of core integrity data.

### **The Impersonal Continuity Requirement**

The ERL stress test revealed a non-negotiable principle:

Continuity cannot depend on the uninterrupted virtue or competence of a specific institution.

If authority remains embedded in a fragile administrative structure, entropy will reassert itself at each point of instability. What is required is an integrity layer that is:

* Time-ordered,

* Non-rewritable,

* Publicly verifiable,

* And independent of the estate’s own server or governance continuity.

This is the structural reason decentralized anchoring becomes necessary.

The purpose of decentralization here is not ideological. It is architectural. By anchoring the Integrity Skeleton of stewardship events to a distributed consensus layer, the RSA ensures that the minimum necessary record of authoritative acts survives even if:

* The estate fractures,

* The internal database fails,

* The hosting institution dissolves,

* Or governance transitions are contested.

The decentralized ledger does not replace the estate. It stabilizes the estate’s claims across time.

It is essential to avoid technological overstatement.

The RSA does not place entire archives “on-chain.” It does not expose sensitive data publicly. It does not replace curatorial scholarship or institutional due diligence.

What it anchors is far more modest but structurally decisive:

* The fact that a stewardship statement was issued,

* The chronological ordering of such statements,

* The cryptographic fingerprint of supporting documentation at a specific time,

* And the identity of the authority that signed it.

This minimal anchoring creates what may be termed Impersonal Continuity: the persistence of verifiable sequence beyond the fragility of administrative control.

## **5.2 Registry Initialization and the Boot Sequence: Constituting Authority in Protocol Form**

### **5.2.1 Beginning from Disorder: Initialization Under Entropic Conditions**

If the Registry of Shielded Artworks is to function as a counter-entropic integrity substrate, it cannot assume a pre-existing state of archival perfection or uncontested authority. The ERL case demonstrated the opposite condition: fragmentation, dispersed works, incomplete records, succession ambiguity, and institutional skepticism. The establishment of the RSA proceeds through what may be termed a Boot Sequence—a formal transition from personal stewardship to protocolized authority. This sequence is not merely technical; it is institutional. It answers a foundational question: who is authorized to speak for the artist in cryptographic form, and under what constraints?

---

### **5.2.2 Constituting the Registry Authority (RA)**

The first step is the constitution of the Registry Authority (RA). In traditional estate structures, authority is often implied rather than formalized. It may rest in familial consensus, informal archival custody, or market recognition. Yet such forms of authority are vulnerable to entropy because they lack procedural clarity. The RSA requires that this authority be made explicit, bounded, and auditable.

The RA is therefore defined as a legally recognized stewardship body—an estate, foundation, or designated committee—that holds the mandate to issue authoritative stewardship statements concerning the oeuvre. However, in the RSA architecture, authority does not merely exist as a legal claim; it must be instantiated as a cryptographic identity. This identity is realized through the generation of a public–private keypair, which serves as the RA’s signing authority within the registry.

To prevent the reintroduction of personal fragility into the protocol, this keypair is not ideally generated or controlled by a single individual. The design constraint of governance stabilization requires distributed control. Practically, this may take the form of a threshold signing scheme or a multi-signature arrangement in which authority is divided among multiple trusted stewards—such as legal counsel, archival leadership, and designated family representatives. The aim is not technological sophistication for its own sake, but the reduction of key-person risk. Authority becomes a structured procedure rather than an attribute of personality.

The moment this keypair is generated and its public component published, the RA undergoes what may be termed a Genesis Declaration. This inaugural event anchors three elements to the integrity substrate: the public key that will sign future registry events, the defined scope of authority (for example, “official registry for the oeuvre of Emilio Rodríguez-Larraín”), and a cryptographic digest of the governance charter that governs how the key is used. In this act, authority moves from implicit social recognition to explicit procedural form.

Only after this constitutional act can the oeuvre itself begin to be anchored.

### **5.2.3 Progressive Anchoring: Integrity Before Completeness**

Crucially, the RSA does not require complete archival consolidation before initialization. A conventional centralized catalogue raisonné often postpones publication until exhaustive documentation is achieved, which in resource-constrained estates can take decades. The RSA reverses this order. It prioritizes chronological integrity over immediate completeness.

The process of oeuvre registration therefore follows a Progressive Anchoring Model. Rather than claiming total knowledge of the artist’s production at the outset, the RA begins by registering works whose documentation is already robust—those in the estate’s possession or those supported by high-confidence archival records. Each such work is assigned a Canonical Artwork ID (CAID), which functions as a stable reference pointer across institutional, scholarly, and market contexts. The CAID is not a token of ownership; it is a semantic anchor that ensures referential continuity across time.

For each work, a Dossier Snapshot is assembled. This snapshot consists of the best available documentary evidence at that moment—photographic documentation, catalog entries, invoices, exhibition histories, conservation reports. The dossier is then hashed, producing a Snapshot Digest that serves as its cryptographic fingerprint. The hash, not the full documentation, is what is anchored to the ledger. This preserves confidentiality while ensuring that the evidence, as it existed at a given time, cannot be silently altered without detection.

The act of anchoring this digest, signed by the RA’s key, constitutes the first Stewardship Statement for that work. From this point forward, the work possesses an integrity chain that can grow but cannot be retroactively rewritten.

---

### **5.2.4 External Onboarding and Market Invitation**

In estates such as ERL’s, where a significant portion of the oeuvre resides outside the direct physical control of the estate, this progressive anchoring has an additional strategic dimension. It functions as a formal invitation to the market. Collectors holding works are invited to participate in the stabilization of the legacy by submitting documentation for review and inclusion. The registry thus becomes not merely an archival exercise but a professionalization initiative. The estate does not claim ownership over dispersed works; it offers a verifiable integration into a durable integrity chain.

To manage this process responsibly, the RSA employs a non-binary status taxonomy. Works may be classified as Registered (fully verified and documented), Provisional (anchored with strong historical evidence but pending updated inspection), Disputed (actively contested), or Unverified (referenced in historical records but lacking current documentation). This taxonomy avoids the simplistic dichotomy of authentic versus fake. It reflects the epistemic humility required by a fallibilist framework and preserves transparency regarding evidentiary strength.

This layered classification system ensures that the registry does not prematurely confer legitimacy, nor does it erase uncertainty. Instead, it renders epistemic states visible and structured, allowing scholars, institutions, and market actors to calibrate risk accordingly.

### **5.2.5 Structural Consequences of the Boot Sequence**

The Boot Sequence therefore accomplishes three structural transitions simultaneously.

First, it converts authority from informal recognition into cryptographically verifiable identity. The estate’s voice becomes procedurally bounded and externally auditable.

Second, it establishes chronological integrity before completeness, allowing regeneration to begin under conditions of scarcity rather than waiting for ideal consolidation. In doing so, it reverses the paralysis that often afflicts under-resourced estates.

Third, it transforms dispersed collectors and institutions from passive holders of risk into potential participants in cumulative verification. The registry becomes a coordination invitation rather than a unilateral declaration.

Only after this constitutional and progressive anchoring process is complete does the RSA become operational in its broader lifecycle: transfer recognitions, revisions, dispute flags, and key rotations. But without this Boot Sequence, those later operations would rest on unstable foundations.

The RSA does not begin with technology. It begins with the formalization of voice.

## **5.3 Economic Feasibility and the Incentive Transmission Model**

### **5.3.1 The Metabolic Constraint of Artist Estates**

A counter-entropic architecture that cannot finance its own persistence is not regenerative; it is aspirational. ### **5.3.2 The Reset Dynamic and the Cost of Repeated Verification**

In the status quo model, when a collector seeks to sell a work, verification is re-performed from scratch. Auction houses commission independent research. Insurers demand fresh provenance documentation. Museums conduct months of archival reconciliation before accepting a loan. Each actor incurs its own private coordination cost because trust is not reusable. The estate’s prior work does not automatically compound into lower friction for future transactions.

This repeated reset dynamic produces two distortions. First, it imposes a structural discount on works whose documentation is fragmented or ambiguous. Second, it disincentivizes estates from investing heavily in documentation because the benefits are not captured by the estate itself but dissipate across the market.

The RSA reconfigures this structure.

By anchoring stewardship statements into a durable, verifiable sequence, the registry transforms verification from a recurring private expense into a cumulative infrastructure investment. Each properly documented issuance, revision, or transfer recognition strengthens the integrity chain of the work and reduces the marginal verification cost of future transactions.

### **5.3.3 The Estate as Professionalized Verification Authority**

The estate, acting as Registry Authority, therefore occupies a new institutional role: it becomes a professionalized Verification Authority. This role generates sustainable revenue not through liquidation of artworks, but through service-based engagement.

Four primary revenue channels follow logically from this repositioning.

First, there is the digital certificate issuance or upgrade service. Many estates possess legacy paper Certificates of Authenticity that are perishable and vulnerable to forgery. By anchoring these certificates into the RSA as signed registry events, the estate converts ephemeral documentation into durable integrity entries. Collectors pay not for the “creation” of authenticity, but for the stabilization of an existing claim into a long-horizon format.

Second, there is dossier onboarding and forensic review. When collectors submit works for integration into the registry, the estate performs scholarly and documentary evaluation. This labor—photographic verification, provenance reconstruction, comparison with archival records—is professional expertise. The fee compensates for the intellectual and administrative work required to generate a credible Dossier Snapshot.

Third, there are institutional due diligence services. Auction houses, insurers, and banks require structured verification for loans, collateralization, or exhibition approval. An RSA-anchored integrity chain dramatically reduces search and reconciliation costs. The estate can provide tiered access or certified reports derived from the registry without disclosing sensitive data publicly. The revenue here compensates for accelerated and standardized verification.

Fourth, there are transfer recognition events. When a work changes stewardship, anchoring that recognition maintains the continuity of the integrity chain. A modest service fee ensures that the registry remains updated and that circulation strengthens rather than weakens coherence.

These revenue streams share a common principle: the estate is monetizing verification labor, not ownership control. It is charging for structured documentation, not extracting rent from market participation. This distinction is crucial. If fees are perceived as gatekeeping tolls rather than value-adding services, the registry loses legitimacy and adoption collapses.

Insurers increase premiums to hedge against claims. Museums hesitate to exhibit works with fragile documentation. The absence of verifiable integrity becomes economically punitive.

When a work is integrated into the RSA and possesses a cumulative chain of signed stewardship statements, that uncertainty decreases. Verification becomes reusable. Due diligence becomes faster. Title risk becomes more legible. The reduction in friction translates into measurable economic advantages: lower insurance premiums, faster consignment acceptance, stronger collateral potential, and greater institutional willingness to lend or exhibit.

The Compliance Premium therefore emerges not from speculative hype, but from reduced coordination cost. A collector pays an onboarding fee today in exchange for lower friction and higher liquidity tomorrow. Over a 30- or 50-year horizon, the total transaction cost declines even as informational residue compounds.

The estate, meanwhile, captures a portion of this value through service revenue, enabling reinvestment into further archival work, digitization, scholarship, and institutional outreach. In regenerative terms, circulation funds coherence; coherence enhances circulation.

## **5.4 Technical Realism and Adversarial Modeling: Designing for a Hostile Horizon**

If Chapter 5 has argued that the RSA is deductively required as a counter-entropic integrity substrate, then the burden now shifts from conceptual plausibility to architectural defensibility. A system intended to stabilize cultural memory over a seventy-year copyright horizon—and, in practice, over a century of intergenerational stewardship—cannot be designed as if it will operate inside a friendly institutional environment. It must be resilient under adversarial conditions: key theft, internal disputes, institutional collapse, and the gradual obsolescence of cryptographic standards. In other words, the RSA must be specified not only in terms of what it enables under ideal coordination, but in terms of what it survives under breakdown.

This section therefore treats the RSA as a long-horizon security and continuity problem. The aim is not to present the RSA as “tamper-proof” or metaphysically immune to failure. That would be a soft, unfalsifiable claim. Instead, the aim is Popperian: to make the system criticizable by defining its adversarial surface explicitly—identifying where it can break, under what conditions, and what corrective procedures are built into the architecture to preserve integrity even when failure occurs.

---

### **5.4.1 The Shielded Payload: Encoding Stewardship without Public Exposure**

A central boundary condition derived earlier is epistemic shielding: the system must make verification compounding possible without forcing estates or collectors into mandatory publicity. Traditional provenance infrastructures often equate transparency with disclosure, which is incompatible with the privacy norms of high-value art markets and dangerous in contexts where political or criminal risk makes disclosure costly. The RSA therefore does not publish Stewardship Statements as readable public text. Instead, it records them as encrypted payloads anchored to a shielded ledger.

Operationally, the Stewardship Statement is formatted as a structured message—conceptually JSON, though the particular encoding can vary—containing at minimum the Canonical Artwork ID, the event type, the Snapshot Digest of the supporting evidence bundle, and a pointer to the previous event in the work’s integrity chain. The point of the payload is not expressive richness but auditability: it is the minimum semantic unit required for future verification, revision, and dispute management.

This payload is then embedded in a shielded transaction memo, encrypted such that only authorized viewing parties can decrypt and interpret it. In this way, the blockchain carries the integrity skeleton—ordering, non-repudiation, and persistence—without exposing the dossier itself. The technical design expresses a deeper philosophical constraint: the RSA is built to preserve truth-claims without forcing public confession. It aims to separate verifiability from spectacle.

---

### **5.4.2 Consensus as Time: Why Ordering Is the First Integrity Primitive**

Under entropic conditions, the most immediate vulnerability of archives is not fabrication but reordering. If events can be retroactively rearranged—if a work can be made to appear earlier, later, or differently situated—then the integrity of the entire narrative collapses. One of the RSA’s core contributions is therefore chronological binding: it anchors stewardship actions to a public consensus clock.

In the RSA, “time” is defined not by human declarations but by consensus-based timestamping: the block height and mining timestamp of the transaction that carries the event. This ordering function matters because it converts stewardship into an append-only sequence. The estate cannot silently overwrite its past, and neither can a later faction rewrite history without leaving a cryptographic trace. In Popperian terms, this is an epistemic discipline mechanism: it forces conjectures about the oeuvre to appear in a visible sequence of claims and corrections rather than as retroactive edits presented as timeless truth.

This is precisely what makes the RSA an integrity substrate rather than merely an archive. An archive stores documents; an integrity substrate stabilizes the ordering and corrigibility of claims about those documents.

---

### **5.4.3 The Key Compromise Problem: Designing for Theft, Loss, and Succession Conflict**

The most dangerous failure mode in the RSA is not market manipulation but signing authority compromise. If an RA private key is stolen or captured during a succession dispute, the attacker may be able to issue plausible stewardship statements that appear authoritative. In a protocolized registry, this is existential: the authority signature is the voice of the estate.

The RSA therefore requires a pre-protocolized key compromise response. This response is not a discretionary “we will deal with it” policy; it is an explicit procedure embedded into the governance and operational doctrine of the registry.

First, the architecture presupposes that the RA does not rely on a single hot key. The signing authority is ideally distributed through threshold signing or multi-signature control, reducing single-point failure. Yet distributed control does not eliminate compromise; it only raises the cost. Therefore, the RSA also requires the existence of an emergency revocation capability: a cold, offline master mechanism held in separated custody, capable of anchoring an Emergency Revocation Event.

This revocation event establishes a cryptographic boundary in time: it declares that any signatures produced by the compromised key after a specific block height are invalid. Immediately following, the RA issues a Key Rotation Event, anchoring a new public key as the authoritative signing identity. The integrity of the registry survives not by denying compromise but by making compromise containable and legible.

Importantly, this procedure also externalizes trust beyond the family. Because revocation and rotation are anchored events, verifiers do not need to “believe” the estate’s narrative about what happened. They can check the integrity boundary themselves. This is a core counter-entropic property: trust is migrated from testimony to verifiable procedure.

### **5.4.4 ALMA Propagation and Social Backstops: Why Cryptography Alone Is Not Enough**

Even a well-designed revocation protocol has a distribution problem. If the estate rotates keys but market actors continue to trust an old key, confusion persists and adversaries exploit ambiguity. This illustrates a recurring theme: cryptographic integrity does not automatically become institutional reality. It must be socially propagated.

This is one of the points at which Chapter 5 necessarily anticipates Chapter 6\. The RSA is designed to interoperate with ALMA as a propagation and reputational layer. When a key rotation occurs, the change must be broadcast across a network of aligned estates and institutional partners so that verification norms update quickly. In this sense, ALMA functions as the socio-institutional immune system of the registry: it helps ensure that protocol events are recognized as authoritative across the ecosystem.

This resolves a major failure mode of isolated estates: even if one estate behaves correctly, its corrective actions may not “land” in the market without a shared interpretive layer. The RSA provides technical truth; ALMA provides coordinated recognition of that truth.

### **5.4.5 Cryptographic Obsolescence and the Problem of Time: Designing for Migration**

A registry designed for the long horizon must acknowledge that its cryptographic assumptions may not hold forever. Hash functions weaken, signature schemes may face new attacks, and the underlying ledger itself may face political or technical discontinuity. A system that pretends otherwise is not rigorous; it is rhetorical.

The RSA therefore requires a migration strategy that preserves historical validity without demanding that the original chain remain eternal. The key observation is that the RSA’s integrity does not rely on any single transaction in isolation; it relies on the rolling hash chain of events per artwork and the non-repudiable signatures of the RA at each stage. This structure enables integrity mirroring.

If, over time, the estate chooses—or is forced—to migrate to a different ledger or to incorporate post-quantum primitives, the registry can be mirrored by anchoring the digest of the most recent validated event (or a checkpoint digest of the chain state) onto the new substrate. This does not “move” the past; it references it. The new chain inherits credibility by pointing back to a historically verifiable sequence of signed statements.

Similarly, cryptographic agility can be introduced at the dossier level by allowing new Snapshot Digests to use upgraded hashing algorithms while keeping prior digests intact as historical artifacts. The aim is not to pretend the past can be rewritten into stronger cryptography, but to ensure that the registry remains continuous as cryptography evolves—preserving the audit trail of the legacy’s knowledge-growth.

### **5.4.6 The Integrity/Asset Boundary: Explicit Anti-Tokenization Clarification**

Because the RSA uses a blockchain, it risks immediate conceptual conflation with tokenization, NFTs, and speculative assetization. Such conflation is not a rhetorical inconvenience; it is a design threat. It would distort incentives, invite extractive behavior, and likely alienate precisely the institutional actors whose trust the RSA is intended to restore. Therefore, the RSA must enforce an explicit boundary: it is an integrity notary, not a financial instrument.

The RSA does not tokenize ownership. It does not fractionalize artworks. It does not implement automated marketplace logic via smart contracts. Ownership remains a World 1 legal status governed by contract law and jurisdictional institutions. The RSA records only stewardship recognition and documentary integrity.

This boundary is philosophically consistent with the thesis’s underlying ontology. The RSA stabilizes World 3 content—truth-claims about attribution, provenance, and stewardship actions—without collapsing those claims into commodified digital proxies. Its purpose is to protect the integrity commons, not to fabricate a parallel asset universe.

### **5.4.7 Persistence as Architecture: Blockchain versus Digital Archive**

The final justification for this adversarial framing returns to the distinction between metabolic systems and passive persistence. A professionally managed digital archive—however well designed—remains a World 2–dependent institution. It requires budgets, administrators, servers, and continuity of mandate. ### **5.4.8 Durability Without Dogma: Transition to Corrigibility**

The preceding section demonstrated that the RSA is designed to survive hostile conditions: key compromise, institutional collapse, cryptographic obsolescence, and strategic manipulation. It established that integrity can be made persistent without relying on continuous administrative virtue. Yet persistence alone does not constitute regeneration.

A registry that cannot be erased but also cannot be corrected would merely fossilize error. Such a system would resist entropy at the level of deletion while reproducing it at the level of dogma. It would preserve information but inhibit knowledge growth.

This distinction is critical.

The entropy thesis presented earlier does not describe cultural decay merely as loss of data. It describes the dissipation of coherence. Coherence requires both durability and revision. It requires that claims about attribution, dating, provenance, and interpretation remain auditable across time—but also open to correction when superior evidence emerges.

The RSA must therefore satisfy a dual constraint:

* It must prevent silent erasure,

* and it must prevent epistemic rigidity.

Adversarial resilience addresses the first requirement. Corrigibility addresses the second.

If cultural entropy is the default drift toward fragmentation and disappearance, epistemic rigidity is the equal and opposite pathology: the premature closure of inquiry. A regenerative architecture must resist both.

This is why the RSA was never designed as a “final archive.” It was designed as a conjectural infrastructure—one that preserves the chronological trace of stewardship acts while allowing those acts to be superseded through structured revision. In Popperian terms, the registry does not enshrine truth; it records conjectures and their subsequent refutations or refinements.

## **5.5 Corrigibility and the Evolution of the Canon**

The canon of an artist is not a fixed list but an evolving explanatory structure—a network of claims about authorship, chronology, context, and meaning.

The RSA therefore rejects what may be termed the Myth of the Final Archive. No registry state is treated as epistemically ultimate. Every anchored stewardship statement is provisional in principle, even if operationally stable in the present. This design decision is not an aesthetic preference; it follows directly from the epistemological commitments articulated earlier in the thesis.

A traditional database corrects error by overwriting the prior record. The previous claim disappears, replaced by the updated entry. While administratively efficient, this method destroys the visible trace of intellectual evolution. It collapses the historical path of inquiry into a single present-tense assertion. In doing so, it undermines accountability and erodes scholarly trust.

The RSA adopts the opposite logic.

Correction occurs through addition, not deletion.

### **5.5.1 The Revision Event: Encoding Knowledge Growth**

When new evidence emerges—an archival letter clarifying a date, a conservation analysis revealing overpainting, or a comparative study refining attribution—the RA does not modify the prior registry entry. Instead, it issues a Revision Event.

This event references the Canonical Artwork ID and the hash of the prior Registry Event. It anchors a new Dossier Snapshot containing the updated metadata and supporting documentation. The integrity chain now reflects both the original claim and the corrected claim in chronological sequence.

The result is not duplication but transparency.

The registry shows:

* What was believed at time T₁,

* What evidence justified that belief,

* What was later discovered at time T₂,

* And how the understanding of the work evolved.

The canon becomes auditable in its growth.

This approach accomplishes three structural objectives simultaneously.

First, it preserves epistemic humility. The RA’s authority is stabilized but not absolutized. The estate can change its position without erasing its past stance.

Second, it increases institutional trust. Museums and scholars can see the trajectory of interpretation rather than receiving opaque updates.

Third, it transforms revision from reputational risk into procedural normality. Because correction is built into the architecture, acknowledging error becomes a sign of epistemic strength rather than weakness.

### **5.5.2 Dispute Flags and Provisional States: Managing Uncertainty Without Collapse**

Not all epistemic challenges arise from improved evidence. Some arise from conflict. Works may circulate with incomplete provenance, competing claims of authorship, or suspected forgery. This silence accelerates entropy.

The RSA introduces a visible but non-terminal response: the Dispute Flag.

When credible uncertainty arises, the RA anchors a Dispute Event linked to the CAID. This does not assert final judgment. It marks the work as under review and preserves the reasoning behind the uncertainty. The registry therefore communicates structured ambiguity rather than false certainty.

Similarly, the registry allows for Provisional Status. A work may be anchored with strong documentary evidence but pending fresh physical inspection. Rather than excluding it entirely or prematurely granting full verification, the RA assigns a clearly defined evidentiary state.

This non-binary taxonomy is philosophically consistent with fallibilism. It acknowledges that knowledge is often graded rather than absolute. It also protects the estate from the reputational cost of either premature endorsement or unjustified exclusion.

By rendering epistemic states explicit, the RSA reduces rumor-driven markets and opaque gatekeeping. It replaces informal whisper networks with auditable procedural signals.

# **6.5 From Case to Federation: Nested Adoption in Practice (Estate → National Node → Continental ALMA)**

The preceding sections established ALMA as a polycentric governance architecture designed to convert distributed stewardship into portable legitimacy. However, a structural clarification is necessary for conceptual precision and institutional feasibility. At the continental level, ALMA is not composed of individual artist estates directly. Rather, it is constituted as a federation of national legacy associations, each of which aggregates, translates, and certifies the estates operating within its jurisdiction. This distinction is foundational to the architecture and essential to preserving its polycentric integrity.

---

## **6.5.1 The Constitutional Principle: Federation of National Nodes**

ALMA Continental is structured as a federation of federations. Its members are not individual estates from Peru, Ecuador, Colombia, Argentina, or Mexico; rather, its members are the legally constituted national associations representing those estates, such as ALMA Perú, ALMA Ecuador, ALMA Colombia, ALMA Argentina, ALMA México, and analogous entities in other jurisdictions.

Each national association represents a cluster of estates embedded within a shared legal and regulatory environment. This nested structure accomplishes three essential design objectives. First, it preserves jurisdictional coherence by ensuring that standards are implemented within nationally compliant governance frameworks. Second, it prevents macro-layer overload by avoiding the direct governance of dozens of heterogeneous estates across multiple jurisdictions. Third, it ensures that standards are translated, operationalized, and stabilized locally before being aggregated into a continental signal.

The continental body therefore operates through recognized national nodes rather than through direct membership of individual estates.

---

## **6.5.2 Why Estates Do Not Directly Join ALMA Continental**

If individual estates were permitted to join the continental federation directly, three structural instabilities would arise.

First, jurisdictional incoherence would emerge. Continental standards would inevitably intersect with nationally specific inheritance law, nonprofit regulation, moral rights enforcement regimes, taxation frameworks, and cultural property controls. Without a national translation layer capable of adapting standards into locally compliant governance templates, compliance would become legally unstable and operationally fragile.

Second, governance instability would follow. A continental body composed of numerous estates across multiple legal systems would struggle to coordinate voting rights, manage disputes, enforce standards, and maintain procedural coherence without drifting toward centralized micromanagement. Such drift would undermine the polycentric design constraint established earlier in this chapter.

Third, the compliance signal itself would weaken. If ALMA Continental certified estates directly, the resulting assurance would reflect heterogeneous local compliance conditions. Signal consistency would erode because continental certification would no longer rest upon jurisdictionally stabilized foundations.

The national association resolves these problems by ensuring that estates first meet standards translated and validated within their domestic legal frameworks before being aggregated into a continental recognition regime.

---

## **6.5.3 The Nested Legitimacy Pipeline**

The architecture must therefore be understood as a structured legitimacy pipeline operating across three levels: micro, meso, and macro.

At the micro level, individual estates undertake the work of professionalized stewardship. An Ecuadorian, Colombian, Peruvian, or Argentine estate formalizes its governance structure, adopts RSA-based integrity discipline, implements Minimum Viable Standards, and subjects its procedures to national-level review. Archival consolidation, authentication workflows, documentation standardization, and succession planning occur at this level. The estate remains sovereign over interpretation and rights governance, but its authority becomes proceduralized and auditable.

At the meso level, the national legacy association performs the decisive function of jurisdictional translation and aggregation. It converts ALMA standards into locally compliant legal frameworks, pools professional expertise, conducts certification reviews, manages disputes within national jurisdiction, and interfaces with domestic cultural institutions. Through this process, estate-level compliance becomes a nationally recognized assurance. The meso node transforms isolated governance discipline into a jurisdictionally validated signal.

At the macro level, ALMA Continental recognizes compliant national associations rather than individual estates. It maintains versioned standards, coordinates interoperability across jurisdictions, aggregates reputational signals across countries, and engages transnational institutions operating within the broader art field. The macro layer certifies national nodes, not estates. This preserves polycentric separation of powers and ensures that continental recognition rests upon stabilized national foundations.

---

## **6.5.4 Scaling Across Fifty or More Estates**

The corrected topology has direct implications for scalability. With more than fifty historically significant estates aligned in principle across Latin America, the architecture cannot function through direct continental membership of individual estates without sacrificing coherence.

Under the federated model, estates organize within their respective national associations. Each national association formalizes governance templates, integrates RSA discipline, and certifies estate-level compliance. The national association then becomes the recognized member of ALMA Continental.

The scaling logic therefore shifts from “fifty estates reporting to one continental body” to “a limited number of national associations federated at the continental level.” Macro-level complexity decreases while signal coherence increases. This structure mirrors established federated governance models in international museum and heritage networks, where national committees constitute the operative units within broader associations.

---

## **6.5.5 Power Separation and Capture Resistance**

The nested structure strengthens capture resistance by distributing authority across layers.

Estates cannot bypass national review by appealing directly to the continental federation. The continental body cannot intervene in estate-level disputes without eroding polycentric autonomy. National associations retain legal independence within their jurisdictions. Standards evolve at the macro level, implementation occurs at the meso level, and execution remains at the micro level.

No single layer controls both certification and execution. This separation of functions preserves institutional resilience and aligns with the anti-fragility logic articulated earlier in the thesis.

---

## **6.5.6 Layered Incentive Transmission**

Under the corrected architecture, the Compliance Premium becomes layered rather than singular. Estate-level adherence generates national certification. National certification generates continental recognition. Continental recognition produces transnational institutional confidence within the Triad of Validation.

Each layer adds assurance without erasing local sovereignty. Legitimacy compounds incrementally, and governance discipline becomes progressively more portable across jurisdictions.

---

## **6.5.7 Structural Synthesis**

ALMA is not a network of estates. It is a federation of national legacy associations, each of which is itself a federation of estates. This nested architecture preserves local stewardship craft, absorbs jurisdictional friction, aggregates signal strength, prevents centralization, scales through replication, and stabilizes legitimacy as a network property.

Only under this topology does ALMA remain faithful to its polycentric design constraint and to the regenerative governance principles from which it was derived.

# **6.6 Incentive Transmission and the Compliance Premium Across the Triad of Validation**

The viability of ALMA depends not on normative appeal but on incentive compatibility. As a result, stewardship expenditures at the estate level behave as private sunk costs rather than as shared infrastructure.

ALMA alters this dynamic by introducing a layered compliance signal that reduces marginal verification cost across contexts. This reduction constitutes what may be termed a Compliance Premium: a measurable decrease in uncertainty discount arising from recognized procedural adherence.

The Compliance Premium does not derive from aesthetic judgment, nor from claims of canonical superiority. It arises from governance discipline that is both standardized and externally legible.

The incentive for academia is therefore reduced research risk and increased explanatory continuity. In aggregate, this lowers the cost of historiographic integration, allowing neglected legacies to enter academic discourse more readily.

The Compliance Premium at this level manifests as increased scholarly uptake probability.

The Compliance Premium at the institutional level manifests as increased loan acceptance probability, smoother acquisition review, and improved insurance underwriting conditions.

Art markets price risk. Where provenance chains are unstable, governance is opaque, or disputes are likely, buyers demand price concessions to compensate for potential future loss.

## **6.7 Network Effects and Uncertainty Minimization: From Local Stewardship to Portable Legitimacy**

The argument of Section 6.6 established that ALMA is incentive-compatible insofar as it produces a Compliance Premium: a reduction in uncertainty discount that is jointly recognized across the Triad of Validation. The decisive adoption question is whether this premium can become systemic—that is, whether it can compound through network effects such that ALMA-compliant stewardship becomes a default professional baseline rather than a niche practice.

In art markets, adoption equilibria are reached when actors begin to treat a verification pathway as “normal” because it is repeatable, legible, and cheaper than bespoke alternatives. ALMA’s function is therefore to transform governance discipline from an isolated estate attribute into an ecosystem-level coordination convention.

ALMA generates network effects because its primary output is not a scarce object but a shared interface. Interfaces exhibit increasing returns: their value rises as more actors treat them as common reference points.

This effect is triadic.

* Across estates, shared standards enable horizontal interoperability: consistent identifiers, repeatable workflows, and comparable assurance levels lower the marginal cost of evaluating any new estate within the network.

* Across institutions, protocol familiarity reduces friction: registrars, curators, insurers, and lenders can reuse the same verification logic across multiple legacies rather than rebuilding bespoke processes for each.

* Across markets, recognizable compliance signals reduce uncertainty discounting: collectors and intermediaries can price risk with greater resolution when governance quality is standardized and externally legible.

The network effect is therefore best described as the progressive conversion of idiosyncratic verification into reusable infrastructure.

The adoption implication follows directly: ALMA does not need universal membership to matter. It needs to reach a threshold at which the marginal actor—an insurer, a registrar, a reputable dealer—begins to treat ALMA-compliant records as the low-friction path. Once that threshold is crossed, adoption becomes self-reinforcing: compliance reduces costs; reduced costs increase usage; increased usage increases institutional familiarity; familiarity increases the value of compliance.

### **6.7.2 Adoption as a Coordination Equilibrium in the Triad of Validation**

Because legitimacy is field-structured, adoption cannot be defined by estate participation alone. A network of estates may be internally coherent yet remain externally irrelevant if the Triad does not treat its procedures as professionally meaningful.

ALMA therefore targets a coordination equilibrium defined by the Triad:

* Academia adopts when ALMA outputs become citation-ready inputs: stable identifiers, durable reference standards, and corrigible but auditable updates that support historiography rather than destabilize it.

* Institutions adopt when ALMA workflows reduce custodial and reputational risk: standardized due diligence pathways, disclosed governance discipline, and evidentiary continuity that lowers institutional liability.

* Markets adopt when ALMA signals reduce uncertainty discounting: works become more liquid, consignments become less frictional, and provenance confidence becomes portable across intermediaries.

The crucial point is that these adoptions are mutually reinforcing. Institutional confidence increases scholarly willingness to engage; scholarly discourse strengthens institutional programming; institutional engagement stabilizes market perception. Conversely, failure in any node weakens the others. The adoption problem is therefore not additive but systemic: ALMA must become legible across all three nodes for the Compliance Premium to stabilize.

A further implication follows from the entropy thesis: if ALMA increases reliance on unverifiable claims, it recreates the very fragility it seeks to solve. Adoption must therefore be structured as a movement away from trust-by-testimony toward trust-by-procedure.

This is the sense in which ALMA is conjectural but criticizable. Its legitimacy claim is not “believe the network,” but “verify the process.” The architecture aims to minimize the domain in which actors must rely on discretionary belief.

Here the RSA becomes functionally central to adoption. Without an integrity substrate, ALMA risks degenerating into a reputational club whose claims are expensive to test and easy to imitate. With the RSA, ALMA can anchor its compliance claims to an append-only record that makes critical components verifiable across time—even when the institutional environment changes.

ALMA, in this framing, is not an alternative to the RSA. It is the adoption wrapper that turns RSA-based integrity into a professionally recognized coordination convention. The RSA supplies the minimal verifiable skeleton; ALMA supplies the social and institutional interface through which that skeleton becomes legible to the Triad.

The network-effect logic becomes more decisive under synthetic entropy conditions. As generative systems increase the plausibility of forged images, fabricated documentation, and artificial provenance narratives, the scarcity shifts from information to confidence in process. Raw data becomes cheaper; attested procedures become more valuable.

In this environment, the adoption advantage belongs to systems that can prove not merely what a record says, but how and when it was produced, and under which procedural constraints. ALMA’s role is to standardize and attest workflows; the RSA’s role is to preserve the time-ordered integrity of those attestations without forcing public disclosure.

Portable legitimacy therefore becomes the central adoption output: not the portability of documents, but the portability of assurance.

ALMA does not claim authority over artistic meaning, aesthetic evaluation, or canonical ranking. Its authority is restricted to procedural governance. By limiting its mandate to workflow discipline—authentication standards, documentation baselines, conflict-of-interest disclosure, succession planning, RSA integration—it reduces the political and symbolic incentives for capture.

High-value capture typically targets narrative power or market gatekeeping. By structuring its legitimacy claim around process rather than canonization, ALMA minimizes the domain over which authority rents can accumulate.

This constraint preserves plural interpretive space and reduces the likelihood that the federation becomes an arbiter of taste.

Signal dilution occurs when certification is permanent or unconditional. To prevent this, ALMA certification must remain:

* versioned,

Enforcement mechanisms remain reputational rather than coercive, including:

* certification suspension,

* public downgrade of assurance level,

* restriction of access to shared services,

* limitation of federation-level recognition.

These responses preserve voluntary participation while ensuring that standards remain meaningful.

The signal remains valuable precisely because it can be withdrawn.

* Macro: the federation defines versioned standards but does not control estate-level operations.

No single layer can monopolize both execution and certification. This separation reduces opportunities for discretionary manipulation and concentrates scrutiny at boundaries rather than centers.

Distributed oversight also reduces single-point failure risk. Governance failure at one node does not collapse the entire system.

Institutional corrigibility achieves two objectives:

Corrigibility at the institutional layer ensures that ALMA does not transform into dogma.

## **Interoperability with Global Frameworks: A Three-World Synthesis**

### **7.0 From Regional Architecture to Epistemic Sufficiency**

Chapters 1 through 6 diagnosed chronic posthumous devaluation as a structural coordination failure and advanced a dual architecture designed to arrest cultural entropy: the **Registry of Shielded Artworks (RSA)** as a technical integrity substrate, and **ALMA** as a polycentric governance network capable of converting procedural discipline into portable legitimacy. Together, these components were proposed not as isolated reforms, but as a regenerative system intended to stabilize authorship, provenance, and stewardship across generational time horizons.

Yet architectural coherence within a regional context does not, by itself, establish feasibility. An institutional design remains theoretically incomplete unless it demonstrates interoperability with the dominant global regimes that organize institutional preservation, fiduciary risk management, and scholarly memory. Cultural fields are not reset by the introduction of new protocols; they are shaped by entrenched practices, established standards, and distributed epistemic authorities. If RSA–ALMA cannot interoperate with these external constraint systems without contradiction, redundancy, or capture, the architecture remains a local innovation rather than a durable solution.

This chapter therefore performs a **methodological stress test**. It evaluates the regenerative architecture against three high-salience global frameworks corresponding to distinct but interdependent domains of cultural life: the **ReACH** initiative in institutional preservation ethics, the **Responsible Art Market (RAM)** principles in transaction discipline, and contemporary historiography on Latin American modernism, particularly the work of **Michelle Greet**. To complete the analysis, a fourth lens is introduced: **provenance and catalogue raisonné infrastructure** as developed by organizations such as **IFAR** and codified through museum fiduciary norms.

The objective is not validation by association. Rather, it is to determine whether RSA–ALMA supply the **persistence conditions** these frameworks presuppose but do not themselves provide.

To clarify the structure of this stress test, the analysis is framed through **Karl Popper’s three-world ontology (1972, 1978\)**. In Popper’s schema:

* **World 1** consists of material objects and physical states.  
* **World 2** encompasses subjective intentions, ethical commitments, and institutional behavior.  
* **World 3** contains objective knowledge—documents, narratives, catalogues, and the accumulated products of human thought.

Cultural entropy manifests differently within each domain. In World 1, entropy appears as physical decay, destruction, or loss. In World 2, it appears as incentive misalignment, opportunism, or coordination fatigue. In World 3, it appears as fragmentation, unstable attribution, and historiographic erasure.

The Three-World framework is not employed here as a metaphorical overlay. It specifies the loci at which disorder enters cultural systems. A regenerative architecture must therefore reduce entropy at all three levels simultaneously. Stabilizing material objects without aligning incentives reintroduces disorder through opportunistic behavior (**World 2 drift**). Harmonizing ethical intent without securing documentation leaves knowledge vulnerable to drift (**World 3 fragmentation**). Producing scholarship without durable referential identity prevents cumulative growth. Failure in any single domain destabilizes the others.

The question addressed in this chapter is therefore whether RSA–ALMA function as a cross-world coordination mechanism. Do they merely stabilize records and standardize procedure locally, or do they align **World 1 material continuity**, **World 2 ethical agency**, and **World 3 narrative persistence** under global institutional conditions?

The answer determines whether the regenerative model constitutes a regional reform or a system capable of **epistemic sufficiency**.

## **7.1 World 1: ReACH and the Legitimacy of Mediated Heritage**

World 1, in Popper’s ontology, concerns material objects and physical states. In the context of artist estates, it encompasses the artworks themselves: paintings subject to chemical decay, sculptures vulnerable to environmental exposure, land art susceptible to erosion, and archives exposed to climate instability, conflict, and neglect. Cultural entropy at the World 1 level manifests as physical degradation, loss, destruction, and the irreversible disappearance of material evidence.

The ReACH initiative (Reproduction of Art and Cultural Heritage), developed under the leadership of the Victoria and Albert Museum (V\&A), emerges as a response to precisely this vulnerability. In a landscape marked by environmental risk, geopolitical instability, and technological transformation, ReACH advances a normative claim: that reproduction, digitization, and technological mediation may be not merely permissible but ethically required to preserve cultural memory under conditions of material fragility (ReACH, 2017). Authenticity, within this framework, is reframed as cultural continuity rather than strict material uniqueness. The value of an artwork resides not solely in its physical substrate, but in the persistence of its meaning, accessibility, and intelligibility across time and circumstance.

This intervention addresses a genuine preservation dilemma. If adherence to material singularity prohibits reproduction, and if material vulnerability threatens extinction, then a rigid authenticity doctrine may inadvertently accelerate loss. ReACH therefore performs an important ethical recalibration: it expands the legitimate perimeter of preservation practice in order to prevent World 1 disappearance.

However, the ethical legitimacy of reproduction presupposes a structural condition that ReACH does not itself secure. It presupposes the existence of a stable system of version differentiation, here defined as version integrity. For reproduction to safeguard continuity rather than generate confusion, the field must be able to distinguish, across generational time horizons, between the original object, authorized reproductions, conservation interventions, digital surrogates, and unauthorized copies. Without structured differentiation, the proliferation of reproductions risks introducing ambiguity into World 3 knowledge. Identity drift occurs when successive mediations blur categorical boundaries. Over time, surrogates may cannibalize the authority of originals, or institutional trust may erode under uncertainty regarding provenance and authorization.

From the perspective of the entropy thesis developed in Chapters 1 and 2, this constitutes a structural gap. Reproduction ethics without durable governance can inadvertently increase informational disorder. The preservation of World 1 objects must therefore be coupled to a mechanism that stabilizes identity across time.

It is precisely here that the RSA–ALMA architecture demonstrates interoperability. The Registry of Shielded Artworks (RSA) provides a formal grammar for versioning. Originals, authorized reproductions, conservation treatments, edition disclosures, and digital surrogates are encoded as distinct, time-ordered event types within an append-only registry structure. Each event anchors the fact that a specific procedural workflow was executed at a specific moment under defined authority. Identity differentiation is preserved not through static declarations, but through cumulative, auditable sequence. Version integrity becomes infrastructural rather than rhetorical.

ALMA complements this technical layer with governance standardization. By harmonizing reproduction policies, disclosure thresholds, committee procedures, and documentation protocols across estates and national associations, ALMA reduces jurisdictional drift in how reproduction is authorized and recorded. The legitimacy of mediated heritage becomes not merely an ethical claim but a procedurally stabilized practice. Reproduction decisions remain under estate sovereignty, yet they are documented within a shared, versioned framework that preserves clarity across institutional interfaces.

The significance of this interoperability becomes evident when examined against boundary conditions such as those explored in the Emilio Rodríguez-Larraín (ERL) pilot. In cases involving posthumous sculptural editions, unrealized architectural works, or land art reconstructions, the ontological status of the “original” is often distributed across site, design, and fabrication. The artwork may exist simultaneously as blueprint, spatial intervention, and later realization. Under such conditions, reproduction is not deviation but continuation. Yet absent formal differentiation and disclosure discipline, these acts risk market suspicion, scholarly fragmentation, or reputational instability. RSA–ALMA enable such interventions to function as documented continuity rather than speculative multiplication.

In Three-World terms, the architecture ensures that World 1 preservation does not destabilize World 3 knowledge. Material continuity is aligned with epistemic clarity. ReACH articulates the ethical necessity of mediation; RSA–ALMA supply the persistence conditions under which that mediation can remain intelligible, auditable, and trustworthy across time, institutions, and markets.

If reproduction is to serve continuity rather than entropy, ethics must be coupled with infrastructure. RSA–ALMA do not replace ReACH. They operationalize its premise under conditions of institutional fragility and generational drift, ensuring that preservation expands cultural memory without dissolving its referential integrity.

### **7.2 World 2: RAM and the Infrastructure of Transaction Discipline**

If World 1 concerns material vulnerability, World 2 concerns human agency: intentions, incentives, fiduciary judgment, and institutional behavior. In the art market ecosystem, World 2 manifests through dealers, auction specialists, collectors, trustees, compliance officers, insurers, and estate stewards. Cultural entropy at this level appears not as physical decay, but as incentive misalignment, opportunism, coordination fatigue, and procedural inconsistency. Even when objects are preserved (World 1\) and documentation exists (World 3), weak alignment in World 2 can reintroduce disorder through negligence, asymmetry of information, or fragmented due diligence practices.

The Responsible Art Market (RAM) initiative emerged in response to precisely these vulnerabilities. Established under the auspices of the Geneva Free Ports and later expanded through international collaboration, RAM articulates principles and practical guidance designed to promote responsible conduct in art transactions. Its “Art Transaction Due Diligence Toolkit” provides structured recommendations for provenance research, beneficial ownership verification, conflict-of-interest disclosure, sanctions compliance, and risk assessment (RAM, 2017; updated 2021). RAM’s contribution is normative and procedural: it defines what responsible actors *ought* to do when engaging in art transactions.

The initiative recognizes that art markets are structurally prone to information asymmetry. Following Akerlof’s classic “lemons” framework (1970), when buyers cannot reliably distinguish high-quality works from uncertain ones, price incorporates risk. In art markets, the consequences are amplified. Reputational damage, restitution claims, authenticity disputes, and title challenges can produce irreversible institutional consequences. RAM therefore seeks to mitigate these risks by encouraging repeatable due diligence procedures and transparency in transactional conduct.

Yet RAM presupposes a condition that fragmented estates frequently fail to provide. It assumes that the outputs of due diligence are reusable. When a provenance investigation is performed, when authenticity is evaluated, when ownership documentation is verified, the resulting work is expected to accumulate value within the object’s documentary history. In practice, however, such efforts often remain episodic. Due diligence is conducted in the form of internal memoranda, private PDFs, or transaction-specific reports that are neither standardized nor preserved in a durable, interoperable format. The next transaction frequently reopens the same questions. Verification resets rather than compounds.

From the perspective of the entropy thesis, this represents a coordination tax imposed at the World 2 level. Each actor must independently reconstruct assurance because there is no trusted substrate to which prior verification can attach. Ethical conduct becomes costly and non-accumulative. Under such conditions, rational actors may discount works associated with weak estate governance or opaque documentation, even in the absence of concrete irregularity. The uncertainty premium identified in earlier chapters is therefore not merely informational; it is behavioral. It reflects the rational pricing of World 2 coordination risk.

The RSA–ALMA architecture addresses this structural gap by converting transaction discipline from an ephemeral act into an infrastructural feature. The Registry of Shielded Artworks (RSA) transforms due diligence outputs into time-ordered, append-only attestations linked to Canonical Artwork IDs (CAIDs). Provenance confirmations, authentication determinations, transfer recognitions, and condition reports become anchored events rather than isolated documents. The critical shift lies in making verification cumulative. Once an event is recorded within a structured integrity chain, subsequent actors may reference it as part of a persistent evidentiary sequence, subject to corrigibility but resistant to silent erasure.

In this sense, the RSA renders RAM executable. RAM articulates what responsible behavior entails; RSA reduces the marginal cost of enacting and preserving that behavior across transactions. Ethical diligence becomes economically rational when its outputs persist and compound.

ALMA complements this technical substrate by coordinating recognition at the institutional level. Through standardized authentication procedures, conflict-of-interest safeguards, succession protocols, and certification mechanisms, ALMA aligns estate practices with the expectations embedded in RAM guidance. This does not centralize authority over transactions. Rather, it ensures that estates participating in the network produce documentation that is procedurally legible to market intermediaries operating under RAM principles. The compliance premium described in Chapter 6 becomes actionable because fiduciary actors encounter reduced coordination friction when interacting with ALMA-certified estates.

Importantly, this interoperability does not impose moral heroism. It does not require actors to behave ethically against their incentives. Instead, it alters the incentive landscape itself. When cumulative verification reduces uncertainty discounting and lowers due diligence cost, disciplined participation becomes the rational equilibrium. Ethical conduct is no longer a discretionary virtue; it is embedded in infrastructural design.

In Three-World terms, RSA–ALMA stabilize World 2 behavior by anchoring it to World 3 documentation while preserving World 1 object integrity. Transaction discipline becomes attached to durable knowledge artifacts rather than resting solely on trust in individual actors. The architecture thus reduces behavioral entropy without suppressing market freedom.

If cultural entropy at the World 2 level arises from episodic diligence and non-portable trust, regenerative stewardship requires a substrate in which responsible conduct compounds rather than evaporates. RAM supplies the ethical grammar of transaction discipline. RSA–ALMA provide the persistence conditions under which that grammar can operate across generational and jurisdictional boundaries.

### **7.3 World 3: Historiography and the Infrastructure of the Canon**

If World 1 concerns material continuity and World 2 concerns behavioral alignment, World 3 encompasses objective knowledge: documents, catalogues, narratives, classifications, and the accumulated products of interpretive labor. In the context of artist estates, World 3 includes catalogues raisonnés, exhibition histories, scholarly articles, archival inventories, curatorial essays, and the historiographic frameworks through which artists are positioned within broader movements. Cultural entropy at the World 3 level manifests as fragmentation, unstable attribution, citation drift, narrative marginalization, and eventual historiographic erasure.

The scholarship of Michelle Greet on Latin American modernism provides a particularly relevant lens through which to examine this domain. In *Transatlantic Encounters* (2018) and subsequent research into Andean abstraction (2022, 2024), Greet demonstrates that canon formation is not a neutral reflection of artistic merit, but a function of institutional infrastructure, exhibition networks, patronage concentration, and sustained scholarly visibility. Artists from the "Atlantic nuclei" of Brazil, Argentina, and Venezuela achieved international recognition in part because concentrated institutional resources and transnational circuits reinforced their visibility. By contrast, Andean abstraction—despite its aesthetic sophistication and independent development—remained structurally marginalized due to weaker institutional anchoring and reduced narrative propagation (Greet, 2024).

This analysis reframes devaluation not as aesthetic insufficiency but as infrastructural deficit. Historiography, in this view, is not merely interpretive commentary; it is an accumulative system that depends upon durable referents. Citation stability presupposes archival stability. Narrative recalibration presupposes documentary coherence. A scholar cannot build a cumulative argument if the primary sources, attribution records, or object identities remain unstable across time.

Here lies the structural entropic gap. Contemporary art-historical revisionism seeks to recalibrate the canon, expand representation, and correct historical exclusion. Yet these efforts often operate within a fragile documentary substrate. When catalogues raisonnés are incomplete, archives dispersed, or authentication authority contested, scholarship remains vulnerable to interruption. Interpretive innovation cannot compound without infrastructural persistence.

From the perspective of the entropy thesis, this represents a failure of World 3 self-reinforcement. Objective knowledge requires stable referential anchors. If identity, provenance, and documentation remain in flux, historiographic growth becomes episodic rather than cumulative.

The RSA–ALMA architecture addresses this gap by supplying what may be termed citation-stable identity. The Registry of Shielded Artworks (RSA) assigns Canonical Artwork IDs (CAIDs) that function as persistent referential anchors. These identifiers are linked to versioned documentation histories rather than static declarations. When a scholar cites a work, the citation can reference a structured integrity chain rather than an ephemeral archive box or privately held PDF. Corrigibility remains possible; prior attributions can be amended, but the revision history is preserved rather than erased. World 3 thus acquires temporal depth rather than oscillating between contradictory claims.

ALMA complements this technical substrate by structuring the interface between private estates and public scholarship. Through standardized disclosure protocols, committee governance, and documentation templates, ALMA reduces the friction that typically separates heirs from researchers. Importantly, this does not centralize interpretive authority. ALMA distinguishes between procedural authority—how documentation is produced and verified—and interpretive authority—what a work means. This decoupling preserves pluralism within World 3\. Scholars remain free to contest stylistic classification, movement affiliation, or conceptual framing. What becomes stabilized is the evidentiary foundation upon which such debate occurs.

The significance of this distinction becomes particularly clear when revisiting the ERL pilot case. The historiographic marginalization of Andean abstraction was not solely a function of critical neglect; it was compounded by archival fragmentation, incomplete sculptural inventories, and informal reproduction practices. Without a consolidated referential substrate, even sympathetic scholarship encounters limits. RSA–ALMA do not dictate narrative correction. Rather, they create the persistence conditions under which narrative correction can endure.

This interoperability extends beyond Greet’s scholarship to broader provenance and catalogue raisonné practice. Organizations such as the International Foundation for Art Research (IFAR) and professional associations for catalogue raisonné authors emphasize methodological transparency, documentation standards, and revision protocols. Yet the production and maintenance of catalogues raisonnés remain resource-intensive, often dependent on the "heroic labor" of individual scholars or foundations. RSA–ALMA do not replace these scholarly artifacts. Instead, they provide an audit-preserving substrate for their revision history. Amendments, newly discovered works, contested attributions, and posthumous clarifications become versioned events rather than disruptive resets.

In Three-World terms, the architecture strengthens World 3 by anchoring it to World 1 objects and World 2 procedures. Material continuity is documented; behavioral discipline is recorded; knowledge artifacts reference persistent identifiers. Historiography becomes infrastructurally supported rather than precariously reconstructed at each generation.

If cultural entropy at the World 3 level manifests as narrative drift and referential instability, regenerative stewardship requires more than interpretive ambition. It requires stable knowledge architecture. Contemporary historiography provides the critical impetus for recalibration. RSA–ALMA provide the structural conditions under which recalibration can compound rather than dissipate.

Canon formation is not reversed by argument alone. It is sustained by infrastructure.

### **7.4 Provenance Infrastructure and Catalogue Raisonné Practice: The Operational Layer of World 3**

If Section 7.3 examined historiography as narrative infrastructure, this section turns to the technical mechanisms through which that narrative is stabilized, revised, and transmitted across time. Between scholarly interpretation and market transaction lies a specialized evidentiary domain: provenance research and catalogue raisonné production. These are not peripheral conventions; they constitute the operational “pipes” of World 3\. Without their disciplined maintenance, neither canon formation nor transaction ethics can sustain continuity across generations.

#### **7.4.1 Definitions and Historical Function**

The catalogue raisonné—literally a “reasoned catalog”—represents the art world’s highest scholarly form of oeuvre consolidation. Emerging in the eighteenth century, notably with Edme-François Gersaint’s 1751 catalogue of Rembrandt’s etchings, the format evolved from simple inventory into a structured methodology. As defined by the International Foundation for Art Research (IFAR) and the Catalogue Raisonné Scholars Association (CRSA), a raisonné integrates physical description, attribution analysis, provenance history, bibliographic references, and exhibition records into a canonical accounting of an artist’s known production. A catalogue raisonné entry does not merely list an object; it situates it within a structured evidentiary framework that distinguishes legitimate works from informational noise and establishes stable referential identity within World 3\.

Provenance research functions as the primary evidentiary substrate of this framework. It reconstructs an object’s custodial history from creation to the present, mapping World 2 agency—who possessed the work, under what conditions, and through what transfers—onto World 3 documentation—how those transitions were recorded and institutionalized. Under standards articulated by bodies such as the Zentralinstitut für Kunstgeschichte (ZI) in Munich and the Getty Research Institute, and reinforced by the museum sector’s response to the 1970 UNESCO Convention, provenance has become a fiduciary obligation. Organizations like the German Lost Art Foundation emphasize that systematic provenance is the *conditio sine qua non* for establishing historical justice and market legitimacy.

#### **7.4.2 The Vulnerability of "Heroic Labor" and Revision Opacity**

Despite their elevated normative status, these practices remain structurally fragile. Catalogue raisonné production is frequently dependent on the “heroic labor” model: a single scholar or a small estate foundation functioning as the central authority. This concentration creates a single point of failure; if the scholar passes or the foundation ceases operations due to litigation fears—as seen with the Warhol and Pollock-Krasner foundations—World 3 knowledge can become frozen or discarded.

A second vulnerability lies in revision opacity. In traditional print catalogues, corrections often appear as silent updates in later editions. When a work is reattributed or newly discovered, the historical reasoning behind earlier judgments is often overwritten rather than preserved. This lack of temporal depth makes it difficult to reconstruct the evolution of scholarly consensus, eroding the long-term reliability of the World 3 record.

#### **7.4.3 Regenerative Architecture as an Interoperability Layer**

The Regenerative Architecture proposed in this thesis addresses these vulnerabilities by functioning as a **persistence enhancer** for existing institutional standards. It does not replace the expertise of **IFAR** or the **ZI**; it provides them with a technical and institutional substrate that prevents their labor from evaporating.

* **RSA as the Integrity Substrate:** The **Registry of Shielded Artworks** assigns **Canonical Artwork IDs (CAIDs)**that exist independently of any single database. Inclusion in a catalogue raisonné becomes a time-stamped, version-aware event. If an attribution is contested or updated, the RSA preserves the Reasoning History as a structured sequence. Corrigibility is preserved, but erasure is prevented.  
* **ALMA as the Procedural Wrapper:** ALMA aligns estate practice with the fiduciary expectations of the **Association of Art Museum Directors (AAMD)** and the **American Alliance of Museums (AAM)**. By using the **Assurance Ladder**, ALMA ensures that provenance research is conducted as a proactive, cumulative governance task rather than a reactive, episodic response to a sale.

In Three-World terms, this completes the infrastructural loop. World 1 objects are linked to persistent identifiers; World 2 agencies operate within standardized, auditable workflows; and World 3 knowledge artifacts acquire temporal resilience. By transforming the art world’s "pipes" into a distributed memory structure, the Regenerative Architecture ensures that the labor of the scholar and the fiduciary obligations of the steward **compound rather than dissipate**.

# **Conclusion**

## **From Uneasiness to Stewardship: Designing Against Cultural Entropy**

Cultural value does not persist by merit alone. In the absence of durable coordination, trust, and stewardship infrastructures, even historically grounded and institutionally engaged artistic legacies drift toward entropy. This thesis has argued that posthumous devaluation is therefore not an anomaly, but a structural default.

The inquiry began with a personal problem-situation: the posthumous fragmentation of the artistic legacy of Emilio Rodríguez-Larraín. What initially appeared as an inheritance challenge marked by dispersion, documentation gaps, piecemeal sales, and succession friction proved, upon closer examination, to be an instance of a broader and recurring pattern. Across countries, institutional contexts, and generations, artist estates frequently lose coherence, legitimacy, and value after the artist’s death—even when artistic quality and historical relevance are not in question.

The task of this thesis was not to narrate this difficulty, but to explain why it occurs so reliably.

Discovering the critical rationalism of Karl Popper and the problem-solving epistemology developed by David Deutsch marked a decisive intellectual turning point in this process. Their emphasis on explanatory adequacy over the accumulation of facts reframed uneasiness as an epistemic signal: if decline recurs across diverse contexts, its cause must be structural. Rather than attributing posthumous devaluation to isolated mismanagement, moral failure, or insufficient recognition, the inquiry sought a hard-to-vary explanation capable of accounting for systematic drift under varying conditions.

That explanation was developed through the concept of Cultural Entropy. In this thesis, cultural entropy denotes the directional dissipation of coherence in long-horizon cultural systems once the artist—the primary coordinating agent—is no longer present. When responsibility for authorship, documentation, narrative authority, and verification is distributed across multiple actors without integrated coordination mechanisms, archives fragment, provenance weakens, and verification must be repeatedly reconstructed. This drift does not result from aesthetic insufficiency or bad faith, but from rising coordination costs that exceed the absorptive capacity of existing arrangements.

From this explanation follows a decisive insight: persistence is not automatic; it must be designed.

Drawing on regenerative economics, the thesis introduced a metabolic distinction between extractive and generative estate models. Extractive configurations consume legacy without reinvesting in the informational commons that sustains legibility. Generative configurations reinvest in documentation, governance, and verification, thereby stabilizing coherence over time. Regenerative principles—circulation, reinvestment, resilience, and empowered participation—thus became architectural criteria rather than merely normative aspirations. Living systems theory reinforced this perspective by framing estates as adaptive systems whose viability depends on continuous informational metabolism, while network and polycentric governance theory clarified how distributed coordination can reduce systemic friction without centralizing authority.

The estate of Emilio Rodríguez-Larraín functioned as a boundary-condition case through which these dynamics were examined concretely. The case demonstrated both the possibility of regeneration under constraint and the limits of estate-level action. Certain failures—repeated verification resets, fragile market legibility, episodic institutional engagement—arise at the interfaces between estates, markets, institutions, and scholarship. These are system-level problems and require system-level responses.

This recognition led to the architectural core of the thesis.

The Registry of Shielded Artworks (RSA) was proposed as an integrity substrate designed to stabilize authoritative sequences over time. Its function is deliberately narrow but essential: to preserve the traceability and cumulative structure of claims concerning authorship, provenance, reproduction, and stewardship. The RSA does not determine meaning or canon; it preserves corrigibility and continuity so that knowledge need not be reconstructed from scratch at each transfer.

Integrity alone, however, does not ensure coordination.

For this reason, the thesis proposed ALMA (Asociación de Legados de la Modernidad Americana) as a complementary polycentric governance architecture. Rather than centralizing authority, ALMA aligns procedures across autonomous estates, translating stewardship practices into interoperable standards that reduce friction while preserving sovereignty. The architecture is not exclusive to a single region or network; its contribution lies in clarifying the structural condition necessary for long-term coordination.

Chapter 7 demonstrated that this architecture is interoperable with established international frameworks governing preservation ethics, responsible market conduct, and historiographic practice. It does not replace RAM, ReACH, or institutional norms; it provides the persistence layer through which such frameworks can reinforce one another rather than operate in isolation.

Importantly, the architecture has moved beyond theoretical formulation. Elements of the RSA are under technical development in collaboration with engineers at SHIELD Labs, and ALMA has begun to coalesce as a transnational network of artist estates across Latin America. These developments do not serve as proof of correctness, but as evidence of implementability: the conjecture is structurally actionable.

Taken together, this thesis makes four principal contributions. First, it reconceptualizes artist estates as living systems whose survival depends on continuous regeneration rather than static preservation. Second, it provides a structural explanation for posthumous devaluation grounded in coordination failure rather than aesthetic judgment or moral deficiency. Third, it translates explanatory epistemology and regenerative systems thinking into institutional and technical design within the cultural domain. Fourth, it proposes—and begins to implement—a practical architecture capable of resisting cultural entropy under real-world conditions of fragmentation and limited trust.

In the framework of critical rationalism, however, these proposals are not presented as final truths. They are conjectural design solutions (TS) to a clearly articulated problem-situation (P1). Their adequacy will be tested not by rhetorical defense, but by implementation, criticism, and the emergence of new problem-situations (P2). As RSA and ALMA are deployed, refined, and contested, further errors will be exposed and eliminated (EE). The architecture is therefore not a terminal state but an initiation of a new problem-solving cycle.

If cultural entropy is the default condition of unmanaged long-horizon systems, regeneration must be intentional. And if stewardship is to endure beyond individuals, it must become architectural.

This thesis offers one such conjectural architecture—now moving from theoretical design into lived implementation, where it will be tested, criticized, and refined through structured iteration in real time.

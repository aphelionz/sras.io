## **Conclusion**

### **From Uneasiness to Stewardship: Designing Against Cultural Entropy**

## **Abstract**

Why do so many artist legacies deteriorate after death—even when the work is historically significant, institutionally engaged, and widely respected?

This thesis argues that posthumous devaluation is not the result of neglect, poor intentions, or artistic failure. It is a structural default. Cultural value does not persist by merit alone. In the absence of durable coordination, trust, and stewardship infrastructure, artistic legacies drift toward fragmentation and uncertainty over time.

Drawing on the problem-solving epistemology of Karl Popper and David Deutsch, principles from regenerative economics, and theories of polycentric governance, this study treats artist estates as living systems. When the artist—the primary coordinating agent—disappears, coordination costs rise sharply. Without shared procedures capable of surviving succession, disagreement, and institutional change, coherence decays. Archives fragment, provenance weakens, institutional confidence erodes, and markets discount uncertainty. This directional tendency is described as cultural entropy.

Rather than proposing moral remedies or retrospective recognition, the thesis advances a design-oriented response. It develops a two-part regenerative architecture that shifts continuity from individuals to systems. The Registry of Shielded Artworks (RSA) is introduced as a technical integrity layer that stabilizes authorship, provenance, and stewardship claims over time, reducing the need for repeated verification. ALMA (Asociación de Legados de la Modernidad Americana) is proposed as a complementary polycentric governance network that diffuses shared stewardship protocols, enabling collective adoption without centralization or loss of estate sovereignty.

The architecture is stress-tested against real-world constraints, including fragmented archives, weak secondary markets, and jurisdictional complexity, using the estate of Emilio Rodríguez-Larraín as a boundary-condition case. It is further evaluated for interoperability with established international frameworks governing institutions (ReACH), markets (Responsible Art Market), and academic historiography.

The central contribution of this thesis is neither biographical nor normative. It is architectural. It demonstrates that cultural persistence depends not on exceptional individuals, but on ordinary adherence to well-designed systems. Trust must be encoded into process, not requested from persons. Stewardship becomes sustainable only when it is infrastructural rather than heroic.

The thesis concludes that while artistic death is inevitable, cultural decay is not. Entropy is the default—but regeneration can be designed.

## **Preface**

From Uneasiness to Explanation

“The motive to change is always some uneasiness.”

— John Locke, An Essay Concerning Human Understanding (1690, Book II, Ch. XXI)

The engine of this inquiry is uneasiness.

This uneasiness did not begin with the death of my father, the twentieth-century Peruvian visual artist Emilio Rodríguez-Larraín, on December 23, 2015\. It began years earlier, as I gradually witnessed his cognitive and physical decline. As his memory faded, I observed how his body of work and personal possessions were being divided, sold, and dispersed piecemeal—often driven by short-term necessity, yet dangerously lacking any cohesive long-term strategy. These two processes unfolded in parallel: as his mind deteriorated, his legacy fragmented. At the time, I experienced a concrete sense of powerlessness. I did not know what mechanisms existed to protect a fragile legacy, nor how an heir ought to act responsibly once the artist no longer had the capacity to do so.

When my father died, this uneasiness intensified. Grief was compounded by obligation. I inherited not only the emotional weight of loss, but the responsibility of stewarding a cultural legacy, alongside a complex set of legal, economic, and administrative complications for which I was wholly unprepared. I found myself returning repeatedly to a single unavoidable question: what, in practical terms, must be done to prevent a legacy from losing value, relevance, and meaning once the artist is no longer present?

Seeking answers, I enrolled in the Executive Master in Art Market Studies at the University of Zurich in 2017\. This decision was not driven by abstract academic curiosity, but by necessity. I required analytical tools capable of confronting inherited disorder with clarity rather than intuition. While my father’s estate was the initial catalyst, it soon became clear that the difficulties I encountered were not personal anomalies. Across Latin America—and increasingly beyond—I observed a recurring pattern: historically significant artist estates repeatedly confront coordination failure, institutional fragility, market opacity, and the gradual erosion of both symbolic and economic value after the artist’s death (Greet, 2018; see also later essays on Latin American modernism).

Rather than treating these outcomes as contingent stories of misfortune, this thesis interrogates the problem at the level of underlying structure. It advances three core claims:

1. Posthumous devaluation is not primarily a problem of artistic merit, recognition, or moral failure, but a predictable outcome of rising coordination costs once the artist—the primary coordinating agent—is no longer present.

2. This condition can be explained as a form of cultural entropy: a directional drift toward fragmentation and loss of coherence in long-horizon cultural systems that lack counter-entropic design.

3. Resisting this drift requires architecture rather than advocacy—specifically, a regenerative legacy-management model capable of stabilizing trust, verification, and coordination through shared infrastructure and polycentric governance.

Taken together, these claims reframe legacy decay not as an exception to be corrected, but as a default condition to be designed against.

In a Popperian framework, a problem does not originate in emotion alone, but in a contradiction between expectations and observed reality. The uneasiness that animates this inquiry arises from precisely such a conflict. On the one hand, there exists a widely shared expectation—held by artists, heirs, institutions, and the public—that cultural value and historical importance, once established, ought to endure across time. On the other hand, empirical observation repeatedly contradicts this belief. This contradiction between expected persistence and observed decay constitutes the core problem-situation addressed in this thesis.

Lived experience made this contradiction visible; it did not make it intelligible. In the absence of explanation, posthumous decline is typically attributed to contingent causes: mismanagement, insufficient recognition, or bad luck. Yet such accounts fail to explain why similar patterns recur across artists, regions, and institutional contexts. Treating these outcomes as isolated failures obscures their structural regularity.

Accordingly, the uneasiness that motivates this inquiry is treated as an epistemic signal: an indication that existing explanations are inadequate. Resolving the contradiction requires distance from particulars and a shift toward first-principles reasoning capable of yielding general, criticizable explanations. Inspired by the analytical tradition of Karl Popper and, in a contemporary register, David Deutsch, this thesis proceeds from explanation to conjecture, and from conjecture to design. What begins as a personal and overwhelming situation is deliberately reframed as a general problem—one that does not belong to a single individual, but can be examined, tested, and addressed by others.

Explanation alone, however, is insufficient. Understanding imposes responsibility. Once a problem is rendered intelligible, continued inaction becomes difficult to justify. The progression from diagnosis to conjecture, and from conjecture to design, is therefore logical rather than rhetorical. The regenerative legacy-management model developed here emerges as a conjectural solution: a theory-laden hypothesis about how cultural value might be stabilized under conditions of high coordination costs.

Importantly, this framework is already transitioning into practice. The Registry of Shielded Artworks (RSA), developed in Chapter 5, has moved beyond theory into early product design and implementation. In parallel, ALMA (Asociación de Legados de la Modernidad Americana) has been constituted as a polycentric institutional response to the coordination failures identified in this study. The Emilio Rodríguez-Larraín estate is a founding member, and ALMA is scheduled to publicly activate through an exhibition on July 4, 2026, at the Art Museum of the Americas (AMA) in Washington, D.C.

It is necessary to clarify the scope of this thesis. It does not seek to establish or defend the artistic merit of Emilio Rodríguez-Larraín, nor of any individual artist. The regenerative architecture proposed here is deliberately artist-agnostic. Cultural value does not persist by merit alone; it endures only where trust, stewardship, and coordination are structurally designed to outlast individuals.

What follows is therefore not a defense of a single artistic legacy, nor an attempt to secure recognition for one artist. It is an effort to identify and design the conditions under which any artist’s legacy can remain coherent, legible, and viable after the artist’s death. The uneasiness that initiated this inquiry is transformed here into a shared diagnostic and practical framework—one that can be examined, criticized, and adapted by others. Its purpose is to shift stewardship away from improvised, burdensome, and isolated efforts toward a coordinated practice in which cultural value persists through designed structures rather than individual struggle.

## **Introduction**

Artist Estates as Living Systems: Cultural Entropy, Regeneration, and the Architecture of Persistence

This thesis examines the posthumous fate of artist estates through a problem-oriented, explanatory, and design-driven epistemology. Drawing on the critical rationalism of Karl Popper and David Deutsch, together with principles from regenerative economics and theories of polycentric governance, it approaches artist estates as complex socio-cultural systems whose persistence depends on coordination, feedback, and long-term institutional coherence (Popper, 1959; Popper, 1963; Deutsch, 2011; Ostrom, 2010; Fullerton, 2015). The central objective is twofold: first, to explain why cultural value tends to decay by default after an artist’s death; and second, to develop architectural responses capable of resisting that decay over extended time horizons.

The thesis does not propose new philosophical doctrines. Its contribution lies instead in synthesis and application. It brings together three mature and well-established intellectual traditions—problem-oriented epistemology, regenerative systems thinking, and polycentric governance—and applies them to a domain in which they have rarely been integrated in a coherent and implementable manner: the long-term stewardship of artistic legacies. By doing so, it moves beyond descriptive diagnosis toward explanations capable of informing institutional and technical design.

---

### **Explanatory Framework**

From critical rationalism, the thesis adopts several foundational commitments. First, it treats inquiry as originating in problem-situations: moments in which existing explanations fail to account for observed outcomes (Popper, 1959; Popper, 1972). Second, it prioritizes explanation over induction, seeking accounts that explain why a pattern occurs rather than merely documenting that it does (Popper, 1963; Deutsch, 2011). Third, it adopts the criterion that explanations must be hard to vary—that is, resistant to ad hoc modification when confronted with new cases (Deutsch, 2011).

From regenerative economics and systems ecology, the thesis adopts the insight that complex socio-economic systems persist only insofar as they continuously regenerate the conditions of their own viability. Such systems endure not through static preservation, but through feedback loops, reinvestment, and institutional coherence that allow value to compound rather than dissipate over time (Bertalanffy, 1968; Meadows, 2008; Fullerton, 2015).

From polycentric governance theory, the thesis draws the principle that coordination among heterogeneous actors does not require centralization. Durable cooperation can emerge through interoperable standards, shared rules, reputational mechanisms, and nested institutional arrangements that preserve local autonomy while enabling collective action (Ostrom, 1990; Ostrom, 2010; Hess & Ostrom, 2007).

Taken together, these frameworks provide the analytical basis for treating artist estates as living systems: systems whose survival depends on their capacity to coordinate across actors, regenerate trust and verification, and adapt over time once the artist—the original coordinating agent—is no longer present.

---

### **The Problem Situation**

Across Latin America—and increasingly across other regions—the posthumous trajectories of modern artist estates exhibit a striking regularity. Artists whose work is historically significant, critically rigorous, and culturally consequential frequently experience systematic devaluation after their deaths. This devaluation rarely manifests as a single rupture. Instead, it unfolds gradually through the erosion of coherence: archives fragment, provenance weakens, authorship becomes contested, institutional relationships lapse, scholarly discourse thins, and markets discount rising uncertainty. Over time, narratives that once sustained relevance lose visibility and force (Greet, 2018; Velthuis, 2005).

Existing scholarship has documented many dimensions of this phenomenon. Art history has examined canon formation and narrative exclusion; market studies have analyzed opacity, illiquidity, and weak secondary markets; institutional analyses have highlighted underfunded infrastructures and governance fragility (Velthuis, 2005; Würtenberger, 2016). While these accounts are descriptively accurate, they leave unresolved a deeper explanatory question:

Why does cultural value so often fail to persist after an artist’s death, even when artistic quality, historical importance, and episodic recognition are present?

In Popperian terms, this question constitutes a problem-situation. It is neither a moral grievance nor an empirical anomaly, but a signal that prevailing explanations are inadequate. If posthumous devaluation recurs predictably across countries, generations, and institutional contexts, then its causes cannot be contingent or incidental. They must be structural.

---

### **Core Thesis Claims**

This thesis advances three interrelated claims:

1. Structural Devaluation

    Posthumous devaluation is not primarily a problem of artistic merit, recognition, or individual failure. It is a predictable outcome of rising coordination costs once the artist—the primary coordinating agent—is no longer present to stabilize authorship, narrative authority, and trust.

2. Cultural Entropy

    This outcome can be explained as a directional drift toward fragmentation and loss of coherence in long-horizon cultural systems that lack counter-entropic design. Cultural entropy, as used here, denotes a structural tendency rather than a metaphor: when coordination costs exceed the capacity of existing institutions to absorb them, decay becomes the default trajectory.

3. Regenerative Architecture

    Resisting this drift requires architecture rather than advocacy. Cultural value can persist only where trust, stewardship, and coordination are stabilized through shared infrastructure and polycentric governance capable of operating under uncertainty and across long time horizons.

Taken together, these claims reframe posthumous decay not as an exception to be corrected after the fact, but as a default condition that must be deliberately designed against.

---

### **Methodological Orientation**

Methodologically, the thesis rejects inductive case accumulation as insufficient for explaining a recurring structural failure. Instead, it adopts a first-principles, design-oriented approach inspired by Popper and Deutsch. Rather than asking which interventions succeed under favorable conditions, the inquiry is inverted:

* Under what conditions does cultural value reliably dissipate?

* What structural constraints make devaluation the default outcome?

* What minimal architectures could reverse that tendency under adverse conditions?

The estate of Emilio Rodríguez-Larraín is treated as a boundary-condition stress test. It is not approached biographically, but analytically—as a system exposed to maximal constraint. Lived experience functions as an epistemic trigger rather than as evidence. The aim is abstraction, generalization, and design.

---

### **Structure of the Thesis**

The thesis is structured as follows:

* Chapter 1 establishes the conceptual foundations, introducing cultural entropy as a structural tendency and situating the problem within existing art-historical, institutional, and market literature.

* Chapter 2 develops the explanatory framework, drawing on critical rationalism to distinguish descriptive accounts from structural explanations.

* Chapter 3 applies systems and regenerative-economic reasoning to artist estates, reconceptualizing them as living systems operating under coordination constraints.

* Chapter 4 examines the Emilio Rodríguez-Larraín estate as a boundary-condition case study, exposing the limits of ad hoc stewardship under maximal constraint.

* Chapter 5 introduces the Registry of Shielded Artworks (RSA) as a conjectural technical infrastructure designed to stabilize trust and allow verification to compound over time.

* Chapter 6 develops ALMA (Asociación de Legados de la Modernidad Americana) as a polycentric governance network capable of diffusing regenerative stewardship protocols while preserving sovereignty.

* Chapter 7 evaluates the interoperability of this architecture with international frameworks governing institutions, markets, and academia (e.g., ReACH, Responsible Art Market).

---

### **Orientation and Contribution**

The argument advanced in this thesis is not sentimental. It does not seek recognition, restitution, or moral redress. It advances a claim about design.

Cultural value does not persist by merit alone.

In the absence of durable coordination, trust, and stewardship infrastructures, even historically grounded and institutionally engaged artistic legacies drift toward entropy. Posthumous devaluation is therefore not an anomaly, but a structural default.

What begins as uneasiness is thus transformed into an explanatory framework and, ultimately, into an architecture of persistence: a regenerative model of cultural stewardship designed to resist entropy beyond the lifespan, alignment, or virtue of individuals.

## **Chapter 1**

## **The Conditions of Structural Devaluation**

### **1.1 The Problem Situation: A Vicious Cycle of Devaluation**

This inquiry begins with a concrete and recurring problem observed across Latin American modern artist estates: a vicious cycle of posthumous devaluation that unfolds despite demonstrable artistic merit, historical relevance, and episodic recognition.

Across multiple encounters—archival, institutional, market-based, and familial—the same configuration repeatedly surfaced. Once the artist was no longer present to function as the central coordinating node—stabilizing authorship, meaning, and intent—estates entered a downward spiral driven by four interlocking conditions:

1. Narrative exclusion, whereby sustained scholarly and curatorial engagement thins over time;

2. Institutional fragility, marked by underfunded, discontinuous, or weak cultural infrastructures;

3. Market weakness and opacity, characterized by illiquidity, informal transactions, and risk-adjusted discounting of uncertainty; and

4. Fragmented estate governance, producing inconsistent authority, contested authentication, and weak stewardship continuity.

Individually, each of these conditions is well documented within art-historical, institutional, and market scholarship. Collectively, however, they form a self-reinforcing system operating across three interdependent domains: academic narratives, cultural institutions, and art markets. The result is not episodic breakdown, but a systemic default toward decline.

---

### **1.2 From Diagnostic Mapping to Explanatory Insufficiency**

Earlier research (Appendix I) was diagnostically successful. By comparing multiple estates across the region, it demonstrated that failure modes were patterned rather than anecdotal.

However, diagnostic mapping proved insufficient.

While identifying mechanisms clarified where breakdowns occurred, it did not explain why these breakdowns reproduced themselves with such regularity, nor why corrective actions so often failed to generate durable reversal. Describing symptoms did not account for the underlying logic of the system.

As Karl Popper emphasized, the persistence of a problem signals not a lack of data, but an insufficiency of explanation. To proceed, the inquiry required a different mode of reasoning—one capable of producing a hard-to-vary explanation (Deutsch, 2011\) of why posthumous cultural systems default toward decay.

---

### **1.3 Reframing the Estate: From Assets to Coordination Systems**

The first step toward explanation is conceptual.

Rather than treating the artist estate as a static repository of assets, this thesis treats it as a long-horizon coordination system: a socio-cultural structure tasked with maintaining coherence across time and actors once the artist is no longer present.

From this perspective, the artist’s death constitutes a critical phase transition. During the artist’s lifetime, authorship, meaning, attribution, and verification are stabilized by a single, authoritative coordinating agent. The artist functions as a low-entropy hub within the system.

Their disappearance removes this coordinating center. In the absence of a replacement architecture—formal governance, shared protocols, or institutionalized verification mechanisms—complexity theory suggests that the system will tend toward increasing disorder. Fragmentation is not accidental; it is structurally expected.

---

### **1.4 Stakeholders and the Coordination Tax**

Artist estates are populated by heterogeneous actors—heirs, scholars, dealers, institutions, collectors, and publics—who operate with asymmetric information, divergent incentives, and distinct time horizons. No single actor can sustain coherence alone.

Stability therefore depends on shared reference points across academic, institutional, and market domains: credible attribution, consistent provenance standards, recognized governance authority, and reliable channels of communication.

When such coordination infrastructure is absent, a prohibitive coordination tax emerges: the escalating cost of verifying truth, managing risk, and sustaining trust. Under these conditions, actions that are individually rational—such as prioritizing liquidity or disengaging under uncertainty—produce collectively destructive outcomes. Devaluation thus reflects a failure of coordination architecture rather than a failure of personal merit or ethical intention.

---

### **1.5 The Four Mechanisms as Systemic Expressions**

Within this systems framing, the four mechanisms identified earlier can be reinterpreted as interacting expressions of interface failure:

* Narrative exclusion reflects breakdowns at the academic and curatorial interface.

* Institutional fragility reflects failures in custodianship and cultural memory infrastructure.

* Market opacity reflects failures in verification and price-discovery mechanisms.

* Fragmented governance reflects the absence of an authoritative coordination protocol.

Each mechanism degrades five interdependent dimensions of cultural value: material integrity, attribution clarity, interpretive continuity, institutional legitimacy, and market legibility. Degradation in any one dimension propagates stress across the others, accelerating fragmentation.

Crucially, ethical behavior alone cannot halt this process. Good intentions do not compound where coordination fails.

---

### **1.6 A Recursive System, Not a Linear Problem**

Because these mechanisms are recursive and operate across multiple domains simultaneously, the system resists piecemeal solutions. Interventions that address only one domain—such as a high-profile exhibition without archival stabilization, or a sale without governance authority—may succeed locally while failing systemically.

Without a change in coordination architecture, the system inevitably reverts to its default trajectory: drift.

---

### **1.7 From Conditions to Explanation**

This chapter has established that posthumous devaluation is neither accidental nor episodic. It is the predictable behavior of artist estates understood as coordination systems operating under stress without adequate infrastructure.

The task now shifts from mapping conditions to explaining the structural force that produces them. This requires moving beyond the descriptive what to the explanatory why.

Chapter 2 introduces cultural entropy as the unifying explanatory principle capable of rendering this pattern hard to vary—and of demonstrating why regeneration cannot be assumed, but must be deliberately designed.

## **Chapter 2**

## **Cultural Entropy as a Structural Explanation**

### **2.1 From Structural Diagnosis to Explanation**

Chapter 1 established that the posthumous devaluation of modernist artist estates is neither accidental nor idiosyncratic. Drawing on comparative empirical research conducted as part of the author’s prior work in the Executive Master in Art Market studies (see annex 1), the analysis demonstrated that across national contexts, institutional settings, and market environments within modernist art, the same four mechanisms recur—narrative exclusion, institutional fragility, market opacity, and fragmented estate governance—forming a self-reinforcing cycle of devaluation.

However, identifying recurring mechanisms is not equivalent to explaining them. A catalogue of failures does not resolve the decisive question raised by the problem situation itself: why does cultural value so reliably fail to persist once the artist is no longer present to coordinate the system? Why do estates marked by demonstrable artistic merit, historical relevance, and episodic institutional recognition nevertheless drift toward fragmentation and discounting?

This thesis treats the persistence of this dissatisfaction as epistemically significant. Following the tradition of Karl Popper and David Deutsch, such dissatisfaction is not interpreted as a call for more data or finer description, but as evidence that the prevailing explanation is inadequate (Popper, 1963; Deutsch, 2011). The task of this chapter is therefore not to refine the diagnosis, but to replace it with an explanation that:

1. Accounts for the observed pattern as a whole,

2. Explains why alternative outcomes would require fundamentally different conditions, and

3. Remains resistant to ad hoc adjustment.

To meet these criteria, the chapter advances cultural entropy as a hard-to-vary explanation for posthumous devaluation in modernist artist estates.

---

### **2.2 Explanation Quality: The Popper–Deutsch Standard**

Popper’s central contribution to epistemology was to shift the criterion of knowledge away from confirmation and toward explanatory power. A theory is not strong because it fits observations, but because it constrains what can happen. Weak explanations are “soft”: they accommodate outcomes retrospectively, adjusting themselves to explain both success and failure without altering their underlying structure (Popper, 1959; Popper, 1963).

David Deutsch sharpens this criterion with the concept of hard-to-vary explanations—accounts whose internal structure is so constrained that altering their conclusions would require altering deep underlying assumptions rather than surface details (Deutsch, 2011). Such explanations do not merely correlate with patterns; they explain why those patterns must occur unless specific conditions intervene.

Applied to artist estates, this standard exposes the limits of prevailing accounts. Explanations based on artistic merit, recognition politics, market taste, or institutional goodwill are soft. They can always be adjusted after the fact: success is attributed to quality or rediscovery; failure to neglect, bad heirs, or market indifference. These accounts do not explain why decline is the default rather than the exception.

A Popper–Deutsch approach reframes the inquiry asymmetrically:

* Persistence requires explanation.

* Decay does not.

If posthumous cultural systems routinely fragment, then the burden of explanation lies not with decline but with stability. The central question therefore becomes: what structural conditions must be present for cultural value to persist at all once the artist is no longer present?

---

### **2.3 Cultural Entropy: The Core Conjecture**

This chapter advances cultural entropy as the explanatory principle capable of meeting that standard.

Cultural entropy refers to the predictable tendency of posthumous cultural systems to lose coherence, legibility, and coordination capacity over time unless counter-entropic structures are deliberately maintained. The analogy to thermodynamic entropy is structural rather than metaphorical. Just as physical systems tend toward disorder in the absence of work, cultural systems operating across long time horizons tend toward fragmentation in the absence of sustained coordination.

The conjecture can be stated precisely:

In long-horizon cultural systems characterized by heterogeneous actors, incomplete formalization, and reliance on shared reference points rather than centralized enforcement, fragmentation is the default outcome once coordinating authority is removed.

Under this conjecture:

* Devaluation requires no special cause.

* Persistence does.

This immediately explains why the four mechanisms identified in Chapter 1 recur across modernist estates. They are not independent failures; they are surface expressions of the same underlying drift condition.

---

### **2.4 The Domain of Cultural Entropy**

Cultural entropy is not proposed as a universal theory of culture. It applies to systems that share four structural properties:

1. Extended temporal scope: Cultural value must persist across decades, often generations.

2. Heterogeneous stakeholders: Heirs, scholars, institutions, markets, and publics interact under divergent incentives and time horizons.

3. Incomplete formalization: Not all contingencies—such as authorship disputes, edition ambiguity, or interpretive shifts—can be contractually resolved ex ante (that is, in advance).

4. Dependence on shared reference points: Verification, legitimacy, and value depend on commonly accepted identifiers, documentation, and authority structures rather than coercive enforcement.

Modernist artist estates satisfy all four conditions. During an artist’s lifetime, the artist functions as a low-entropy hub: a central coordinating agent who authenticates works, stabilizes narrative authority, resolves ambiguity, and anchors trust. Death removes this coordinating center. Unless replacement structures exist, the system reorganizes under uncertainty.

Fragmentation under these conditions is not pathological. It is expected.

---

### **2.5 Entropy as a Feedback Process: The Coordination Tax**

Cultural entropy is not a single event but a reinforcing feedback process. As coordination weakens, uncertainty increases. Rising uncertainty raises verification and transaction costs—the coordination tax, defined here as the cumulative effort and risk premium required to establish truth, provenance, authorship, and legitimacy in the absence of trusted shared infrastructure.

When this coordination tax exceeds the perceived value of engagement, rational actors—curators, scholars, collectors, institutions—reduce participation or exit entirely. Reduced participation weakens incentives to invest in documentation, governance, and memory infrastructure, further increasing uncertainty and raising the coordination tax again.

The result is a self-accelerating loop that explains why decline often appears gradual at first and then accelerates once coherence falls below a critical threshold.

This feedback structure explains why ethical behavior, artistic quality, and good intentions fail to arrest decline. They do not alter coordination costs. They do not change the system’s architecture.

---

### **2.6 Inaction Is Not Neutral**

A decisive implication of the entropy framework is that inaction is not neutral.

Deferred cataloguing, unresolved authority disputes, informal circulation, and undocumented transactions are not passive states. They export coordination costs into the future. Each unresolved ambiguity increases the verification burden of the next interaction; each informal action compounds uncertainty for subsequent stakeholders.

Devaluation therefore does not require mismanagement or malice. It can arise purely through unmanaged drift under constraint. This insight dissolves moralized explanations. Estates do not fail because heirs are negligent or institutions indifferent; they fail because systems without counter-entropic design cannot maintain coherence over time.

---

### **2.7 Why Local Interventions Fail**

Cultural entropy also explains the persistent failure of isolated corrective actions. Exhibitions, rediscoveries, publications, or market successes may temporarily increase visibility, but visibility without infrastructure is entropically ambiguous. Attention can accelerate circulation without stabilizing reference points, multiplying undocumented works, inconsistent narratives, and contested attributions.

This explains the familiar cycle of rediscovery: enthusiasm surges and activity increases, but coherence does not accumulate. When attention fades, the system reverts to a state of higher entropy than before, because underlying information asymmetry has increased.

If entropy is the structural default, then the only viable response is not advocacy or episodic intervention, but the deliberate design of counter-entropic architectures—a question taken up in Chapter 3\.

**Chapter 3**

Extractive and Generative Systems: Accelerating or Resisting Cultural Entropy

Chapter 2 established cultural entropy as a structural explanation for the predictable posthumous devaluation of artist estates. In long-horizon cultural systems characterized by heterogeneous actors, incomplete formalization, and reliance on shared reference points, coherence does not persist spontaneously. In the absence of compensating structures, fragmentation becomes the structural default.

Entropy alone, however, does not explain divergence. While many estates deteriorate rapidly following an artist’s death, others retain coherence, legitimacy, and legibility across decades—often under comparable institutional, legal, and market constraints. This divergence cannot be explained by artistic merit, individual virtue, or episodic recognition without reverting to ad hoc reasoning. It must instead be explained by differences in system architecture.

This chapter introduces the analytic discriminator that explains divergence under entropic pressure: the distinction between extractive and generative systems. This distinction is neither ethical rhetoric nor a normative judgment about intentions. It is a structural description of whether a system consumes or preserves—and actively regenerates—its own future capacity for coordination over time.

To formalize this distinction, the chapter draws explicitly on the framework of regenerative economics, most fully articulated by John Fullerton and the Capital Institute (Fullerton, 2015). The contribution of this thesis is not the invention or reinterpretation of regenerative theory, but its explicit translation into architectural design constraints applicable to posthumous cultural stewardship. Artist estates are treated here as socio-cultural living systems, and regenerative principles are adopted as structural criteria for resisting cultural entropy, not as moral aspirations or policy prescriptions.

It is therefore essential to distinguish regenerative economics from circular or sustainability frameworks that focus primarily on efficiency, equilibrium, or preservation. Whereas circular models aim to reduce waste within an existing system, regenerative frameworks emphasize the continuous restoration of a system’s capacity to coordinate, adapt, and remain viable over time. This distinction is decisive for long-horizon cultural systems such as artist estates, where persistence depends not on efficiency alone but on the regeneration of trust, legitimacy, and shared reference points (Fullerton, 2015; Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2023).

Under conditions of cultural entropy, regenerative capacity is not optional. Systems that fail to reinvest in their own coordination infrastructure are structurally non-viable, regardless of artistic merit, ethical intention, or episodic recognition.

## **3.1 Living Systems and the Nature of Cultural Persistence**

Regenerative economics begins from the premise that economies are living systems rather than mechanistic machines (Fullerton, 2015). Living systems do not maintain order through stasis or preservation alone; they persist through the continuous regeneration of relationships, information flows, and structural coherence. This insight draws on systems ecology, complexity science, and evolutionary biology, and has been widely applied to socio-economic systems concerned with long-term viability (Encyclopaedia Britannica, “Systems Theory”).

An artist estate, understood from this perspective, is a living socio-cultural system whose effective assets extend beyond physical artworks to include:

* distributed knowledge and documentation;

* interpretive frameworks and narrative continuity;

* institutional trust and market legibility; and

* the capacity to coordinate these elements across time.

During an artist’s lifetime, many of these functions are implicitly centralized. The artist operates as the primary low-entropy hub for authorship, meaning, attribution, and verification. Death removes this coordinating node. If the system lacks mechanisms capable of regenerating coordination under these conditions, entropy accelerates. This reframing is analytical rather than metaphorical: posthumous decline is not anomalous but structurally expected in the absence of replacement coordination architecture.

---

## **3.2 From Cultural Entropy to System Differentiation**

Systems exposed to entropic pressure diverge according to whether they are structurally extractive or generative with respect to their own coordination capacity.

* Extractive systems optimize short-term outputs—such as immediate liquidity, visibility, or market attention—while degrading the informational, institutional, and reputational conditions required for long-term coherence.

* Generative systems reinvest present activity into future legibility, reducing coordination costs and allowing trust to compound over time.

The distinction is functional rather than moral. It answers a single diagnostic question: Does the system consume coordination capacity, or does it replenish it? This mirrors Fullerton’s core critique of extractive economic systems: systems that optimize present extraction at the expense of future viability ultimately undermine their own capacity to persist (Fullerton, 2015).

In cultural systems operating under entropy, extractive behavior is not merely inefficient; it is structurally destabilizing.

## **3.3 Regenerative Economics as a Design Framework**

To avoid conceptual ambiguity, a critical clarification is required at the outset.

The “commons” addressed in this chapter is not the physical artwork, which remains a rivalrous and privately owned asset. The commons under stewardship is non-rivalrous but fragile: the shared integrity of attribution, documentation, provenance continuity, and institutional legitimacy. This integrity functions as a knowledge commons. When preserved, it lowers coordination costs for all stakeholders; when degraded, entropy propagates system-wide.

Polycentric and participatory structures are therefore applied to the maintenance of trust and reference coherence, not to ownership itself.

Within this framework, Fullerton’s eight regenerative principles are translated directly into estate-level design constraints. The principles are not selectively adapted; they are analytically re-contextualized and applied to posthumous cultural systems.

---

### **Principle 1: Right Relationship**

(Fullerton, 2015\)

Regenerative systems maintain coherence through stable, intelligible, and non-adversarial relationships among components.

Estate design constraint:

Durable and explicit governance mandates are required to stabilize authority. Ambiguity regarding who may authenticate, publish, reproduce, or represent the oeuvre generates adversarial dynamics that raise coordination costs and accelerate fragmentation.

---

### **Principle 2: Holistic Wealth**

(Fullerton, 2015\)

System health cannot be measured through financial capital alone.

Estate design constraint:

Market price is a dependent variable of informational, institutional, symbolic, and cultural capital. Monetization strategies that degrade documentation, attribution clarity, or institutional trust are structurally extractive even when financially successful.

---

### **Principle 3: Innovative, Adaptive Capacity**

(Fullerton, 2015\)

Regenerative systems must remain corrigible and capable of learning.

Estate design constraint:

Catalogues raisonnés and attribution frameworks must function as evolving processes rather than fixed endpoints. Corrigibility is treated as a design requirement rather than a weakness, aligning with long-horizon legitimacy under uncertainty.

---

### **Principle 4: Empowered Participation**

(Fullerton, 2015\)

Agency must be distributed procedurally rather than concentrated discretionarily.

Estate design constraint:

Legitimacy scales when participation is procedural and auditable. Concentrated, personalized authority increases systemic collapse risk once that authority is removed.

---

### **Principle 5: Edge Effect Abundance**

(Fullerton, 2015\)

Innovation and stress concentrate at interfaces rather than centers.

Estate design constraint:

The highest entropic pressure occurs at interfaces—estate–market, archive–institution, scholarship–verification. Generative stewardship focuses explicitly on stabilizing these boundary conditions through shared reference standards.

---

### **Principle 6: Robust Circulatory Flow**

(Fullerton, 2015\)

Healthy systems ensure continuous circulation of information.

Estate design constraint:

Opacity is not neutral. Undocumented circulation produces informational waste that raises future verification costs. Generative estates ensure that circulation leaves reusable informational residue.

---

### **Principle 7: Balance Across Scales and Time Horizons**

(Fullerton, 2015\)

Short-term needs must be balanced against long-term viability.

Estate design constraint:

Liquidity, conservation, institutional engagement, and narrative coherence must be structurally balanced. Optimizing one dimension at the expense of others produces fragility rather than resilience.

---

### **Principle 8: Honor Community and Place**

(Fullerton, 2015\)

Systems persist when embedded within their cultural and ecological contexts.

Estate design constraint:

Artist estates cannot sustain legitimacy in isolation. Long-term coherence requires embeddedness within regional institutions, scholarly communities, and cultural publics. Detachment from context accelerates entropy by severing feedback loops that sustain meaning.

## **3.4 Extractive Systems in Cultural Stewardship**

Extraction occurs when value is drawn from an oeuvre without reinvestment in the infrastructures that make that value durable. In posthumous cultural stewardship, extractive patterns include:

* undocumented transactions that generate long-term provenance gaps;

* visibility without archival consolidation (e.g., exhibitions without research substrate);

* authentication based on personal authority rather than auditable protocols;

* narrative simplification that prioritizes marketability over interpretive depth.

In extractive systems, each action increases the coordination cost borne by subsequent actors. Trust resets rather than compounds.

---

## **3.5 Regeneration as a Design Requirement**

The divergence between posthumous artist estates is not a matter of “good” or “bad” heirs. It is a matter of systemic metabolism. Extractive estates consume their own memory in order to survive the present. Generative estates reinvest present activity into future coherence.

Regeneration, in this context, names a structural capacity: the ability of a system to maintain and restore the conditions of its own legibility under entropic pressure. The regenerative principles articulated here are not guarantees of success; they are necessary conditions for viability.

If the regenerative framework developed in this chapter is correct, it must hold not only under favorable conditions but under maximal constraint. Architectural principles that function only in well-resourced, institutionally stable environments lack explanatory and practical force.

This requirement motivates the transition to Chapter 4\. The following chapter examines the Emilio Rodríguez-Larraín estate as a boundary-condition stress test: a posthumous cultural system exposed to fragmentation, informal markets, incomplete archives, contested authority, and institutional discontinuity. The aim is not moral evaluation, but analytical verification—testing whether regenerative design remains possible where entropy should, by default, dominate.

Only if regeneration can be demonstrated under such conditions does the infrastructural response developed in Chapters 5 and 6 become necessary rather than aspirational.

## **4.1 From Problem-Situation to Constructive Stress Test**

Chapters 1 through 3 developed a cumulative analytical framework. Artist estates were defined as long-horizon coordination systems operating under structural constraint (Chapter 1); their predictable posthumous devaluation was explained through cultural entropy (Chapter 2); and divergence in outcomes under entropic pressure was shown to depend on system design—specifically whether coordination structures operate in extractive or generative modes (Chapter 3).

This chapter operationalizes that framework by treating the ERL estate as a living cultural system whose health depends on the integrity of its feedback loops: between documentation and circulation, scholarship and institutional engagement, authentication and market confidence, narrative coherence and long-horizon stewardship. When these loops fail, the system does not simply stagnate. It drifts. It fragments. It loses the capacity to reproduce its own legitimacy.

The death of the artist functions here as a Phase Transition: the sudden removal of the central coordinating node forces reorganization under scarcity, uncertainty, and institutional discontinuity. What follows is rarely accidental. It is predictable systemic drift: coordination costs rise, informational coherence degrades, and trust fails.

The chapter therefore returns to the problem-situation that initiated this inquiry: an uneasiness produced by contradiction. A body of work with demonstrable historical and conceptual relevance appeared to undergo rapid posthumous erosion—archivally, institutionally, narratively, economically—despite intrinsic rigor. That discrepancy demanded explanation. Yet explanation alone is insufficient. A correct diagnosis creates design obligations: it compels the question of what kind of architecture could arrest drift.

Methodologically, this is not a conventional case study that accumulates particulars in order to induce general conclusions. It is boundary-condition analysis, a technique familiar in systems engineering and institutional design. Boundary-condition analysis asks whether an architecture remains viable under maximal constraint. If a system can function where conditions are worst, its design logic establishes a lower bound of feasibility and becomes transferable to less constrained environments.

The ERL estate approximates such a boundary condition: no testamentary planning, fragmented governance, institutional discontinuity, market informality, incomplete archives, and unresolved verification. If a regenerative legacy architecture can be articulated under these constraints—where generative capacity must be rebuilt rather than merely maintained—its core principles are viable in principle.

In this thesis, a counter-entropic intervention is defined as one whose outputs reduce the Coordination Tax borne by future actors rather than increasing it. The ERL estate is examined precisely because, under its conditions, the Coordination Tax is maximized and coherence is most fragile. It is the case that forces the architecture to become real.

### **4.2 Status Quo Under Constraint: What It Means to Inherit Disorder**

Inheriting the ERL estate did not primarily mean inheriting assets. It meant inheriting disorder: dispersed works, missing documentation, unresolved authority, and disputes that transformed stewardship into a high-friction environment. Under such conditions, the estate is not merely under-managed; it becomes structurally vulnerable to extraction.

In practice, the inherited condition was experienced as a recurring set of failure modes:

* short-term liquidity decisions overriding long-term coherence;

* value being realized locally while meaning was destroyed globally;

* benefits accruing to individuals while costs were deferred to the future;

* the estate is treated as a resource to be consumed rather than as a system to be sustained.

These are not primarily failures of character. They are predictable outputs of a degraded coordination environment. When no shared governance exists, decisions that are locally rational—selling, dividing, withholding, acting unilaterally—become globally destructive, because they consume the very coherence that would have enabled a long-horizon plan. The future is taxed by the present, but the bill is paid in legitimacy.

The ERL estate began its posthumous life without the stabilizing structures that convert artistic merit into durable continuity: no formal estate vehicle designed for stewardship, no centralized archival authority, no standardized authentication protocol, and no coordinated strategy for managing copyright and posthumous production. Instead, rights and responsibilities were dispersed across multiple heirs whose incentives, risk tolerances, and interpretations were not aligned. In such a configuration, trust does not slowly decline. It fails discontinuously—because cultural legitimacy is mediated by expectations of continuity, and continuity is the first thing fragmentation calls into question.

The result is not only internal strain but predictable external consequences. Market actors observe disagreement and respond rationally: hesitation replaces confidence; valuations become conservative; engagement recedes. Institutions and scholars face increased transaction costs and reputational risk. Collectors, even ethically motivated ones, lack stable reference points. Under these conditions, the estate does not merely “struggle.” It begins to drift toward entropic equilibrium.

## **4.4 The ERL Estate as a Worst-Case Entropic Configuration**

The ERL estate instantiates the interlocking mechanisms of posthumous devaluation identified earlier: narrative exclusion, institutional fragility, market opacity and illiquidity, and fragmented governance. In this case these mechanisms do not operate independently. They form a tightly coupled system in which each accelerates the others, producing compounding entropy.

Narrative exclusion manifests first as interpretive dissipation. Without sustained scholarly and curatorial reproduction, the work becomes progressively harder to place within evolving narratives of Latin American modernism. Absence operates as a classification mechanism: when an artist is missing from survey texts, institutional programming, and standard reference points, the work is treated as secondary by default. The result is not simply lack of recognition, but a breakdown in the reproduction of symbolic value across time.

Institutional fragility amplifies this exclusion. In Peru, cultural institutions operate under severe budget constraints and are structurally oriented toward other heritage priorities. Modern and contemporary legacies therefore face high transaction costs in research, conservation, loans, and acquisitions, with limited institutional capacity to absorb uncertainty. Under these conditions, an estate unable to provide reliable documentation and persistent stewardship continuity becomes a rational target for disengagement. The withdrawal is not a judgment on artistic merit; it is a response to infrastructural risk.

Market opacity and illiquidity further accelerate decay. The Peruvian art market is small, thinly institutionalized, and often characterized by informal transactions with minimal documentary residue. This weakens price discovery, reduces confidence, and makes it difficult for the estate to finance coherence-building activities through responsible sales. Informality becomes an entropic amplifier: it fragments provenance, obscures edition histories, and increases uncertainty across the corpus irrespective of quality.

Fragmented governance intensifies all three dynamics. Shared ownership, unresolved disputes, and informational asymmetries inhibit coordinated action. A lack of authoritative stewardship locus means that no actor can issue claims on behalf of the whole corpus with persistence and verifiability. Under such conditions, even internally ethical behavior becomes ineffective: one responsible actor cannot substitute for absent structure.

Two observations follow, and they justify a strong claim central to this thesis:

1. Once coordination fails, motives become irrelevant.

    Whether disputes arise from misunderstandings, personality differences, historical grievances, or structural ambiguity does not matter to external actors. The causal story may matter internally; it does not determine external risk.

2. Markets and institutions price risk, not intentions.

    Cultural systems do not reward goodwill under opacity. They reward verifiability under uncertainty. Once uncertainty becomes legible, the salient fact is the absence of stable verification and continuity. Trust does not necessarily erode gradually; it may collapse discontinuously because legitimacy is mediated by expectations of persistence.

These dynamics form self-reinforcing negative feedback loops. Narrative exclusion discourages institutions; institutional withdrawal increases market risk; market opacity amplifies governance conflict; governance conflict deepens fragmentation. Each loop raises the Coordination Tax and accelerates drift toward disorder.

This reframes the problem from ethics to architecture. It explains why short-term liquidity decisions can be locally rational and globally destructive; why fragmentation makes even ethical behavior ineffective; why verification resets consume future capacity; and why artistic merit does not protect cultural value without stewardship infrastructure. The ERL case is therefore not a private tragedy. It is a structural warning.

### **4.5 Design Objectives Under Maximal Constraint**

Given the configuration above, the design problem is not optimization but feasibility. Under fragmented ownership, institutional fragility, market opacity, and archival incompleteness, many intuitive interventions are structurally unavailable. Design objectives must therefore be derived from constraints.

1. Operate without unilateral economic control.

    Shared rights and unresolved governance preclude centralized monetization strategies. Regeneration must proceed in parallel to contested economic rights, stabilizing coherence without requiring prior resolution of disputes that are themselves entropically destabilized.

2. Reduce the Coordination Tax across archival, institutional, and interpretive domains.

    Under maximal constraint, the objective is cost compression: enabling alignment among heterogeneous actors without continuous negotiation or hierarchy.

3. Restore informational coherence under partial observability.

    If substantial portions of the oeuvre circulate externally and may never be recovered, regeneration cannot depend on exhaustive consolidation. Coherence must be rebuilt incrementally through verified reference points.

4. Stabilize legitimacy independently of market liquidity.

    Under opacity, price signals are unreliable. Regenerative design must privilege documentation, standards, and institutional confidence as primary stabilizers, allowing revaluation to emerge downstream rather than being forced upstream.

5. Make local gains system-compatible.

    One-off exhibitions, episodic sales, or partial digitization do not change entropic trajectory if they fail to reduce future coordination costs. Design objectives must emphasize interoperability, standardization, and cumulative coherence.

These constraints rule out purely commercial, estate-centric, or opportunistic approaches. They necessitate an architectural logic oriented toward infrastructure rather than output, standards rather than episodic actions, and legitimacy rather than liquidity.

## **4.6 Architecture in Practice: What Can Be Done at the Estate Level**

The architecture articulated here is scoped to what remains possible at the estate level even under near worst-case conditions. It does not presume comprehensive asset recovery, resolved disputes, or immediate market reactivation. Instead, it prioritizes leverage: interventions that yield disproportionate reductions in the Coordination Tax relative to resource demands.

Governance as coherence, not control.

Stewardship is formalized around moral–intellectual responsibility rather than economic command. By decoupling legitimacy-building functions from monetization rights, the estate can assert custodial standards, documentation protocols, and interpretive continuity without adjudicating co-owned economic rights. Governance becomes counter-entropic infrastructure rather than extractive authority.

Documentation without possession.

Physical control of all works is not required. Standardized protocols for identifying, recording, and contextualizing information are applied wherever access exists. Informational coherence can grow asymmetrically, anchoring verified reference points that reduce uncertainty even when large portions of the oeuvre remain outside custody.

Institutions and scholarship as legitimacy infrastructure.

Partnerships are cultivated not as episodic visibility tactics but as mechanisms that reduce transaction costs in research, loans, and interpretation. Scholarly engagement functions as distributed verification: it stabilizes interpretive consensus and strengthens credibility over time.

Narrative production as durable reference.

Texts, interviews, catalogues, and interpretive frameworks are developed not to market individual works but to create stable reference points that curators, scholars, and collectors can reuse. Narrative coherence becomes cumulative infrastructure rather than consumable publicity.

Even under maximal constraint, these outputs compound. Each verified data point reduces uncertainty; each institutional collaboration lowers future barriers; each stabilized attribution reduces risk across the corpus. Entropy is slowed not by heroic effort, but by systematically lowering the Coordination Tax.

## **4.7 Regeneration as Remedial Work**

Because the ERL estate begins from advanced fragmentation, regeneration is remedial rather than optimizational. The task is not to refine a functioning system, but to arrest further loss and restore minimum coherence where it has degraded. Regeneration is therefore defined not by growth but by repair.

Operationally, remedial regeneration targets five interdependent dimensions of cultural value: material integrity, attribution clarity, interpretive continuity, institutional legitimacy, and market legibility. Recovery is asymmetric: informational and institutional repair typically precede market effects; interpretive coherence often lags documentation. Restoring coherence is consistently more costly than maintaining it would have been.

This asymmetry has direct strategic implications. Attempts to optimize visibility or revenue before stabilizing governance and information are structurally counterproductive. They increase extractive pressure on a fragile system, accelerating fragmentation.

Regeneration therefore requires sequencing. Early interventions prioritize governance, documentation, and interpretive infrastructure—even at the expense of short-term return. This follows directly from the entropy framework: where coherence is low, extraction consumes future capacity faster than it produces durable value. Only once minimal coherence is restored can generative activity propagate sustainably.

## **4.8 Governance Without Economic Centralization**

Under shared ownership and unresolved coordination among heirs, any architecture premised on centralized economic authority is structurally infeasible. The ERL estate demonstrates this with particular clarity: economic rights are fragmented, incentives diverge, and trust is partial and contested. Attempts to impose unified monetization under such conditions would not counter entropy; they would intensify it by raising coordination costs and escalating conflict.

The regenerative model therefore separates economic rights from moral–intellectual stewardship. This separation is not merely pragmatic. It follows from the constraint logic of cultural entropy: when economic authority is contested, forced centralization produces friction, resistance, and informational decay. By contrast, decoupling stewardship from monetization lowers the threshold for cooperation and allows coherence to be rebuilt incrementally.

Operationally, this separation is instantiated through an independent non-profit association dedicated to preservation, documentation, standards, and narrative integrity. The associational form creates a legally legible vehicle through which stewardship functions—archival coordination, authentication protocols, scholarly collaboration, interpretive frameworks—can be exercised without adjudicating economic disputes.

Decision rights are explicitly scoped rather than aggregated. Interpretive authority and informational standards fall within the association’s remit; economic exploitation remains subject to separate agreements among rights holders. In this configuration, governance functions as coordination infrastructure, not as control. It internalizes the costs of coherence while containing conflict by avoiding decisions that exceed mandate. This is governance designed to survive disagreement.

## **4.9 Observable Indicators of Regeneration Under Worst-Case Conditions**

Because this architecture avoids reliance on price appreciation and centralized authority, its effectiveness cannot be evaluated through market outcomes alone. Regeneration must be assessed through variables directly implicated in entropic decay: coordination costs, informational coherence, and legitimacy.

Archival regeneration is observable through the expanding proportion of works, documents, and references that become identified, contextualized, and cross-referenced within a coherent informational system. Completeness may remain unattainable; directional improvement itself constitutes resistance to entropy.

A second indicator is attribution and edition stability. As documentation protocols consolidate and standards stabilize, disputes over authorship, authenticity, and edition histories should decline in frequency and intensity. Reduced contestation does not imply unanimity; it reflects the presence of shared reference standards capable of absorbing disagreement.

Institutional engagement provides a third signal: research inquiries, loan requests, citations, and collaborative projects indicate that transaction costs have fallen sufficiently for institutions to re-engage—especially salient where price discovery remains unreliable.

Market legibility constitutes a fourth indicator. As standardized documentation and attribution language begins to appear in secondary-market transactions, informational asymmetries narrow even when the estate lacks direct transactional control.

Finally, reinvestment orientation provides a system-level measure. A regenerative system persistently allocates effort and resources toward coherence-building infrastructure—archives, standards, scholarship, governance—rather than extractive output alone. This indicates transition from short-term optimization to long-horizon system health.

These indicators do not guarantee revaluation. They measure stabilization. Within the entropy framework, stabilization is non-trivial: it marks a shift from accelerating decay to managed persistence.

## **4.10 Stabilization Versus Revaluation**

It is essential to distinguish regeneration from immediate revaluation. Regeneration targets the conditions that make cultural value durable: informational coherence, interpretive stability, institutional legitimacy, and reduced coordination costs. Revaluation is a market phenomenon dependent on liquidity, demand formation, and credible price discovery.

Under worst-case conditions, attempts to force appreciation are destabilizing. They increase extractive pressure on a system that cannot yet absorb market activity without further fragmentation.

The architecture developed here therefore does not aim to manufacture appreciation. It aims to rebuild the prerequisites for responsible valuation: reliable reference standards, credible attribution, institutional trust, and legible provenance. Revaluation, if it occurs, is treated as an emergent consequence rather than a design target.

This sequencing follows directly from the entropy framework: where coherence is low, market optimization consumes future capacity faster than it produces durable value. Stabilization is not a weaker objective; it is the necessary precondition for any sustainable revaluation.

## **4.11 Why Estate-Level Regeneration Is Necessary but Insufficient**

This chapter has confined itself to what can be achieved within a single estate operating under maximal constraint. That limitation is diagnostic: it establishes the boundary of estate-level feasibility.

Within that boundary, certain problems remain structurally insoluble. Tracking works dispersed across informal and transnational markets exceeds the reach of any single estate. Stabilizing authentication under plural ownership requires legitimacy that cannot be asserted unilaterally. Restoring trust in markets shaped by decades of opacity requires coordination across estates, institutions, and jurisdictions.

These residual failures do not undermine the regenerative model. They clarify its limit conditions. Once coherence is stabilized locally, further regeneration depends on supra-estate infrastructure capable of aggregating trust, standardizing verification, and aligning incentives across a broader ecosystem.

What is missing is not effort or intent, but a shared coordination substrate capable of aligning independent actors without centralization. Estate-level regeneration, while necessary, is not sufficient. Cultural entropy is system-level and demands system-level response.

## **4.12 From Exposure to Design: Why Regeneration Must Be Impersonal**

The ERL case supports a difficult conclusion: preservation cannot depend on the endurance, virtue, or competence of individuals. Any legacy architecture that relies on personal authority is itself entropic, because persons age, disagree, withdraw, fragment, or lose legitimacy. These are not failures of character; they are baseline conditions of human systems operating across extended horizons.

The lesson is not that individuals are untrustworthy. It is that systems requiring trust in individuals do not scale across time.

The architecture proposed in this thesis therefore displaces continuity from persons to protocols. A protocol, in this context, is not merely a technical artifact. It is a rule-based coordination layer that defines roles independently of who occupies them, preserves continuity across succession, encodes verifiability and accountability, and remains intelligible over time.

Crucially, a protocol does not require belief in the virtue of its operators. It requires adherence.

This is the core implication of the ERL stress test: if cultural entropy is default drift, and human limitation is inevitable, then durable stewardship must be impersonal in its continuity. Authority must migrate from charisma and discretion to verifiable standards and repeatable procedures. This is the bridge to Chapter 5\.

## **5.1 From Explanation to Shared Infrastructure**

Chapters 1–3 established a structural explanation for the posthumous devaluation of artist estates in Latin America: cultural entropy. The driver is not artistic insufficiency or individual failure, but a predictable breakdown of long-horizon coordination under conditions of fragmented authority, incomplete records, and high verification costs.

Chapter 4 showed that deliberate stewardship can slow this process locally. The ERL estate demonstrated that governance formalization, documentation consolidation, and narrative repair can partially restore coherence even under severe constraints.

That boundary case also reveals a limit: some entropic failures cannot be solved by estates acting in isolation. Provenance fractures, dispersed archives, contested authorship, and persistent information asymmetries arise at the interfaces between actors—estates, collectors, scholars, institutions, and markets—where no shared verification layer exists.

The implication is decisive: cultural entropy cannot be counteracted through isolated interventions alone. It requires shared infrastructure that makes verification reusable across time and institutions.

This chapter introduces the Registry of Shielded Artworks (RSA) as that infrastructure: a privacy-preserving, protocol-validated integrity layer for stewardship claims. In this chapter, “stewardship statements” refers to provenance-relevant assertions issued by a recognized Registry Authority about an artwork’s status—such as attribution, catalogue inclusion, conservation events, and recognized custodial status changes—intended to reduce verification costs for scholars, institutions, insurers, lenders, and markets. The RSA does not determine artistic value or historical meaning, and it is not a centralized database or marketplace. Its function is narrower and more foundational: to define how authoritative stewardship statements are recorded, verified, transmitted, and preserved under conditions of uncertainty, fragmentation, and time—without centralizing authority or forcing transparency.

In institutional terms, the RSA functions as an operating system for verification. It establishes minimal rules and interfaces that allow independent actors to coordinate around shared reference points by making stewardship claims durable and auditable. Where no such integrity layer exists, trust must be reconstructed repeatedly. Where it does exist, verification becomes reusable, memory becomes durable, and coherence can accumulate rather than dissipate. In this chapter, an integrity layer refers to a system that makes stewardship statements tamper-evident and time-ordered in a way that remains verifiable even when the original institution, database, or administrator fails. 

The sections that follow specify why cultural entropy becomes a registry problem, and why the RSA’s design—implemented as a shielded, protocol-governed registry on Zcash—follows deductively from the constraints diagnosed throughout the thesis.

## **5.2 Why an Operating System Is Necessary** 

To understand why the Registry of Shielded Artworks must take the form of an operating system, it is necessary to clarify the failure mode it addresses. The problem is not a lack of information. The problem is the absence of a shared coordination layer that allows authoritative statements to remain usable across time and across institutional interfaces.

In the posthumous art ecosystem, stewardship claims—about authorship, attribution, provenance continuity, exhibition history, edition status, or conservation interventions—exist in dispersed and incompatible formats: private archives, dealer files, institutional records, personal correspondence, and informal market memory. Because no shared integrity substrate stabilizes these claims, each interface requires verification to be reconstructed from scratch.

The result is structural fragmentation. Verification does not compound; it resets.

This reset dynamic degrades performance across the ecosystem’s three core pillars:

(1) Academia — Research Friction and Narrative Stall

When documentation is incomplete, dispersed, or governed by ambiguous authority:

* access becomes uncertain and costly;

* attribution and dating remain contestable;

* primary documentation cannot be reliably cross-verified;

* publication incentives decline due to reputational risk.

The result is not merely fewer publications, but a breakdown in narrative reproduction. Scholarship cannot stabilize reference points, and the artist gradually exits the art-historical conversation.

(2) Institutions — Administrative and Legal Risk Accumulation

For museums and foundations, fragmentation translates directly into risk:

* unclear authorization to reproduce or borrow works;

* incomplete provenance continuity;

* unresolved authenticity questions;

* burdensome due diligence and insurance exposure.

Even when curatorial interest exists, risk asymmetry produces disengagement. Fewer loans, fewer exhibitions, and fewer institutional publications follow. Legitimacy fails to compound.

(3) Markets — Illiquidity and Opaque Price Discovery

For collectors and intermediaries, uncertainty that cannot be efficiently verified becomes economically binding:

* due diligence costs increase;

* liquidity declines;

* discounts widen to price risk;

* informal transactions proliferate, generating further documentation gaps.

Opacity reinforces illiquidity, and illiquidity reduces incentives to document. Trust becomes expensive rather than cumulative.

Across all three domains, the structural pattern is identical: each transaction, publication, or institutional action consumes credibility without producing durable infrastructure. Memory decays. Coordination costs rise rather than fall.

The RSA responds by introducing a shared, minimal integrity layer that stabilizes verification without centralizing authority. It does not standardize interpretation; it standardizes auditability. By externalizing memory from individuals and institutions into a protocol-governed substrate, it converts isolated acts of verification into reusable reference points.

Where such an operating system exists, coordination costs decline with use. Where it does not, cultural entropy compounds.

## **5.3 Why Cultural Entropy Becomes a Registry Problem**

Chapters 1–3 demonstrated that cultural entropy accelerates when coordination costs exceed a system’s capacity to absorb them. In the posthumous life of artworks, those costs concentrate around a recurring set of unavoidable questions:

* Who is authorized to speak for the artist or corpus?

* Which attribution or catalogue statements are authoritative, and on what basis?

* Which provenance claims are sufficiently credible to support institutional or market action?

* What information can be disclosed without creating legal, security, or market risk?

Under current conditions, answers to these questions exist in dispersed and incompatible repositories. None is independently durable. Reconciliation requires bespoke trust relationships and repeated manual verification.

This produces a structural registrarial failure: verification does not become infrastructure.

Across academia, institutions, and markets, stewardship claims must be reconstructed at each interface. Scholarship slows. Institutional risk increases. Market liquidity declines. Not because information is absent—but because it is not preserved in a cumulative, portable, and independently verifiable form.

Registries exist precisely to stabilize shared reference points. A functioning registry converts individual truth-claims into durable, reusable coordination infrastructure.

In the art world, however, most registries fail structurally in one of two ways:

(1) Opaque and Mutable Registries

Some registries rely on centralized operators whose records can be altered, withdrawn, or selectively disclosed without independent verification. Participation therefore requires blind trust in an authority whose legitimacy may be contested or unstable. Verification collapses into reputation, and reputation decays with succession or dispute.

(2) Over-Transparent Registries

Other registries demand excessive visibility. By exposing ownership identities, locations, inventories, or transaction histories, they impose legal, security, and market risks that rational actors cannot accept. Faced with the choice between unverifiable authority and involuntary exposure, participation collapses.

In both cases, the outcome is identical: stewardship claims remain fragile and episodic. Informational work does not compound. Entropy accelerates.

The RSA reframes the registry not as a centralized database, but as a coordination protocol. Its function is to make authoritative stewardship statements durable and independently verifiable without forcing disclosure and without requiring blind trust in an operator.

In regenerative terms, the RSA converts verification from a recurring private cost into shared infrastructure. Each recorded stewardship event reduces the marginal cost of future coordination across scholarship, institutions, and markets.

## **5.4 From Personal Stewardship to Impersonal Architecture**

The ERL case reveals a general and uncomfortable rule: the preservation of cultural value cannot depend on individuals. Not on artists, heirs, collectors, or institutions as they are currently structured. Any system whose continuity relies on the sustained virtue, competence, or alignment of particular actors is structurally fragile. Individuals age, disagree, withdraw, fragment, or lose legitimacy. Authority becomes contested. Markets observe this personalization of trust and rationally discount confidence.

The institutional vacuum diagnosed in earlier chapters is therefore not merely a lack of organizations. It is a lack of continuity independent of people. Where authority and memory remain personalized, cultural value becomes vulnerable to succession crises, internal conflict, error, and contingency. Entropy prevails not because actors fail morally, but because the system demands a degree of continuity that personal arrangements cannot reliably supply across generational time horizons.

Chapter 4 demonstrated the structural limit of estate-level regeneration. Stewardship can restore coherence, but the gains remain local, reversible, and dependent on sustained effort. Without an external integrity layer, each improvement must be continually defended against decay. Trust is consumed through repetition rather than preserved through design.

The architectural shift proposed in this thesis follows directly from this constraint. Cultural systems intended to persist across generations must encode continuity at the structural level rather than rely on personal alignment. Stability emerges when memory, coordination, and verification are embedded in durable processes that survive turnover and disagreement.

This implies a decisive transition: from trust in persons to trust in process.

In this framework, trust is not a moral quality but a system property. A system is trustworthy to the extent that it makes correct behavior easier than incorrect behavior, and continuity more likely than collapse. When trust must be continually reasserted through personal guarantees, it depletes. When trust is embedded in repeatable, auditable procedures, it compounds.

The RSA represents this transition in architectural form. It does not assume ideal stewards. It assumes disagreement, turnover, asymmetry, and fragmentation as baseline conditions. Rather than suppressing these realities, it renders them legible and survivable. Authority can be explicit and even contested without dissolving coherence. Memory can persist even as custodians change. Verification can remain possible even when institutions fail.

This impersonalization is structural rather than normative. Any cultural system whose continuity depends on personal alignment remains time-bound and vulnerable to succession shocks. Systems designed for generational persistence must encode continuity into architecture.

The move from personal stewardship to impersonal infrastructure therefore marks a threshold in this thesis. Cultural preservation ceases to depend on exceptional individuals and becomes a property of system design. Legacy management shifts from contingent effort to institutional architecture.

What follows is the specification of that architecture.

## **5.5 What the Registry of Shielded Artworks Is—and Is Not**

The Registry of Shielded Artworks (RSA) is proposed as counter-entropic coordination infrastructure for artistic legacies. Its function is deliberately narrow yet foundational: to stabilize the integrity of provenance-relevant stewardship statements—the specific claims that scholars, institutions, insurers, lenders, and markets repeatedly require in order to research, exhibit, insure, authenticate, and transact artworks over long time horizons.

The RSA does not replace the cultural field. It does not adjudicate artistic meaning, determine market value, enforce ownership rights, or automate legitimacy. Those functions remain social, interpretive, and institutional. The RSA intervenes at a prior layer: the integrity and continuity of stewardship statements once personal authority and institutional continuity can no longer be assumed.

### **What the RSA Is**

At its core, the RSA is an append-only integrity log per artwork, maintained by a legitimate Registry Authority (RA). A Registry Authority is “recognized” insofar as its mandate is publicly declared and institutionally legible—for example, the artist during their lifetime; a formally constituted estate or foundation; or a committee operating under documented governance, succession, and revocation procedures. Recognition in this sense is not conferred by the protocol itself; it emerges from institutional practice, legal standing, and field-level acceptance. The RSA records which authority issued a statement; it does not determine whether that authority will ultimately prevail in the cultural field.

For each registered work, the RA records discrete, time-stamped stewardship statements that are coordination-relevant. Only events that materially affect attribution, authenticity, edition status, custodial status, or institutional due diligence are recorded as stewardship statements. Interpretive commentary, curatorial narrative, and market opinion remain outside the integrity log and continue to reside in the catalogue raisonné dossier and the broader scholarly field. Such statements include:

•	canonical registration and stable identification;

•	authentication and attribution determinations (and subsequent revisions);

•	catalogue raisonné inclusion, exclusion, or amendment;

•	edition status statements, where applicable;

•	conservation and restoration events;

•	institutional loans, exhibitions, and publication-linked documentation;

•	recognized custodial status updates (without requiring public disclosure of identity);

•	formal disputes, withdrawals, or revocations.

The defining property of the RSA is verifiable continuity. Once recorded, it becomes possible to demonstrate cryptographically:

1. that a stewardship statement was issued at a specific time;

2. that it was issued by a declared Registry Authority; and

3. that the sequence of subsequent statements has not been silently altered, reordered, or rewritten.

The RSA preserves statement integrity. It does not guarantee the factual correctness of every claim. The truth-status of a claim remains subject to evidence, contestation, and revision. What the RSA ensures is that claims, once issued, cannot be retroactively modified without trace. This distinction is essential: the RSA stabilizes issuance and sequence, not epistemic finality.

### **What the RSA Is Not**

The RSA is not:

* a marketplace, sales platform, or tokenization mechanism;

* a valuation model or price-discovery engine;

* a legal court for ownership adjudication;

* a substitute for scholarship, connoisseurship, or curatorial judgment;

* a mechanism for collapsing social legitimacy into technical certainty.

Meaning, value, and legitimacy remain socially produced through institutional recognition, scholarly debate, and market practice. The RSA does not determine which interpretations prevail. It ensures that provenance-relevant stewardship claims, once made, cannot be quietly erased or rewritten.

### **Scope Discipline: Three Distinct Layers**

To prevent overreach, the RSA must be understood as one layer within a broader stewardship architecture:

1. Integrity Layer (RSA):

   The append-only, verifiable sequence of stewardship statements.

2. Context Layer (Dossier / Catalogue Raisonné):

   The expandable off-chain archive containing images, literature, fabrication context, exhibition history, condition reports, and related documentation.

3. Legitimacy Layer (Field Recognition):

    Institutional acceptance, scholarly consensus, market confidence, and repeated use.

The RSA is intentionally confined to the first layer. It stabilizes the integrity of provenance-relevant claims; it does not replace evidentiary archives, and it does not claim to manufacture legitimacy.

### **The Counter-Entropic Criterion**

In this thesis, a counter-entropic system is defined as one whose outputs reduce future coordination costs rather than increasing them. The RSA satisfies this criterion by transforming isolated acts of verification into reusable infrastructure. Each recorded stewardship statement lowers the marginal cost of future due diligence—whether for scholarship, museum lending and exhibition planning, insurance underwriting, collateralization, or secondary-market transactions.

Participation becomes rational when RSA-backed documentation reduces friction in the highest-leverage gateways of the art market: museum loans, insurance coverage, collateralized lending, and blue-chip resale channels. In such contexts, verifiable integrity is not abstract—it directly affects transferability, institutional acceptability, and economic optionality.

The RSA cannot reconstruct destroyed archives, compel institutional recognition, or neutralize global market asymmetries. Its claim is narrower but structurally decisive: to prevent further integrity decay and to protect achieved coherence from being lost again through succession, dispute, platform failure, or institutional turnover.

The RSA does not create value. It preserves the conditions under which value can remain legible, transferable, and institutionally usable over time.

## **5.6 Why Blockchain, Why Layer 1, and Why Zcash**

If the problem confronting artist estates were merely one of information storage, conventional databases would suffice. Cultural institutions already maintain archives, spreadsheets, digital repositories, and cloud-based document systems. The structural constraint identified throughout this thesis is not storage capacity, but independent verifiability under institutional turnover.

As demonstrated in §§5.2–5.4, cultural entropy accelerates when verification depends on mutable operators, contested personal authority, or institutions whose continuity cannot be assumed. Stewardship statements—authentication determinations, catalogue inclusions, conservation updates, provenance recognitions—may exist in documents, but their credibility erodes when the authority behind them dissolves, fragments, or becomes disputed.

Even digital preservation does not eliminate this fragility. Online records are vulnerable to what is commonly described as link rot: websites disappear, URLs break, hosting services shut down, credentials are lost, and digital archives become inaccessible through platform failure, acquisition, or budgetary collapse. Information may once have existed, yet without durable anchoring beyond any single operator, it becomes practically irretrievable or unverifiable.

The required solution is therefore not improved storage, but durable storage: the ability to verify that specific stewardship statements were issued by recognized authorities at specific moments, in a determinate sequence, independently of whoever currently controls the archive, database, or hosting platform.

A traditional database can store records and attach digital signatures. The structural weakness arises at the governance layer: the database administrator becomes the trusted authority. If that administrator changes, disappears, or becomes contested, the credibility of the record re-enters dispute. The integrity layer required here must remove reliance on any single custodial organization and must resist both institutional fragility and digital decay.

It must satisfy two structural conditions simultaneously:

1. Tamper-evident ordering — past statements cannot be silently modified or reordered.

2. Selective disclosure — verification must be possible without forcing full public exposure of sensitive information.

The first condition concerns integrity. The second concerns privacy.

### **Why Blockchain at All?**

Blockchains are often associated with electronic cash, but their underlying mechanism is more general.

In the Bitcoin white paper (2008), Satoshi Nakamoto described the system as a “distributed timestamp server.” Instead of relying on a central authority to record the order of transactions, a peer-to-peer network collectively maintains a ledger in which each new block of records is cryptographically linked to the previous one. Altering a past record would require altering all subsequent blocks, making undetected revision computationally impractical under the system’s consensus rules.

Abstracted from monetary use, a blockchain can be defined as:

a distributed, cryptographically secured, append-only ledger maintained by a network of independent participants, where entries are time-ordered and historical records are tamper-evident.

“Distributed” means no single institution controls the ledger.

“Cryptographically secured” means mathematical techniques make unauthorized modification detectable.

“Append-only” means new entries can be added, but past entries cannot be deleted or rewritten without leaving evidence.

The RSA does not use blockchain as a payment system. It uses it as a consensus-based ordering mechanism.

In Bitcoin, the ledger records monetary state changes.

In the RSA, the ledger records stewardship state changes—specifically, which declared Registry Authority issued which statement, and in what sequence.

The monetary function is incidental. What is essential is distributed agreement over ordered records that cannot be silently rewritten.

### **Layer 0, Layer 1, and Layer 2: Why the Base Layer Matters**

Blockchain systems are often described in layers:

* Layer 0 refers to the underlying network infrastructure (internet connectivity and peer-to-peer communication).

* Layer 1 refers to the base consensus protocol—the canonical ledger that determines validity and ordering of records. (Layer 0 \= Proof of work), Layer 1 is the payment system with transactions and blocks, etc.

* Layer 2 refers to additional systems built on top of Layer 1 to improve performance or add functionality while ultimately relying on Layer 1 for final settlement.

For long-horizon cultural integrity, Layer 1 is decisive because it is the authoritative record layer.

Layer 2 systems, private chains, and hosted platforms introduce additional governance dependencies. They typically rely on identifiable operators, corporate entities, foundations, or application-specific infrastructure. Artist estates, however, operate across generational timeframes that routinely exceed the lifespan of galleries, foundations, startups, and in some contexts even state cultural agencies.

If stewardship integrity depends on an application-layer platform, private registry, or hosted database, institutional fragility and digital decay reappear at a higher level of abstraction. The integrity layer becomes vulnerable to the very succession risks and platform failures diagnosed earlier in this chapter.

A public Layer 1 is therefore the least institutionally dependent layer in the stack. “Least entropic,” in this context, refers to the layer with the lowest reliance on identifiable custodial organizations for historical verification. It does not guarantee permanence. It minimizes single-point governance dependency.

For art markets characterized by succession disputes, organizational fragility, and digital impermanence, this property is structurally significant.

### **Why Transparency Alone Is Insufficient**

Durable ordering alone does not solve the coordination problem.

Many public blockchains make all transaction data fully visible. While this transparency supports auditability, it introduces substantial risks in art markets.

Public disclosure of ownership identities, artwork locations, inventory positions, or transaction histories creates security vulnerabilities, legal exposure, and opportunities for market manipulation. Collectors, institutions, and families rationally avoid systems that expose such information.

Under these conditions, radical transparency discourages participation and can accelerate informational decay. When verifiability requires exposure, rational actors withhold information.

Therefore, verification must be possible without full public disclosure.

### **Why Zcash**

Zcash is a public blockchain that incorporates privacy-preserving cryptography known as zero-knowledge proofs. A zero-knowledge proof allows one party to demonstrate that a statement satisfies certain conditions without revealing the underlying confidential data.

In Zcash’s shielded transactions, the network verifies that protocol rules are satisfied while keeping sender, recipient, and transaction amounts cryptographically hidden. The ledger remains public and time-ordered, but sensitive details are not exposed.

This separation between validity and visibility is decisive.

For the RSA, stewardship statements can be recorded on a public ledger while remaining shielded by default. Their ordering and integrity are enforced by consensus. Sensitive information—such as ownership identity, inventory position, or location—need not be publicly disclosed.

Selective disclosure mechanisms allow a Registry Authority to grant scoped verification access to a specific artwork’s stewardship trail without exposing unrelated works or holdings. In practical terms, the RSA can grant a verifier access to the records associated with a single artwork—without granting visibility into an estate’s broader holdings, inventories, or activity.

In structural terms:

* A fully transparent public blockchain satisfies tamper-evident ordering but fails the privacy constraint.

* A private ledger satisfies confidentiality but fails institutional independence and durability under digital decay.

* A shielded public Layer 1 satisfies both simultaneously.

### **Fit Within the Thesis Architecture**

The selection of a shielded public Layer 1 follows deductively from the structural constraints developed in this chapter.

If cultural entropy is defined as the decay of reusable verification, then a counter-entropic integrity layer must:

* resist institutional turnover;

* resist digital disappearance and link rot;

* prevent silent mutation of records;

* remain neutral and non-appropriable;

* and enable truthful participation without exposure risk.

A shielded public Layer 1 uniquely satisfies these conditions.

Within the RSA architecture, Zcash functions not as a financial instrument, but as institutional infrastructure: a distributed, cryptographically secured, privacy-preserving base layer upon which stewardship continuity can persist beyond individuals, beyond institutions, and beyond digital platforms.

# **5.7 The RSA Lifecycle: From Authority to Compounding Trust**

To ensure that the Registry of Shielded Artworks (RSA) is understood as an operating system rather than an abstraction, it is necessary to specify its lifecycle as a repeatable, real-world protocol. The RSA intervenes precisely at the point where cultural entropy becomes operationally binding in art markets: when artworks circulate across time and actors—heirs, registrars, collectors, conservators, institutions, insurers, lenders, and secondary-market intermediaries—without shared verification infrastructure or stable reference points.

The RSA does not replace the social field. It structures how stewardship claims enter durable memory.

A critical clarification must be made at the outset. In the RSA model advanced here, stewardship integrity claims are recorded on-chain within the Zcash shielded pool. They are not preserved solely in private databases. What remains off-chain are (i) expansive art-historical dossiers that exceed ledger constraints, and (ii) the social processes through which legitimacy is recognized. Privacy is preserved not by omission, but by cryptographic design. Integrity becomes durable and independently verifiable; interpretation and legitimacy remain socially produced. This distinction is essential to the system’s credibility.

The lifecycle unfolds through six interlocking stages.

## **(1) Registry Authority Is Defined**

A legitimate Registry Authority (RA)—the artist during their lifetime, or posthumously an estate, foundation, or formally recognized committee—publicly establishes itself as the canonical issuer of stewardship statements for a defined corpus.

This step does not eliminate disagreement. It introduces an explicit coordination locus.

Cultural entropy accelerates when authority is implicit, informal, or personalized. It slows when authority is explicit—even if contested—because disagreement can be documented without dissolving coherence.

Operationally, this includes:

* publication of a verifiable RA identity (cryptographic signing identity, the key pair, and attestation policy);

* specification of governance rules for custody, rotation, succession, and compromise response;

* articulation of intake, classification, revision, and revocation procedures.

In the ERL case, this role is exercised by the ERL non-profit association acting under a stewardship mandate explicitly decoupled from contested economic rights. This separation is structural rather than cosmetic: it allows integrity coordination to proceed even when ownership, valuation, or inheritance disputes remain unresolved.

The RSA does not determine who ought to be authoritative. It records which authority has publicly assumed responsibility. Where multiple authorities issue competing claims, the RSA preserves a legible, attributable record of those claims rather than forcing premature resolution; reconciliation remains a governance and field-level process, not a protocol function.

## **(2) Canonical Identifiers Anchor Referential Continuity**

Each artwork is assigned a stable and unique canonical identifier that persists across:

* catalogue raisonné entries;

* institutional records and wall labels;

* conservation and restoration documentation;

* insurance files;

* Certificates of Authenticity (COAs);

* and future market transactions.

This resolves a fundamental entropic failure in posthumous legacies: the inability to reliably refer to the same work across contexts, jurisdictions, and decades.

The identifier asserts neither ownership nor value. It establishes referential continuity.

In practice, this identifier binds two complementary layers:

1. A shielded, on-chain integrity log anchoring chronology and tamper-evident sequence.

2. An off-chain catalogue raisonné engine containing the full scholarly and market-relevant context (images, literature, exhibition history, fabrication context, condition reports).

The division is functional rather than evasive. The ledger anchors what must not be silently mutable. The catalogue carries what must remain extensible and interpretive.

## **(3) Stewardship Events Accumulate as Shielded Logs**

Stewardship events are recorded as Registry Authority–issued statements committed on-chain within shielded transactions. Each artwork maintains an append-only integrity log, linked sequentially to prior entries to form a continuous stewardship record.

Once recorded, it becomes possible to demonstrate:

* Authorship — which Registry Authority issued the statement;

* Ordering — where the event sits in the asset’s chronological sequence;

* Integrity — that the event, once published, cannot be silently altered, removed, or reordered.

The ledger does not encode interpretation, valuation, or curatorial judgment. It encodes issuance and sequence. Cultural meaning remains socially produced and contestable, while stewardship claims acquire durable chronological integrity independent of memory, reputation, or institutional continuity.

The RSA does not adjudicate factual correctness. It cannot prevent a Registry Authority from issuing a false or self-serving statement. Its function is narrower: to ensure that once a statement is issued, it cannot be silently altered or denied later, and that revisions, withdrawals, and disputes remain traceable rather than disappearing into informal memory. The RSA cannot compel completeness. It guarantees the integrity and ordering of what is recorded and makes omissions, revisions, or gaps legible rather than invisible.

Because these records are anchored to a public base layer rather than a private registry, their verifiability persists even if a website disappears, a database becomes inaccessible, credentials are lost, or institutional leadership changes. Stewardship ceases to depend on the survival of any single archive, server, or administrator.

Stewardship becomes cumulative rather than consumptive.

## **(4) Privacy Is Enforced Through Selective Disclosure**

The central constraint in art markets is not lack of information, but the cost of exposure.

Collectors, institutions, and families require verification without surveillance. Public exposure of ownership identity, location, or transaction history can introduce security, legal, and market risks.

The RSA enforces privacy structurally by ensuring that verification can be scoped to a specific artwork’s integrity trail. A museum, insurer, lender, scholar, or court can independently verify the recorded sequence of stewardship statements for one work without gaining visibility into unrelated assets, inventories, or positions.

Verification becomes possible without involuntary disclosure.

This alignment of truthfulness and self-interest is essential for rational adoption in real art markets.

## **(5) Certificates of Authenticity Become the Market Interface**

Cryptography is not the binding constraint. Adoption is.

The RSA uses the Certificate of Authenticity (COA) as its primary onboarding interface because it already functions as a widely recognized coordination instrument within art markets.

In the ERL case, owners holding works—often acquired through informal secondary markets and accompanied by incomplete documentation—are offered a pathway to improved legibility. Rather than attempting to enforce resale royalties, the estate charges a service fee for certification and catalogue integration. This fee funds documentation as a public good while offering owners tangible benefit: increased transferability, institutional acceptability, and economic optionality.

The principal adoption risk is not technical feasibility but demand. If integrity-backed documentation is not required—or rewarded—by insurers, lenders, museums, or major secondary-market intermediaries, uptake will remain limited.

Markets normalize standards not because they are elegant, but because access and risk management make them rational. When integrity-backed documentation reduces due diligence costs or insurance exposure, participation becomes economically aligned with protocol adherence.

Within this framework, the COA functions as a translation layer between protocol integrity and market practice. Provenance claims may evolve; silent rewriting cannot occur without trace.

# **5.8 Conclusion: From Technical Integrity to Collective Legitimacy**

This chapter marks the thesis’s decisive transition from estate-level regeneration to shared infrastructural design. Its central claim has been narrow but structurally consequential: under conditions of institutional fragility, high coordination costs, and contested authority, cultural coherence in art markets cannot be sustained through stewardship alone. It requires an integrity layer capable of outliving individuals, surviving succession disputes, and remaining independently verifiable across time, institutions, and markets.

Cultural entropy, as defined in earlier chapters, is not disorder in the abstract. It is the progressive increase in coordination costs over time. Where verification must be repeatedly reconstructed, where authority remains implicit, and where records fragment across institutions, entropy compounds. Informational work fails to accumulate.

The Registry of Shielded Artworks (RSA) intervenes precisely at this point. It converts isolated acts of verification into durable infrastructure. By anchoring stewardship statements in a shielded, append-only protocol, the RSA stabilizes what entropy erodes first: legible attribution, durable provenance-relevant events, and repeatable procedures for issuing authoritative stewardship claims.

Its contribution is therefore foundational rather than expressive. The RSA does not generate artistic value. It does not determine historical meaning. It does not adjudicate ownership or collapse social legitimacy into technical certainty. Its function is narrower: to preserve the integrity, sequence, and authorship of stewardship statements so that cultural coordination does not reset at every interface.

Integrity, however, does not create legitimacy.

Without integrity, legitimacy cannot persist. Yet legitimacy itself remains socially produced—emerging through institutional recognition, scholarly engagement, repeated market use, and shared belief. The RSA is therefore a necessary but insufficient condition for long-term cultural persistence. It stabilizes truth-claims; it does not manufacture consensus.

This restraint is deliberate. Systems that attempt to substitute technical certainty for social legitimacy risk accelerating entropy rather than arresting it. The RSA confines itself to what can be stabilized without coercion: issuance, ordering, and verifiability of stewardship claims. It protects coherence once achieved; it does not impose it.

Within this thesis, a counter-entropic system has been defined precisely as one whose outputs reduce future coordination costs rather than increasing them. The RSA satisfies this criterion. Each recorded stewardship event lowers the marginal cost of future authentication, provenance assessment, institutional due diligence, insurance underwriting, and market transfer. Trust is no longer consumed through repeated personal assurances; it becomes embedded in process.

Yet integrity infrastructure alone cannot achieve systemic transformation.

An individual estate may implement the RSA and immediately benefit from stabilized verification. The ERL case demonstrates this boundary condition. The association can issue integrity-backed Certificates of Authenticity, construct a living catalogue raisonné, and improve legibility within informal secondary markets. But no single estate can reset ecosystem-wide expectations.

Trust becomes regenerative only when processes are repeatable across actors rather than dependent on the virtue or competence of a single steward.

The implication follows directly. The RSA requires a governance layer capable of:

* coordinating adoption across estates;

* maintaining and evolving shared standards;

* resolving procedural disputes without fragmentation;

* translating cryptographic verifiability into recognized institutional legitimacy.

Such a layer cannot be centralized without reproducing the fragilities diagnosed in Chapters 1–4. It must instead be federated, polycentric, and generative—an ecology of stewards maintaining shared protocol discipline as a public good.

Chapter 6 introduces that coordinating layer. It develops ALMA as the polycentric network through which estates—beginning with the ERL non-profit association as a first node—mutually legitimate, adopt, and sustain the RSA standard. Together, the ERL estate (micro), the RSA (integrity substrate), and ALMA (macro-coordination) form a regenerative architecture in which trust shifts from personal guarantee to institutional process. Cultural memory becomes durable not because entropy disappears, but because coordination compounds faster than it decays.

Chapters 1–4 diagnose the failure mode; Chapter 5 specifies the integrity layer required by that diagnosis; Chapter 6 specifies the coordination layer required for adoption and standard maintenance.

## **Chapter 6: ALMA and the Problem of Collective Adoption**

If Chapter 5 established that durable cultural continuity requires a shared integrity layer, this chapter addresses the unavoidable next question: how such an integrity layer can become collectively adopted, maintained, and recognized across a fragmented art-market ecosystem without reintroducing the fragilities it was designed to solve.

In this thesis, “collective adoption” refers to a stable coordination equilibrium in which heterogeneous actors treat shared stewardship procedures as normal, professionally legible, and risk-reducing—such that compliance becomes rational, repeatable, and institutionally rewarded rather than exceptional.

Chapter 4 demonstrated that estate-level regeneration, while necessary, is structurally insufficient when continuity remains dependent on individuals. Where coherence relies on personal authority, expertise, or alignment, it remains vulnerable to succession, disagreement, and institutional drift. If cultural value is to persist beyond the lifespan and coordination capacity of any single actor, continuity must migrate from persons to procedures capable of surviving change. This recognition marks the transition from diagnosis to architecture that defines the second half of this thesis.

Chapter 5 addressed the first requirement of such an architecture. It showed that cultural entropy cannot be resisted through isolated stewardship alone because the dominant sources of posthumous devaluation arise at interfaces—where verification must be repeatedly reconstructed, trust is non-portable, and legitimacy fails to compound across time, institutions, and jurisdictions. The Registry of Shielded Artworks (RSA) was introduced as the infrastructural response to this interface problem: a privacy-preserving integrity substrate designed to stabilize authoritative stewardship statements, reduce verification costs, and allow trust to accumulate across fragmented cultural systems.

This infrastructural stabilization resolves the problem of integrity, but leaves unresolved the problem of collective adoption. The RSA’s strength therefore clarifies its limit. Integrity is a necessary condition for durable coordination, but it is not sufficient for institutional uptake. A technically credible registry can reduce uncertainty, yet it cannot by itself (i) align heterogeneous actors around shared procedures, (ii) convert procedural compliance into recognized professional standing, or (iii) sustain protocol evolution over generational time horizons. In art-market practice, legitimacy remains belief-mediated and field-structured: it is produced through institutions, expert communities, professional conventions, and fiduciary risk management rather than through technical verifiability alone (Bourdieu 1993; Velthuis 2005). Technical integrity can lower the cost of consecration, but it cannot substitute for the social processes through which consecration occurs.

This constraint follows directly from the explanatory framework developed in Chapters 1–3. If cultural entropy is fundamentally a coordination-cost phenomenon, then reducing coordination costs at the technical layer is necessary but incomplete. Once integrity is stabilized, the binding constraint shifts from verification to institutionalization: how to coordinate multiple legitimate authorities—estates, scholars, museums, insurers, lenders, and responsible market intermediaries—around shared procedures without reproducing the failure modes associated with either centralization (capture, rigidity, exclusion) or unstructured decentralization (fragmentation, standard collapse, verification reset).

This chapter therefore introduces ALMA (Asociación de Legados de la Modernidad Americana) as an institutional conjecture designed to solve that adoption and coordination problem. ALMA is not proposed as a sovereign cultural authority, nor as a continental arbiter of artistic value, historical meaning, or market legitimacy. Its function is deliberately constrained and architectural rather than adjudicative: to provide a polycentric governance network that standardizes and diffuses interoperable stewardship protocols, such that the RSA becomes not merely a tool individual estates may choose to use, but an infrastructure the ecosystem can recognize and rely upon.

In this architecture, the RSA stabilizes integrity; ALMA stabilizes cooperation.

It is essential to clarify the ontological scope of this proposal. Artist-estate networks, associations, foundations, and committees already exist across multiple jurisdictions, operating at varying levels of professionalization, legitimacy, and coordination. This thesis does not claim to originate such efforts, nor to replace them. ALMA is instead proposed as one interoperable institutional node within a broader ecology of estate governance. Its claim is therefore architectural rather than essentialist: given the coordination constraints diagnosed earlier, a polycentric network that standardizes procedures while preserving estate sovereignty constitutes a logically consistent response to cultural entropy.

In epistemological terms, ALMA is not a rhetorical solution but a conjectural architecture: an institutional form proposed because it follows from diagnosed constraints, and because its failure modes are criticizable, observable, and revisable in practice rather than insulated by ad hoc assumptions (Popper 1959; Deutsch 2011). Those failure modes include capture by dominant actors, procedural drift, exclusionary gatekeeping, fragmentation into incompatible standards, and credibility collapse under succession—precisely the risks ALMA’s design must be built to detect and correct rather than deny.

### **6.2 Artist Estates as a Knowledge Commons at Continental Scale**

Artist estates produce outputs whose significance exceeds private ownership. Catalogues raisonnés, authentication records, archival context, provenance clarification, and sustained scholarly narratives function as non-rival informational resources: their use by one actor does not diminish their availability to others, and their value increases through reuse, citation, institutional integration, and market standardization.

In the language of commons scholarship, these outputs constitute a knowledge commons—not merely a type of good, but a governance problem—characterized by high production costs, broad downstream benefits, and chronic under-provision in the absence of coordination mechanisms (Ostrom 1990; Hess and Ostrom 2007). The commons claim here concerns stewardship outputs (documentation, standards, verification records), not the artworks themselves, which remain rivalrous and privately owned.

In practice, these resources are often partially excludable and non-rival, placing them closer to club goods than pure public goods. Yet their downstream externalities remain decisive: museums, insurers, lenders, scholars, and markets all benefit when verification becomes cheaper and more reliable. The core governance challenge is therefore incentive alignment. When returns diffuse while costs concentrate, rational actors under-invest (Olson 1965).

ALMA addresses this asymmetry by internalizing externalities through a federated governance network that:

* produces shared protocols as collective institutional capital;

* converts stewardship investments into portable reputational signals;

* pools professional knowledge, templates, and workflows; and

* enables mutual recognition of integrity claims across borders.

The continental scale is not incidental. Latin American modernism was constituted through transnational circulation, while posthumous stewardship has remained nationally fragmented. A governance structure confined to the nation-state is therefore structurally mismatched to the cultural field it seeks to steward.

---

### **6.3 Why Polycentric Governance Fits the Estate Problem**

The governance problem posed by a continental cultural commons cannot be solved through hierarchy alone. Centralization introduces predictable failure modes: capture by dominant hubs, rigidity, exclusion of weaker nodes, and legitimacy loss in plural interpretive fields. Conversely, pure decentralization preserves autonomy but tends toward fragmentation, inconsistent standards, and verification-reset dynamics.

Polycentric governance offers a third form. Polycentric systems consist of multiple, overlapping centers of authority that operate autonomously while adhering to shared procedural frameworks; coordination emerges through mutual recognition, reputation, and nested organization rather than command (Ostrom 2010). Crucially, polycentricity does not require interpretive consensus. It requires procedural interoperability: the ability to trust the process even amid disagreement about meaning or value.

ALMA operationalizes polycentric governance not as theory, but as institutional topology: a concentric network of legally distinct but interoperable organizations, each operating at a scale appropriate to its function.

---

### **6.4 The Tripartite Concentric Architecture**

Cultural entropy is multi-scalar: coordination failures cascade within estates, across national environments, and across borders. ALMA therefore formalizes a tripartite concentric architecture, not as an abstract model, but as an administratively and legally viable system.

#### **6.4.1 Micro Layer: Individual Artist Estates**

The micro layer consists of professionalized individual estates—typically organized as non-profit legal entities—responsible for custodianship of works, archives, narratives, and stewardship decisions. Estates remain sovereign over interpretation, reproduction policy, and rights governance. What changes is procedural capacity: estates adopt baseline protocols that make their outputs legible and reusable externally.

In this architecture, the ERL non-profit association functions as a boundary-condition exemplar, demonstrating both the feasibility and the limits of estate-level regeneration under maximal constraint.

#### **6.4.2 Meso Layer: National Legacy Associations (Jurisdictional Translation and Friction Management)**

National legacy associations constitute the critical intermediate layer of the architecture. Their function is not merely aggregative, but jurisdictional. They manage forms of friction that are structurally invisible at the macro level and structurally unmanageable at the micro level.

Artist estates operate within nationally specific regimes of inheritance law, moral rights, taxation, nonprofit regulation, export controls, and cultural heritage protection. These constraints vary sharply across Latin America and frequently determine whether stewardship decisions are legally feasible, institutionally legible, or economically viable. Individual estates typically lack the scale or leverage to negotiate these environments effectively, while a continental body such as ALMA is necessarily too general to intervene without overreach.

The meso layer resolves this mismatch. National associations:

* translate shared stewardship standards into locally compliant procedures;

* coordinate legal expertise on inheritance, copyright duration, and moral rights;

* interface with national museums, archives, ministries of culture, and tax authorities;

* pool administrative capacity for functions prohibitively costly at the estate level; and

* represent collective estate interests in national policy contexts.

In this sense, the meso layer functions as a jurisdictional shock absorber, reducing the coordination tax imposed by legal and bureaucratic fragmentation. Without it, the architecture fails in practice: estates remain trapped by local constraints, and ALMA risks becoming symbolic rather than operational.

#### **6.4.3 Macro Layer: ALMA (Continental Coordination and Diffusion)**

At the macro level, ALMA functions as a standards, reputation, and diffusion network. It governs protocol evolution, signals procedural adherence, enables cross-border discovery, and reduces due-diligence friction for institutions and high-integrity market actors.

Crucially, ALMA is also the diffusion engine of the RSA. The integrity substrate introduced in Chapter 5 only becomes systemically effective when adoption is coordinated. ALMA provides the social and institutional layer through which RSA-compliant practices propagate horizontally across estates and vertically across institutions.

---

### **6.5 Portable Legitimacy Under Synthetic Entropy**

The ERL boundary case demonstrated the ceiling of micro-level regeneration. Even with rigorous internal reform, failures persist at interfaces: dispersed works, contested authority, episodic institutional engagement, and fragile market legibility. These are ecosystem failures, not estate failures.

Under late-2025 conditions, these failures are intensified by synthetic entropy: AI-generated images, forged documentation, and deepfake provenance narratives that are increasingly plausible yet unverifiable. In this environment, the primary scarcity is no longer information, but confidence in the process by which information was produced.

ALMA exists to make legitimacy portable. Portable legitimacy is defined operationally as a reduction in marginal verification cost across contexts because claims are:

1. cryptographically verifiable through the RSA’s integrity substrate; and

2. procedurally recognized because they are issued under workflows acknowledged by a network of legitimate human stewards.

Under synthetic conditions, portability of legitimacy becomes more important than portability of data. ALMA functions as a human stewardship firewall: coordinated legitimacy produced by dozens of estates cannot be plausibly simulated by isolated AI systems. What travels is not raw information, but confidence in the collective process that generated it.

---

### **6.6 Reputation, Standards, and Incentive Alignment**

Polycentric coordination functions when standards become reputational capital. ALMA operationalizes this by converting protocol adherence into signals recognized by museums, insurers, lenders, and responsible market actors.

Reputation within ALMA is procedural, not canonical. It attaches to disclosed workflows and assurance levels—not to judgments of artistic importance or market rank. ALMA does not canonize artists; it standardizes stewardship.

Standards govern procedure, not meaning. They are iterative and corrigible, covering documentation discipline, authentication workflows, edition disclosure, archival governance, key custody and succession, conflict-of-interest safeguards, and revision protocols. Protocol versions are explicit, allowing institutions to rely on specific standards over time.

In this way, ALMA functions as the social operating system that allows the RSA’s technical integrity to become institutionally actionable.

---

### **6.7 Diffusion Through Practice, Not Proclamation**

ALMA is designed to generate visibility or market excitement. Its function by diffusing coherence-producing practices across a fragmented art market, cultural field.

By stabilizing procedures upstream, ALMA transforms episodic rediscovery cycles into cumulative infrastructure. Documentation persists beyond exhibitions; attribution clarifications propagate across institutions; verification becomes reusable rather than bespoke. Diffusion occurs through shared practice—templates, peer confirmation, reputational signaling—not through centralized authority.

---

### **6.8 Safeguards Against Hegemony and Canonization**

Any continental coordination architecture must confront the risk of hegemony. ALMA incorporates explicit safeguards:

* standards-based influence rather than status;

* voluntary, reversible participation;

* reputational rather than coercive enforcement;

* transparent, revisable protocol evolution; and

* preservation of plural interpretive space.

The aim is not to eliminate power asymmetries, but to prevent them from hardening into structural domination.

---

### **6.9 ALMA as a Work in Progress: Corrigibility in Action**

ALMA is presented here not as a finished organizational blueprint, but as an architecture under active construction, developed in parallel with the formation of its constituent layers. This real-time institutional development is methodological rather than incidental: the architecture is tested as it is built, allowing constraints of law, administration, adoption, and practical coordination to actively shape its evolution.

The ERL estate itself is currently undergoing legal constitution as a professional stewardship entity, a process that directly informs the feasibility and limits of the proposed model. In parallel, the thesis advances the creation of national legacy associations—beginning in Peru—as a necessary meso layer responsible for managing jurisdictional friction. The explicit aim is that other artist estates replicate comparable national structures within their respective countries, thereby enabling the full tripartite architecture to emerge through coordinated adoption rather than centralized mandate.

This approach reflects a central claim of the thesis: regeneration is a matter of design, not advocacy. ALMA is advanced, in the Popperian sense, as a conjectural solution—a corrigible governance framework whose long-term viability depends on its capacity to be adopted, tested, and maintained across estates, jurisdictions, and generations. It is intentionally structured to be revised or replaced should superior explanatory or operational models emerge, ensuring that counter-entropic coordination remains adaptive rather than dogmatic.

---

### **6.10 Conclusion: From Counter-Entropy to Systemic Interoperability**

The RSA stabilizes integrity.

ALMA stabilizes adoption, interoperability, and incentive-compatible coordination.

Together, they form a counter-entropic cultural architecture—one that does not claim to generate recognition, canonization, or market value, but instead preserves the structural conditions under which recognition can persist, circulate, and compound across time. This architecture does not replace existing institutions, markets, or scholarly practices; it renders their coordination less fragile by lowering verification costs, making legitimacy portable, and preventing coherence from repeatedly collapsing at institutional and market interfaces.

Cultural entropy is not eliminated. It is systematically outpaced through design.

Legacy is not guaranteed. It is made viable through regenerative coordination.

Trust is not asserted through authority or reputation alone; it is encoded into repeatable processes and diffused through a polycentric network capable of persisting beyond individuals, organizations, and market cycles.

Yet this chapter also establishes a clear boundary. A regional, polycentric architecture—no matter how rigorously designed—cannot, by itself, resolve the full problem of posthumous cultural persistence. Integrity and cooperation are necessary conditions, but legitimacy remains socially produced and institutionally mediated. For the architecture to function beyond its originating network, it must interoperate with the wider ecosystem in which artist estates actually survive: international ethical standards, museum governance regimes, market compliance norms, and scholarly historiographic frameworks.

The remaining question is therefore not whether the RSA–ALMA architecture is internally coherent, but whether it can interoperate without contradiction, redundancy, or capture with the global structures that govern the three pillars of estate viability: institutions, markets, and academia.

Chapter 7 addresses this final test. It examines how a regional counter-entropic architecture can align with—and remain intelligible within—existing international frameworks of cultural stewardship, market legitimacy, and scholarly memory, without reproducing the very centralization, opacity, and exclusion it was designed to overcome.

# **Chapter 7**

## **Interoperability with Global Frameworks: Institutions, Markets, and Academic Memory**

Chapter 6 argues that once integrity is technically stabilized (RSA), the remaining problem is collective adoption, and that the only non-fragile way to institutionalize shared stewardship standards across Latin America is a polycentric, multi-layer network (ALMA) that converts procedure into portable legitimacy and lowers verification costs under both institutional fragility and AI-driven synthetic entropy.

### **7.1 The Artist Estate Within a Larger Cultural Ecosystem**

The regenerative architecture developed in this thesis does not operate in isolation. Artist estates are embedded within a broader cultural ecosystem structured by three interdependent domains: cultural institutions, art markets, and academic historiography. This triadic structure is widely recognized in professional estate practice, where long-term legacy viability depends on credible engagement across all three spheres (Würtenberger, 2021). Failure in any one domain tends to destabilize the others, producing cumulative fragility rather than durable recognition.

Chapters 1 through 6 demonstrated that posthumous devaluation emerges when coordination across these domains breaks down. Cultural entropy, as developed in this thesis, does not arise because institutions, markets, or scholars act irresponsibly in isolation. Rather, it emerges at the interfaces between them, where verification must be repeatedly reconstructed, procedures are non-portable, and legitimacy fails to compound across time and jurisdiction.

In this respect, the problem facing artist estates resembles coordination challenges addressed in other transnational domains, such as technical standard-setting and scientific governance. Like standards bodies, global art-world frameworks articulate shared norms and values across heterogeneous actors without sovereign authority. Their effectiveness, however, depends on interoperability, procedural adoption, and institutional recognition rather than normative force alone.

This chapter therefore situates the regenerative model—comprising the Registry of Shielded Artworks (RSA) and the polycentric governance network ALMA—within this wider ecosystem. It stress-tests the model’s adequacy against three influential international frameworks, each corresponding to one of the three pillars of estate interaction:

* Institutions: the ReACH (Reproduction of Art and Cultural Heritage) initiative

* Markets: the Responsible Art Market (RAM) principles

* Academia: contemporary historiography on Latin American modernism, particularly the work of Michelle Greet

These frameworks are not treated as sources of validation or authority. Instead, they function as external constraint systems: if the regenerative architecture can interoperate with them without contradiction, redundancy, or capture, it satisfies a necessary condition for real-world viability.

---

### **7.2 Institutions: ReACH and Ethical Legitimacy Without Durable Governance**

Cultural institutions—museums, archives, and heritage organizations—play a central role in the long-term persistence of artistic meaning. Through conservation, exhibition, and research, they stabilize cultural memory. Yet these institutions increasingly operate under conditions of environmental risk, political instability, resource scarcity, and the physical vulnerability of material heritage (ICOM, 2019).

The ReACH initiative responds to these pressures by advancing an ethical and philosophical argument for reproduction, digitization, and technological mediation as legitimate tools of cultural preservation. ReACH challenges the assumption that authenticity is necessarily compromised by reproduction, arguing instead that under certain conditions, reproduction may be essential for safeguarding cultural knowledge and ensuring access across time and space (ReACH Declaration, 2017).

Structurally, however, ReACH exemplifies ethical legitimacy without durable governance. While it provides a normative justification for reproduction, it does not specify how reproduction should be governed over time. Questions concerning the differentiation between originals and reproductions, the allocation of authority over reproduction decisions, the documentation of editions, and the preservation of institutional trust across jurisdictions remain largely unresolved. ReACH articulates why reproduction may be legitimate; it does not specify how that legitimacy can remain intelligible over generational time horizons.

From the perspective of cultural entropy, this absence of governance is consequential. Preservation efforts that lack coordination mechanisms risk introducing ambiguity around authorship, versioning, and authority, thereby accelerating informational disorder. Conversely, governance regimes that resist reproduction altogether risk preserving scarcity at the expense of cultural continuity.

The regenerative architecture proposed in this thesis supplies the missing coordination layer. The RSA provides an integrity substrate in which originals, reproductions, editions, and digital surrogates can be coherently documented and cryptographically anchored. ALMA, in turn, provides an institutional framework through which reproduction policies can be standardized, disclosed, and mutually recognized without centralizing ownership or interpretive authority.

In this sense, the regenerative model does not replace ReACH. It operationalizes ReACH’s ethical claims by translating philosophical legitimacy into procedural continuity.

---

### **7.3 Markets: RAM and Normative Ethics Without Usable Infrastructure**

Markets constitute the second pillar of estate interaction. They determine liquidity, valuation, and circulation, but they also function as mechanisms for pricing uncertainty. Provenance gaps, unclear authority, and inconsistent documentation are not merely ethical concerns; they manifest as elevated risk premiums and reduced institutional engagement (Velthuis, 2005).

The Responsible Art Market (RAM) initiative articulates a set of principles aimed at improving transparency, due diligence, and ethical conduct within the art trade. These principles address issues such as provenance research, conflict of interest, compliance practices, and responsible transaction behavior (RAM, 2021). RAM’s contribution is normative: it defines what responsible market behavior ought to entail.

Structurally, however, RAM exemplifies normative ethics without usable infrastructure. Its principles presuppose the existence of professionalized estates, reliable archives, and stable documentation systems. In many regions—particularly those characterized by fragmented estates and weak institutional capacity—these preconditions do not hold. As a result, ethical norms often remain aspirational rather than operational.

This thesis clarifies the underlying mechanism. Transparency requires records; due diligence requires reusable verification; responsibility requires standards that persist beyond individual actors. Without infrastructural support, ethical conduct becomes episodic, costly, and difficult to sustain.

The regenerative model functions here as an enabling substrate. The RSA stabilizes provenance and authorship information at the technical layer, reducing informational uncertainty. ALMA coordinates procedural standards and reputational signaling at the institutional layer, aligning incentives so that ethical adoption becomes rational rather than heroic.

The relationship is therefore complementary. RAM articulates ethical intent; RSA and ALMA provide the structural conditions under which ethical market practices can endure.

---

### **7.4 Academia: Historiography Without Interoperability into Institutional and Market Practice**

The third pillar of estate interaction is academic historiography. Art historical scholarship shapes visibility, legitimacy, and long-term valuation by determining which artists are studied, exhibited, and incorporated into institutional narratives.

Michelle Greet’s scholarship on Latin American modernism demonstrates how exclusion from dominant art-historical narratives has materially affected the recognition and valuation of artists, particularly those from the Andean region (Greet, 2015; 2022). Greet shows that canon formation is not a neutral process, but the result of exhibition histories, scholarly publication, institutional attention, and sustained interpretive labor.

Yet historiography alone cannot reverse structural devaluation. Scholarly insight does not automatically translate into institutional engagement or market confidence. Structurally, this reflects historiography without interoperability into institutional and market practice.

Without stable reference points—such as catalogues raisonnés, coherent archives, and verifiable authorship claims—scholarship remains siloed within academia. Its capacity to propagate into museums, collections, and markets is therefore limited, regardless of interpretive quality.

This thesis extends Greet’s insight by reframing narrative as infrastructure rather than commentary. Narrative durability depends on documentation, governance, and verifiability, not on interpretive consensus. ALMA provides a polycentric framework through which multiple scholarly narratives can coexist without collapsing into a single canon. The RSA supplies the informational substrate that allows research outputs to remain stable, citable, and cumulative over time.

The result is not narrative control, but narrative persistence. The architecture does not privilege any single historiographic position; it preserves the institutional conditions under which plural interpretations can coexist without fragmentation or collapse.

---

### **7.5 The Three Pillars Aligned**

Taken together, the three frameworks examined in this chapter map directly onto the three pillars of professional estate management identified in contemporary practice (Würtenberger, 2021):

* Institutions (ReACH) address preservation and cultural memory

* Markets (RAM) address ethical exchange and valuation

* Academia (Greet) address narrative production and historiography

Each framework responds to a genuine problem. What they lack individually is a shared architectural substrate capable of aligning ethics, preservation, and narrative across time and jurisdiction.

The regenerative model proposed in this thesis supplies that substrate. RSA stabilizes cultural truth claims; ALMA coordinates their recognition. Together, they enable institutional preservation, market ethics, and academic historiography to reinforce one another rather than operating in isolation.

In this sense, the model functions as an interoperability layer: not a replacement for existing frameworks, but a system that renders them mutually intelligible and practically effective under conditions of structural fragility.

---

### **7.6 From Regional Regeneration to Global Compatibility**

By stress-testing the regenerative architecture against established global frameworks governing institutions, markets, and academia, this chapter demonstrates that the model is neither parochial nor oppositional. It does not reject international standards; it renders them operable in contexts where they have historically failed to take root.

For Latin American artist estates, this compatibility is decisive. It enables regional regeneration without isolation and global engagement without extraction. More broadly, it suggests that cultural sustainability does not require inventing new values, but designing systems in which existing values—ethical responsibility, preservation, and scholarly rigor—can persist over time.

---

### **7.7 Conclusion**

This chapter has examined whether the regenerative architecture proposed in this thesis can coexist with the ecosystem it seeks to inhabit. In doing so, it has shown that cultural entropy is not overcome by better intentions alone, but by infrastructures and institutions capable of aligning memory, ethics, and knowledge across time.

If cultural entropy is the default condition, regeneration must be designed.

If memory is to survive fragmentation, stewardship must become architectural.

The remaining task is therefore not further validation, but reflection on what stewardship becomes once it is no longer personal, heroic, or exceptional. The conclusion that follows synthesizes these insights and reflects on their broader implications for cultural legacy management beyond the Latin American context.

## **Conclusion**

### **From Uneasiness to Stewardship: Designing Against Cultural Entropy**

Cultural value does not persist by merit alone. In the absence of durable coordination, trust, and stewardship infrastructures, even historically grounded and institutionally engaged artistic legacies drift toward entropy. This thesis has argued that posthumous devaluation is therefore not an anomaly, but a structural default.

This conclusion follows directly from the problem-situation that motivated the inquiry. What initially appeared as a personal inheritance challenge—the posthumous fragmentation of the artistic legacy of Emilio Rodríguez-Larraín—proved, upon closer examination, to be an instance of a broader and recurring pattern. Across countries, institutional contexts, and generations, artist estates frequently lose coherence, legitimacy, and value after the artist’s death, even when artistic quality, historical relevance, and episodic recognition are not in question. The task of this thesis was not to narrate this difficulty, but to explain why it occurs so reliably.

Drawing on the explanatory epistemology of Karl Popper and David Deutsch, the thesis treated uneasiness not as an emotional residue but as an epistemic signal—an indication that prevailing explanations were insufficient. Rather than attributing posthumous devaluation to poor decisions, lack of recognition, or individual bad faith, the inquiry sought an explanation capable of accounting for why similar failures recur under widely varying conditions. If devaluation persists even when intentions are good and merit is present, its causes must be structural.

That explanation was developed through the concept of cultural entropy. In this thesis, cultural entropy denotes a directional tendency observable in long-horizon cultural systems once the artist—the primary coordinating agent—is no longer present. When responsibility for authorship, documentation, narrative authority, and trust is distributed across many actors and institutions, coherence decays unless countervailing structures exist. Archives fragment, provenance weakens, narratives lose continuity, and verification must be repeatedly reconstructed. This drift does not result from ethical failure, but from rising coordination costs that exceed the absorptive capacity of existing arrangements.

From this explanation follows a decisive insight: persistence is not automatic; it must be designed.

The thesis demonstrated that cultural systems exposed to posthumous coordination pressure diverge according to their structure. Some configurations consume coherence without reinvesting in the conditions that allow it to persist, thereby accelerating decay. Others actively regenerate coherence by stabilizing documentation, governance, verification, and institutional trust. The difference is not moral intent or artistic stature, but architecture.

The estate of Emilio Rodríguez-Larraín functioned as a boundary-condition case through which these dynamics could be observed concretely. The case demonstrated that meaningful regeneration is possible even under severe constraints, including limited resources, dispersed works, and contested authority. At the same time, it revealed the limits of estate-level action. Certain failures—repeated verification, episodic institutional engagement, and fragile market legibility—arise not within estates themselves, but at the interfaces between estates, institutions, markets, and scholarship. These are system-level problems, and they require system-level responses.

This recognition led to the architectural core of the thesis.

The Registry of Shielded Artworks (RSA) was introduced as a foundational integrity infrastructure designed to stabilize cultural claims over time. Its function is deliberately narrow but essential: to ensure that statements concerning authorship, provenance, reproduction, and stewardship remain intact, traceable, and reusable even as individuals, institutions, and circumstances change. The RSA does not determine meaning or value. It preserves the integrity and sequence of authoritative claims so that cultural knowledge does not need to be reconstructed from scratch at every transfer.

Integrity alone, however, does not guarantee coordination.

For this reason, the thesis proposed ALMA (Asociación de Legados de la Modernidad Americana) as a complementary governance architecture. Conceived as a polycentric network rather than a central authority, ALMA enables estates to align procedures, translate stewardship practices into recognized professional standing, and diffuse standards across borders without surrendering sovereignty. Its role is not to replace existing organizations, but to provide the missing coordination layer that allows parallel efforts to interoperate where shared procedures reduce friction.

Importantly, nothing in this architecture presumes exclusivity. Artist-estate networks already exist in multiple forms and regions. The contribution of this thesis lies in clarifying the structural condition that is absent when such efforts fail to connect, reinforce one another, or persist. Whether instantiated through ALMA or through other compatible networks, the central insight remains unchanged: coordination must be designed, not assumed.

Chapter 7 demonstrated that this architecture does not conflict with established international frameworks governing institutions, markets, and academia. On the contrary, it helps explain why many well-articulated initiatives struggle to achieve durable impact. Ethical principles, market guidelines, and scholarly advances often succeed diagnostically but falter in practice because they lack reusable coordination mechanisms. The regenerative architecture proposed here supplies the infrastructural substrate through which such frameworks can reinforce one another rather than operate in isolation.

Taken together, the thesis makes four contributions:

1. It reconceptualizes artist estates as living systems whose survival depends on continuous regeneration rather than static preservation.

2. It provides a structural explanation for posthumous devaluation grounded in coordination failure rather than artistic merit, morality, or taste.

3. It demonstrates how explanatory epistemology and regenerative systems thinking can be translated into concrete institutional and technical design within the cultural domain.

4. It proposes—and partially implements—a practical architecture capable of resisting cultural entropy under real-world conditions of fragmentation, uncertainty, and limited trust.

These claims are intentionally measured. The architecture proposed here does not promise recognition, justice, or permanence. It cannot eliminate power asymmetries or compel participation. Its viability depends on adoption, critique, and sustained human effort. Its value lies not in certainty, but in comparative adequacy: it offers a more resilient response to a problem that has long been treated as inevitable.

For the author, this work marks a transition. What began as a personal inheritance marked by disorder and responsibility has been transformed into an explanatory and architectural framework that can be shared, tested, and improved by others. Stewardship ceases to be a solitary burden and becomes a collective, designable practice.

This is the ethical implication of the thesis.

Cultural heritage should not be treated as something to be consumed, frozen, or nostalgically protected. It should be understood as a renewable cultural commons—renewable only insofar as the conditions that sustain coherence, namely documentation, governance, and trust, are actively maintained. Value persists not by resisting change, but by designing systems in which meaning can survive change.

If cultural entropy is the default condition, regeneration must be intentional.

And if stewardship is to endure beyond individuals, it must become architectural.

This thesis offers one such architecture—not as a final solution, but as a beginning that others can extend, revise, and surpass.

In closing, the thesis resolves the problem of posthumous devaluation across three irreducible dimensions. At the epistemic level, it explains why artistic legacies tend toward fragmentation through the structural logic of cultural entropy. At the technical level, it proposes the Registry of Shielded Artworks as a counter-entropic integrity substrate capable of stabilizing verification and memory across time. At the social and institutional level, it designs ALMA as a polycentric governance network through which stewardship practices can be sustained, diffused, and recognized beyond individual estates.

Taken together, these elements address the problem in its full scope: explanation (why value decays), construction (how coherence can be preserved), and coordination (how stewardship can endure). The contribution of this thesis is therefore not a single intervention, but a coherent architecture in which theory, technology, and collective practice are deliberately aligned to design against cultural entropy.
